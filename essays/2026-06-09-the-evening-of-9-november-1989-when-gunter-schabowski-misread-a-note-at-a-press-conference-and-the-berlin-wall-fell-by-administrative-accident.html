The Evening of 9 November 1989 When Günter Schabowski Misread a Note at a Press Conference and the Berlin Wall Fell by Administrative Accident

On the evening of Thursday, 9 November 1989, at 18:53 Berlin time, in the press room of the East German Council of Ministers building on the Mohrenstrasse, the Politburo spokesman Günter Schabowski looked down at a sheet of paper that had been handed to him by Egon Krenz forty minutes earlier and read out, in the flat administrative cadence of a man reading something he had not seen before, the new East German travel regulations. He was asked by Riccardo Ehrman of the Italian news agency ANSA when the new rules came into force. Schabowski hesitated, looked at the paper again, said "as far as I know — effective immediately, without delay." It was 18:57. Within ninety minutes the checkpoints at Bornholmer Strasse were under physical pressure from a crowd that the East German border troops had no orders to engage, no authorisation to refuse, and no political cover to fire on. By 23:30 the gates were open. By midnight the Wall, as a functioning instrument of the East German state, had ceased to exist.

The standard account treats the fall of the Wall as the culmination of a popular revolution. There is a popular revolution in the background — the Leipzig Monday demonstrations, the trek through the West German embassy in Prague, the hollowing-out of SED authority in the Bezirke through October. All of this is real and necessary. None of it explains the specific fact that the Wall fell on 9 November, at that hour, in that way, rather than three weeks later in a managed and humiliating process of staged concessions. The Wall fell on 9 November because a sixty-year-old apparatchik with a head cold, working from a typewritten note he had not been briefed on, used the words "ab sofort" — "effective immediately" — when the document in his hand was meant to come into force the following morning, after the border troops had been instructed and the West German government had been notified through diplomatic channels. This was not a popular victory. It was an administrative accident, and the people who made the accident possible had spent the previous seventy-two hours producing the conditions in which a single misread sentence could bring down a state.

To understand what was in the document, one has to understand the problem that the document was meant to solve. By the first week of November, the East German state was bleeding citizens at a rate that no Politburo had ever had to confront. The Hungarian frontier with Austria had been opened on 11 September; the East German embassies in Prague and Warsaw had been swamped in late September and emptied by Genscher's special trains in early October; the rate of departure through Czechoslovakia in the first week of November was running at approximately ten thousand a day. Krenz, who had replaced Honecker on 18 October, had spent his first three weeks attempting to construct a regulated emigration regime that would convert the haemorrhage into a manageable trickle and earn a hard-currency loan from Bonn in the process. The draft regulation produced by the Interior Ministry on the morning of 9 November was the operational instrument for this. It allowed any East German citizen to apply for a passport and travel permit for either temporary travel or permanent emigration. It required the applicant to apply through the People's Police precinct office. It specified that the regulation would come into force at 04:00 on Friday, 10 November, after which time precinct offices would begin issuing the permits subject to a processing period.

The draft had been approved by the Politburo at 16:00 on the afternoon of 9 November, in a meeting at which Schabowski was not present. The decision to release it through the regular six o'clock press conference was taken at the last possible moment, in a corridor between the Politburo room and the press room, by Krenz, who handed Schabowski the typewritten draft with two pencilled amendments at approximately 18:00. Krenz did not brief Schabowski on the embargo. He did not brief him on the implementation timeline. He did not brief him on the precinct application procedure. He told him, in the words Schabowski himself reconstructed in his 2003 deposition to the Mauerschützenprozesse historical inquiry, that the regulation was "ready to be communicated" and that "it would create a sensation." Schabowski entered the press room at 18:00, conducted the routine business of the daily briefing for fifty-seven minutes, and turned to the paper in his folder only when Ehrman raised the question of travel — twenty-five minutes before the scheduled end of the session — at which point he read the paragraph aloud and was asked when it took effect.

The text in his hand did not say "effective immediately." It said that applications would be processed beginning the following morning. Schabowski, asked the question, looked for an effective date and did not find one — because there wasn't one in the paragraph he was reading from, the timing being in a separate operational annexe that had not been included in his briefing materials. Faced with a roomful of foreign journalists and a direct question, he improvised. "Sofort" — immediately — was the safest-sounding phrase he could find. He repeated it twice. The press conference ended at 19:01. By 19:05 the Reuters and AP wires had moved the story. By 19:30 the West German evening news, the Tagesschau, was preparing a lead bulletin that would broadcast at 20:00 and be received by every East German household in range of West German television — which, by November 1989, meant approximately ninety per cent of the population of the GDR.

The crowd that began to assemble at the Bornholmer Strasse crossing from 20:30 onwards was therefore acting on information that was not just incorrect but operationally impossible. There were no permits being issued. There was no procedure in place. There was no political decision to open the Wall. The border troops on duty at Bornholmer Strasse, under the command of Oberstleutnant Harald Jäger, had received no instructions. Jäger telephoned his superior, Generalmajor Gerhard Neiber at the MfS, at 21:20 and asked for guidance; he was told to verify identities and stamp passports as if for permanent emigration, thereby annulling the citizenship of anyone crossing. This was the moment at which the East German state could have closed the door, at the cost of a confrontation it might still have won. It did not. The reason it did not is that Neiber, on the basis of the orders he himself had received from Krenz earlier that evening through Mielke's office, judged that the use of force on a crowd of unarmed citizens at a border crossing at which Western television cameras were now present would be the end of the regime. By 22:45 Jäger, with the crowd at his gate now numbering several thousand and with the West Berlin police visible on the other side of the bridge, took the operational decision to open the barrier without waiting for further authorisation. The other crossings followed within ninety minutes, each commander watching the precedent of the one upstream.

The administrative accident, in other words, did not by itself bring down the Wall. It produced a crowd at a border crossing that had been physically impassable for twenty-eight years, on the basis of a press-conference statement that misrepresented a regulation that had not yet been implemented. What brought down the Wall was the prior decision — taken implicitly in late October, made explicit by Krenz on the evening of 9 November — that the SED would not use lethal force to suppress public order events of this scale. This was the actual revolution. It happened inside the heads of perhaps forty men in the East German political and security leadership over a period of three weeks, and it was the consequence of two specific facts. The first was the visit Gorbachev had paid to East Berlin for the GDR's fortieth anniversary on 7 October, during which he had told Honecker, in language reported back to the Politburo by Schalck-Golodkowski, that Soviet forces would not intervene to support the SED against its own population. The second was the experience of Leipzig on Monday, 9 October, when the Stasi had massed forces to break up the Monday demonstration and had then, at the last operational moment, stood down — because Krenz, watching the situation from East Berlin, had calculated that a bloodbath at Leipzig would produce a Romanian outcome with East German characteristics. The Leipzig stand-down was the precedent. Every subsequent SED decision was constrained by it.

What Schabowski's misreading did was to compress this whole process into a single evening. The slow-motion collapse of SED political authority, which on the morning of 9 November still had perhaps three weeks of orderly retreat in front of it, was telescoped into ninety minutes of crowd dynamics at a single bridge over a railway cutting in the north of Berlin. The Krenz government, which had hoped to extract a hard-currency loan from Bonn in exchange for a managed emigration regime, woke up on the morning of 10 November with no leverage, no border, and no coherent state. The regulations that had been drafted to extend the life of the GDR became, by being prematurely announced, the immediate cause of its dissolution. Within ten months the German Democratic Republic had ceased to exist.

The lesson is narrow and worth taking seriously. Authoritarian states fall not when their citizens stop believing in them but when their officials stop being willing to use the violence required to enforce belief. Once the SED leadership had decided, between 7 October and 9 November, that it would not shoot, the question of when and how the Wall fell was no longer a political question. It had become a question of operational triggering. Schabowski's misread sentence was the trigger. If he had read the paragraph correctly and given the effective date as 04:00 the following morning, the trigger would have been pulled by something else within seventy-two hours — a botched precinct opening, a confrontation at a Czechoslovak crossing, a Politburo leak. The Wall was going to fall. It fell on the night of 9 November because of a press-conference mishap of the kind that any state apparatus, in any week, can produce by accident. The interesting historical question is not why it fell when it did. It is why the Politburo that had built it had, by that evening, lost the willingness to keep it standing — and the answer to that is Leipzig, four weeks earlier, and Gorbachev, four weeks before that. The crowd at Bornholmer Strasse was the symptom. The decision not to fire was the disease.
