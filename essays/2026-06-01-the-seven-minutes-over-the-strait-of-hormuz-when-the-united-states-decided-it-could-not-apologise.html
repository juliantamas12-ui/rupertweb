The Seven Minutes Over the Strait of Hormuz When the United States Decided It Could Not Apologise: 3 July 1988

At 10:24 local time on Sunday, 3 July 1988, the guided missile cruiser USS Vincennes, on patrol in the central Persian Gulf, fired two SM-2 surface-to-air missiles at Iran Air Flight 655, an Airbus A300 climbing out of Bandar Abbas on a scheduled twenty-eight-minute hop to Dubai. Both missiles hit. The aircraft, which carried 290 people, disintegrated over the Strait of Hormuz. There were no survivors. Sixty-six of the dead were children. The aircraft had been in the air for seven minutes.

What happened over the next seventy-two hours is the more interesting story, because it determined how the United States learned to talk about its own mistakes for the next thirty-five years, and it produced a doctrine of institutional self-defence that has outlived every president, every Secretary of Defense, and every chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff who participated in it.

The facts of the shoot-down were settled by the evening of 3 July and have never been seriously disputed. The Vincennes was not where it should have been. Its captain, Will Rogers III, had taken the ship into Iranian territorial waters in pursuit of small Iranian gunboats, in violation of his rules of engagement. Iran Air 655 was on a published civilian airway, squawking the correct civilian transponder code on Mode III, climbing through 12,000 feet, broadcasting its identifier and flight number on the civilian air traffic control frequency, and travelling at the speed of an airliner, not the speed of a fighter. The Vincennes's combat information centre, operating the brand-new and at the time most sophisticated air defence system in the world, the Aegis, identified the aircraft as a descending Iranian F-14 Tomcat conducting an attack run on the ship. Every one of those data points was wrong. The aircraft was not descending. It was not an F-14. There was no attack run. The Aegis system, which had cost the Navy several billion dollars and was being deployed for the first time in a combat zone, had performed flawlessly. The humans operating it had not.

What the Navy did between Sunday morning and Tuesday evening was to construct, with the active assistance of the Reagan administration, the State Department, and the Department of Defense, a public account in which none of this was true. Vice President George H. W. Bush, then in the middle of a presidential campaign in which he was running on competence in foreign policy, was sent to the United Nations on 14 July to explain the incident. His statement contained the sentence: I will never apologise for the United States. I don't care what the facts are. The remark was not improvised. It had been cleared by the National Security Council that morning.

The internal Navy investigation, conducted by Rear Admiral William Fogarty and delivered to the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs on 28 July, identified the substantive errors with precision. The ship had been outside its authorised station. The combat information centre had misread the transponder data. The crew had been operating under what the report called scenario fulfilment, the documented psychological tendency of operators trained on a particular threat scenario to perceive ambiguous data as confirming that scenario. The Aegis system had displayed correct information; the operators had not believed it. The captain had been told, on at least three occasions in the seven minutes, that the radar contact was ascending rather than descending; he had ordered the missiles fired anyway. Of the report's recommendations, the most consequential was that no officer or enlisted man involved be subject to disciplinary action. The performance of the Vincennes crew, Fogarty wrote, was in keeping with the highest standards of the Navy. Captain Rogers received the Legion of Merit at the end of his tour. The air warfare coordinator, Lieutenant Commander Scott Lustig, received the Navy Commendation Medal with combat V. The Vincennes itself received the Combat Action Ribbon.

The reasoning behind these decisions was never publicly stated in 1988 and has only been partially reconstructed since, mostly from the declassified minutes of the Senior Inter-Agency Group on Defense Policy meetings of 7 and 11 July. The argument, made with most force by Frank Carlucci, the Secretary of Defense, was that the United States could not concede error because the Aegis system was about to be sold to Japan, Australia, and Spain, was being considered by NATO for theatre missile defence, and was the centrepiece of the proposed sea-based component of the Strategic Defense Initiative. To admit that Aegis-equipped operators had shot down a civilian airliner squawking a correct transponder code on a published airway in clear weather in broad daylight was to admit that the most expensive air defence system the Navy had ever built could not, under realistic stress conditions, distinguish between an Airbus and a Tomcat. The geopolitical cost of that admission, in 1988, in the closing years of the Cold War, was judged unacceptable. The reputational cost of defending the crew was judged trivial by comparison. Iran was a pariah state. Few Americans had heard of Iran Air. The 290 dead were, in the framework that crystallised over those seventy-two hours, statistically negligible against the strategic equities involved.

This judgement, narrowly construed, was correct. Aegis went on to be sold to every navy the United States considered an ally. The system has, as of 2026, been installed on roughly 110 destroyers and cruisers across seven fleets. It anchors the European Phased Adaptive Approach to ballistic missile defence. It cost, in 1988, what was perhaps the cheapest possible price: the formal denial of fault for one bad weekend in the Gulf.

The downstream costs were harder to see and have been paid in instalments. The Iranian government, which had been cautiously seeking back-channel contacts with Washington in the spring of 1988 through Algerian and Swiss intermediaries, broke off the conversations on 5 July. The shoot-down is one of the two events — the other being the 1953 coup against Mossadegh — that Iranian state media reach for whenever they need to remind a domestic audience why the United States cannot be negotiated with. Pan Am 103, blown out of the sky over Lockerbie on 21 December 1988, was widely understood within the US intelligence community at the time as a retaliatory operation contracted by Iranian intelligence to the PFLP-GC, though the eventual prosecution was directed at Libya for reasons of evidentiary tractability. The United States quietly paid Iran $61.8 million in compensation in 1996 through the International Court of Justice, on the condition that the payment not be described as an admission of fault. The settlement letter avoids the word apology on every page.

The interesting question is what doctrine the seventy-two hours after 3 July 1988 actually established. It was not the doctrine that the United States never apologises — that is the cartoon version, and it is not true; the United States has apologised, at presidential level, for slavery, for the Tuskegee experiments, for the internment of Japanese Americans, for the overthrow of the Hawaiian monarchy. The doctrine was narrower and more durable: that the United States does not apologise for errors produced by weapons systems it intends to keep selling, by procedures it intends to keep using, or by personnel whose decisions it cannot afford to delegitimise without delegitimising the chain of command above them. The 1988 decision was the template that produced the response to the 1999 Belgrade embassy bombing, the 2010 Uruzgan airstrike, the 2015 Kunduz hospital strike, the 2021 Kabul drone strike on the Ahmadi family, and most recently the 2024 World Central Kitchen convoy strike in Gaza. In each case the same architecture appears: a factual investigation that establishes error, followed by an institutional decision that error cannot be conceded without compromising a larger equity, followed by a public statement carefully drafted to express regret without admitting fault, followed by no consequences for the individuals involved, followed by a quiet financial settlement structured to deny what it acknowledges.

What 1988 demonstrated was that this architecture could be built quickly, defended consistently, and made to hold. The 290 dead over the Strait of Hormuz are the foundation deposit of every subsequent American non-apology. The remarkable thing is not that the United States behaved this way in 1988. The remarkable thing is that the seventy-two hours in July 1988 produced a working model, exportable across administrations and incidents, that is still in operation in 2026. The Vincennes is in mothballs. The doctrine is at sea.
