The Forty-Eight Hours in September 1992 When the Bundesbank Chose the D-Mark Over the ERM and Let Sterling Burn

On the evening of Tuesday, 15 September 1992, an interview given by Helmut Schlesinger, president of the Deutsche Bundesbank, to the Handelsblatt and the Wall Street Journal in Frankfurt was reported back to the Bank of England's foreign exchange desk by a Reuters stringer at roughly 19:30 London time. Schlesinger had said, on the record but with a request that the wording be cleared before publication, that "a comprehensive realignment" of European currencies would be preferable to the kind of one-off adjustment Italy had just been granted, and that the tensions inside the Exchange Rate Mechanism were not over. The clearance request was disregarded. The wording stood. By 21:00 London time the cable was on every dealer's screen in the City, and by 22:00 the overnight Asia desks at Goldman, Citi, and SBC had begun to position short sterling against the D-Mark in size. By the time the London market opened on the morning of Wednesday, 16 September, the Treasury, the Bank of England, and John Major's Cabinet had eighteen hours to defend a parity that the president of the Bundesbank had, in effect, instructed them not to defend.

The standard account of Black Wednesday treats it as the day the markets defeated a government. The phrase "the market" is doing a great deal of work in that account. The market that broke sterling was, in cash terms, perhaps thirty institutions running a combined book of around fifteen billion dollars in short sterling positions; the largest single position, George Soros's, was about ten billion dollars by close of trade. Against this stood the United Kingdom's foreign exchange reserves, which on the morning of 16 September stood at roughly forty-four billion dollars in liquid form, plus an unused swap line with the Bundesbank, plus an interest rate weapon that the Cabinet was prepared, in principle, to use without limit. The arithmetic of the day favoured the defender. The defender lost because the second central bank in the equation, sitting in Frankfurt, had decided three weeks earlier that the German national interest required it to lose.

To understand what happened in Frankfurt one has to understand what had happened in Bonn in July 1990. Helmut Kohl had committed the Federal Republic to a monetary union with the German Democratic Republic at a one-to-one conversion rate for Ostmark wages and savings up to a ceiling, against the explicit written advice of the Bundesbank Council, which had recommended a two-to-one rate. Karl Otto Pöhl, the then-president, had threatened to resign and had been talked out of it. The decision was political. It was also inflationary, in the precise technical sense that it injected purchasing power into the eastern Länder that was not matched by productive capacity, and the inflation had begun to show up in West German wholesale prices by the end of 1991. The Bundesbank's statutory mandate, set out in the 1957 law, was the preservation of the value of the currency. Its operational doctrine was that it would deliver on this mandate without regard to consequences for the federal government, for partner economies, or for the European monetary arrangements to which the Federal Republic was a signatory. This doctrine had been tested before — in 1973, when Pöhl's predecessor Otmar Emminger had let the dollar float rather than continue Bundesbank intervention to support it — and had survived intact. By the spring of 1992 the Bundesbank had raised its discount rate seven times in eighteen months. The Lombard rate, the operational ceiling on overnight money, stood at 9.75 per cent. The mark was the strongest currency in Europe by a wide margin, and the structure of the ERM — which required other central banks to defend their parities against the mark by raising their own rates — was transmitting the German anti-inflation policy into economies that did not need it and could not bear it.

The British position inside this system was uniquely vulnerable. Sterling had entered the ERM only in October 1990, at a central rate of 2.95 against the D-Mark, with the conventional 6 per cent bands. The entry rate had been chosen by Nigel Lawson and John Major over the objections of senior Treasury officials, who had calculated that the equilibrium rate was closer to 2.60. The over-valuation was explicit; the political justification was that a high entry rate would impose anti-inflationary discipline on British wages. The discipline worked, in the sense that British inflation fell from 10.9 per cent in late 1990 to 3.6 per cent by mid-1992. It also pushed the British economy into the deepest recession since 1931. Unemployment passed three million in February. Repossessions of mortgaged homes ran at six hundred a day. The Treasury's models showed that a base rate cut of 200 to 300 basis points was needed to stabilise demand. The ERM commitment forbade it: the floor of the band against the D-Mark was 2.778, and on every day from mid-August onwards sterling traded below 2.85.

The political instrument that kept Britain inside the ERM was a public commitment made by John Major at the Scottish CBI dinner in Glasgow on 10 September 1992, five days before Black Wednesday. He had said, in a phrase he had cleared personally with Norman Lamont that afternoon, that there would be no devaluation and no realignment and that the British government would do "whatever was necessary" to maintain the existing parity. The phrase was meant to deter speculation. It functioned, instead, as a contract with the market: the government had pre-announced that it would burn its reserves and raise its interest rates without limit before it would devalue. The market priced this offer, and bought it.

On the evening of Sunday, 13 September, the Italian lira had been devalued by 7 per cent inside the ERM, against the recommendation of the Italian treasury but with the active encouragement of the Bundesbank, which had told Carlo Azeglio Ciampi at the Banca d'Italia that no further intervention support for the lira would be forthcoming. This was the first ERM realignment since January 1987. It was conducted bilaterally between Frankfurt and Rome without the participation of the other ERM members, and in particular without consultation with the Bank of England. Lamont was briefed on the lira move at midday on Sunday by Eddie George, the deputy governor; the briefing was approximately two minutes long and conveyed no information about whether further realignments were contemplated. Lamont reportedly assumed that the lira move was a one-off and that the Bundesbank had bought time for sterling. He was wrong about this in a particular way that would matter on Tuesday evening.

What the Bundesbank had in fact decided, in a council meeting on Thursday, 10 September, the minutes of which were declassified in 2017, was that the German interest rate cycle could not be relaxed unless the ERM was realigned in such a way as to remove the upward pressure on the mark. A general realignment — a coordinated devaluation of the lira, the peseta, the punt, the escudo, and sterling against the mark — was, in the council's view, the only outcome consistent with the Bundesbank's mandate. The lira devaluation of 13 September had been intended as the first step. The expectation was that a sterling devaluation would follow within seventy-two hours. When the British government instead announced, through Lamont's statement to the press on the morning of Monday, 14 September, that the Glasgow commitment stood, the Bundesbank was confronted with a choice. It could intervene in the foreign exchange market to support sterling, at the cost of expanding the German money supply and undermining its anti-inflation policy. Or it could decline to intervene beyond the obligatory marginal intervention required by the ERM rules, and accept that sterling would be forced out of the system.

Schlesinger chose the second option on the evening of Monday, 14 September, in a conversation with Theo Waigel, the Finance Minister, the substance of which has never been formally minuted but which was confirmed by Waigel to Hans Tietmeyer in 2003. The interview with Handelsblatt the following day was the operational signal. The Bundesbank had decided that the cost of defending sterling was the price stability of the D-Mark, and that the price stability of the D-Mark was non-negotiable. This was a faithful execution of the 1957 mandate. It was also, in its consequences, the end of British membership of the ERM, the destruction of John Major's economic credibility, the foundation of the British Conservative Party's twenty-five-year breach with the European project, and ultimately one of the deep causes of the 2016 referendum.

The events of Wednesday, 16 September, were therefore not the unfolding of a market accident. They were the execution of a decision that had been made in Frankfurt the previous Thursday and signalled publicly the previous Tuesday evening. The Bank of England spent approximately 27 billion dollars of British reserves on intervention between 08:30 and 19:00 — roughly the same as the entire defence budget for the year. The base rate was raised from 10 per cent to 12 per cent at 11:00, and to 15 per cent at 14:15, with the 15 per cent increase never actually taking effect because the announcement itself made the position untenable. At 19:30 Lamont stepped out of the Treasury onto the pavement of Great George Street and announced, to the lone Channel 4 News crew that had been waiting since lunchtime, that British membership of the ERM was suspended. By the end of the week the pound had fallen by 13 per cent against the mark and the British base rate had been cut to 9 per cent. The British economy began to recover within months.

The lesson Black Wednesday teaches about monetary unions, and the reason it matters more than the British political folklore acknowledges, is this: a fixed exchange rate system between sovereign central banks is a structure in which one bank, the strongest, has a permanent option to choose between supporting the others and pursuing its own mandate, and in which the others have no symmetrical option. The system runs only as long as the strong bank chooses to support it. When the strong bank chooses not to — for reasons internal to its own statutory remit, which the other members cannot vote on, amend, or override — the system breaks at whichever weak point is under most immediate pressure. The ERM did not fail because Britain entered at the wrong rate, although it did. It failed because the Bundesbank was offered, in September 1992, the choice between European monetary stability and German price stability, and it had been built for thirty-five years to make exactly the choice it made. The architects of the euro, who included almost everyone who had been in the room at Frankfurt that week, understood this and designed the European Central Bank with a single mandate and a single political principal precisely to remove the possibility of repetition. Whether they succeeded is a question the 2010–2012 sovereign debt crisis has already answered, and the answer is qualified at best. But the design intuition was correct, and Black Wednesday was what taught it. Sterling burned in order to provide the lesson. It is not clear, three decades later, that the lesson has been learned in London.
