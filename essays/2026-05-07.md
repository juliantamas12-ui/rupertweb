# The Weekend That Made Partition Inevitable: Jinnah's Direct Action Day

On 16 August 1946, Muhammad Ali Jinnah called for a general strike across British India. By the morning of 17 August, Calcutta was on fire. By the time the rioting subsided four days later, somewhere between 5,000 and 10,000 people were dead in a single city, perhaps 15,000 more were wounded, and roughly 100,000 were homeless. The corpses lay in the streets so thick that the British garrison commander, General Bucher, ordered army trucks to collect them by the lorry-load and dump them in the Hooghly because the Hindu burning ghats and the Muslim graveyards could not keep pace. The vultures were so engorged they could not fly.

The standard textbook version is that the Great Calcutta Killing was a tragic communal explosion, that it was followed by reprisals in Noakhali and Bihar, and that the cumulative violence convinced a war-exhausted Britain to leave India in a hurry. This is correct as far as it goes and entirely misses the point. **What actually happened on 16 August 1946 was the deliberate weaponisation of mob violence by a constitutional politician who had run out of constitutional moves, and the moment at which the British and the Congress simultaneously discovered that the cost of refusing Pakistan had become higher than the cost of granting it.** Direct Action Day was not a riot that got out of hand. It was a demonstration, and the thing being demonstrated was that Jinnah could produce as many corpses as he needed.

The constitutional position in the summer of 1946 is what makes this legible. The Cabinet Mission Plan of May 1946 had offered the All-India Muslim League almost everything Jinnah had asked for short of partition itself: a three-tier federation in which the Muslim-majority provinces of the northwest and the northeast would be grouped together with internal autonomy on every subject except defence, foreign affairs, and communications, with a right to secede after ten years. Jinnah accepted it on 6 June. Nehru, on 10 July, gave a press conference in Bombay in which he declared that Congress, on entering the constituent assembly, would consider itself bound by nothing — not by the groupings, not by the federal scheme, not by the Plan itself. It was, in retrospect, the most expensive press conference in Indian history. Within a fortnight Jinnah had withdrawn the League's acceptance, declared 16 August Direct Action Day, and announced — in a phrase that has been argued over for eighty years — that the Muslims of India had said goodbye to constitutional methods.

The mechanics of what followed are what make Direct Action Day a different kind of event from the riots that preceded and followed it. Calcutta in 1946 was governed by a Muslim League ministry under Huseyn Shaheed Suhrawardy, who held the home portfolio and therefore controlled the police. Suhrawardy declared 16 August a public holiday — a critical decision, because it ensured that the Hindu majority of Calcutta would be at home or on the streets rather than at work, and that the police, almost entirely Hindu and Sikh in their lower ranks, would be on holiday rotation under Muslim League political supervision. The Muslim League rallies converged on the Maidan at noon. Suhrawardy addressed the crowd from the steps of the Ochterlony Monument and told them — in a speech the British CID transcribed and Suhrawardy later denied — that he had arranged for the police and the military not to interfere. The first attacks began that afternoon, on Hindu shops along Harrison Road. They were not spontaneous. They were carried out by organised groups carrying lathis, knives, and lists.

The Hindu response, when it came on the second day, was equally organised. The Calcutta gangster networks of the Marwari trading community and the akhara wrestling clubs of north Calcutta mobilised under the loose direction of the Hindu Mahasabha and elements of the Congress provincial machinery. By 18 August the Muslim League's initial advantage had been reversed and the killing in the Muslim quarters of central Calcutta was running at a rate the British army, finally deployed in force on the morning of the 19th, could not contain in a single day. The casualty ratio at the end of the week is the tell. The dead were not predominantly Hindu or predominantly Muslim. They were almost evenly split, in a city that was 73 percent Hindu. Whatever Direct Action Day had been intended to be, it had ended as a demonstration that organised communal violence in India was now a two-player game and that the players had reached parity.

This is what broke the British. Wavell, the Viceroy, wrote in his journal on 22 August that the events in Calcutta had "rendered the Cabinet Mission Plan a dead letter" — not because the violence was unprecedented in scale, but because for the first time the colonial state had lost the monopoly on lethal force in a major Indian city for ninety-six hours, and it had lost it to a political party in office. The instrument that had ruled India for two centuries was the credible threat that the British army would arrive in time. After Calcutta this was no longer credible, and Wavell knew it. The British cabinet knew it by September. Attlee announced the irrevocable transfer of power on 20 February 1947. Mountbatten arrived in March. The Radcliffe Line was drawn in five weeks by a barrister who had never been east of Paris. **The reason partition happened on a timeline that produced one to two million deaths and twelve million displaced was not British incompetence in the summer of 1947. It was British clarity in August 1946 about who would die if they did not leave by August 1947, and how many.**

The lesson Direct Action Day teaches, and the reason it deserves to be looked at directly rather than averted from, is about the political economy of communal violence in late colonial states. Jinnah's predicament in July 1946 was the predicament of a constitutional politician whose constitutional gains had been repudiated by his rival, who could not match Congress's mass base, and whose only remaining asset was the ability to make the cost of governing without him intolerable. He used that asset. The asset worked. The Pakistan that emerged from this was not the Pakistan he had wanted — it was a smaller, mutilated, two-winged state that lasted twenty-four years before splitting at Bangladesh — but it was a state, and it would not have existed without the corpses in the Hooghly. *This is the part of the story that the official histories on both sides find difficult to incorporate, because it implies that Pakistan was founded in a deliberate political act of violence and that India was founded in the willingness to negotiate with the people who had committed it.* Both governments have preferred different stories. Neither story explains why partition happened in 1947 rather than 1957 or never. Direct Action Day does.

What Calcutta demonstrated, and what every successor regime in the subcontinent has had to live with, is that the threshold for credible communal mobilisation in South Asia was lowered in those four days to a level it has never returned from. The Bombay riots of 1992, the Gujarat pogrom of 2002, the Delhi violence of 2020 — each of these is a smaller variation on a template that was forged in August 1946 by a barrister from Karachi who understood, more clearly than any of his constitutional opponents, what kind of weapon was actually on the table.
