The Fortnight at Erfurt When Talleyrand Sold Napoleon to the Tsar: September-October 1808

On the evening of 27 September 1808, in a candle-lit room in the Hôtel des Princes at Erfurt, Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord, Prince of Benevento and Grand Chamberlain of the French Empire, requested a private audience with Alexander I, Emperor of All the Russias. He had been instructed by Napoleon to assist in tightening the Franco-Russian alliance forged at Tilsit fifteen months earlier. He used the audience to do the precise opposite. "Sire," he said — and the sentence has been transmitted to us through three independent sources, including the Tsar's own marginalia — "the French people are civilised, their sovereign is not; the sovereign of Russia is civilised, his people are not. The sovereign of Russia must therefore be the ally of the French people." With those two sentences, the most useful French diplomat of his generation went into private treason against the man who had made him.

This is the moment the First Empire's destruction was set in motion. Not Bailén two months earlier, not the Spanish ulcer, not the Russian winter of 1812. The Empire was killed in private conversation at Erfurt, in the late September and early October of 1808, by the man whom Napoleon trusted to manage the relationship with the only continental power capable of breaking him. Everything that followed — the Austrian war of 1809, the diplomatic isolation that preceded the Russian campaign, the Bourbon restoration in April 1814 — runs through the fortnight when Talleyrand decided that Napoleon's appetite was now incompatible with the survival of France as he understood it, and acted accordingly.

The conventional account treats Talleyrand's defection as gradual and ambiguous: a series of doubts, an accumulation of private grievances over the Spanish enterprise, finally crystallising into open opposition only in 1814. This is wrong in the same way that treating Yalta as the start of the Cold War is wrong; it confuses the public manifestation with the operational decision. By the time Talleyrand stood in the courtyard of the Tuileries on 28 January 1809 and was called a "filth in silk stockings" by an apoplectic Emperor, the substantive treason had been complete for four months. What Napoleon was responding to in January was an intelligence leak, not the original act.

The setting matters because it explains the audacity. Erfurt in the autumn of 1808 was the most concentrated display of imperial power Europe had seen since the coronation of Charlemagne. Four kings, thirty-four reigning princes, the entire diplomatic corps of the continent, and a troupe from the Comédie-Française shipped from Paris to perform Racine and Voltaire in the evenings. Talma performed Oedipe before an audience of crowned heads, and at the line "the friendship of a great man is a benefit from the gods," Alexander rose from his seat to take Napoleon's hand in front of the assembled monarchs. The entire production was orchestrated by Talleyrand, who as Grand Chamberlain controlled the protocol, the seating, and — critically — the private audiences. Napoleon had brought him to Erfurt precisely because no one else understood how to choreograph imperial spectacle on this scale. He could not have known that the choreographer was using the production as cover.

Talleyrand's analysis was both moral and strategic, and the strategic component is the one that matters. He had concluded, by the spring of 1808, that the Spanish intervention represented a category error: Napoleon was no longer pursuing French interests in the form of defensible borders and continental balance, but a personal dynastic project that committed France to wars without natural termination. The Bayonne meetings in May, at which the Spanish Bourbons had been deposed under duress, had been for Talleyrand the decisive demonstration. The Empire was now consuming French strategic capital faster than France could generate it, and the only continental power capable of forcing a recalibration was Russia. If Alexander could be persuaded to withhold the cooperation that Napoleon needed against Austria, Napoleon would be compelled — by the simple mathematics of his manpower — to negotiate.

The mechanism Talleyrand used at Erfurt was therefore precisely calibrated. He did not propose an alliance. He did not propose action. He proposed, to Alexander, that the Tsar simply not give Napoleon what Napoleon was at that moment formally requesting: a Russian guarantee of military assistance against Austria in the event of a fresh Habsburg war. The point was passivity, not action. Talleyrand drafted, in his own hand and in the small hours of 30 September, the formula that Alexander would use in the negotiations the following day — phrases of friendship sufficient to satisfy Napoleon's public requirements, accompanied by reservations sufficient to neutralise the substantive commitment. Caulaincourt, the French ambassador to St Petersburg, was in the next room and did not see the document until it surfaced in the final communiqué. Napoleon, on reading the text, said to Caulaincourt: "I am content." He was not content. He had been outflanked by the man who had written the language.

The deepest consequence is institutional, not biographical. Talleyrand had demonstrated — to himself, to Alexander, to Metternich (who took the lesson and reproduced it in 1813) — that the personal alliance system of 1807 was hollow because it had no constituency inside the French state. Tilsit was a treaty between two men. There was no Senate, no Conseil d'État, no permanent foreign ministry committed to its preservation. The instant the senior French negotiator concluded that the treaty was contrary to French interests, the treaty was effectively dead, because there existed no institutional weight to hold it in place against his subversion. This is the structural lesson that every subsequent attempt to bind France to a Napoleonic project would relearn — by Napoleon III at Plombières in 1858, by Pétain at Montoire in 1940. Personal alliances built on the prestige of a single ruler do not survive the defection of the apparatus.

There is a final detail that the standard histories tend to soften. When Talleyrand returned to Paris in October 1808, he stopped on the road at the château of his niece by marriage, the Duchess of Courland, and dictated to her a letter to Metternich in Vienna informing the Austrian ambassador in precise terms of what had occurred at Erfurt and what Vienna might now safely undertake. He charged his niece £400 in gold to suppress the carbon copy. The Austrian war began the following April. The Empire died, slowly, from a wound inflicted in a candle-lit hotel room by a lame Périgord bishop who had decided, on his own authority, that France could no longer afford its emperor.

Sources

  • Emmanuel de Waresquiel, Talleyrand: Le prince immobile (Fayard, 2003) — the standard modern biography, essential for the chronology of the Erfurt defection and the documentary basis of the Alexander audiences
  • Duff Cooper, Talleyrand (Jonathan Cape, 1932) — still indispensable in English for the political analysis of the 1808 turning point
  • Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord, Mémoires, ed. duc de Broglie, 5 vols. (Calmann-Lévy, 1891-92) — Talleyrand's own retrospective account, to be read with the scepticism the man himself would have recommended
  • Armand de Caulaincourt, Mémoires du général de Caulaincourt, duc de Vicence, grand écuyer de l'Empereur, 3 vols. (Plon, 1933) — the French ambassador to St Petersburg, present at Erfurt, indispensable for the diplomatic mechanics
  • Albert Vandal, Napoléon et Alexandre Ier: L'alliance russe sous le Premier Empire, 3 vols. (Plon, 1891-96) — the classic study of the Tilsit system and its unravelling, still unsurpassed on the Russian side
  • Dominic Lieven, Russia Against Napoleon: The Battle for Europe, 1807 to 1814 (Allen Lane, 2009) — for Alexander's strategic calculations and the long-run consequences for the Russian position
  • Philip Mansel, Prince of Europe: The Life of Charles-Joseph de Ligne, 1735-1814 (Phoenix, 2003) — the sharpest contemporary observer of Erfurt's social and ceremonial life, with details available nowhere else
