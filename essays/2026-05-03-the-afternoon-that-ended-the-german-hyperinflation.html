The Afternoon That Ended the German Hyperinflation: How a Fictional Currency Saved the Weimar Republic

The German hyperinflation of 1923 is one of those events that has become a parable, stripped of its actual mechanics. Wheelbarrows of cash. Children making kites out of banknotes. Restaurants printing menus twice during a single meal. The numbers are genuinely staggering — by November 1923 the mark had fallen to roughly 4.2 trillion to the dollar, from 4.2 to the dollar in 1914 — and the parable usually ends with a sermon about fiat currency, central bank discipline, and the need for hard money. This is the wrong lesson, because the German hyperinflation did not end through any of those things. It ended on the afternoon of 15 November 1923, when a man named Hjalmar Schacht introduced a currency that was backed by nothing, redeemable for nothing, and whose collateral consisted of an imaginary mortgage on land that the issuing entity did not own. It worked instantly. The hyperinflation stopped that day. The mechanism by which it stopped tells us almost the opposite of what the parable claims.

The Rentenmark, as the new currency was called, was a confidence trick in the most literal sense. Its formal backing was a mortgage of 3.2 billion gold marks levied on all German agricultural and industrial property — a mortgage that existed only on paper, that the government had no legal authority to foreclose, and that would never be collected. It was issued by the Rentenbank, an institution created two weeks earlier with no gold reserves, no foreign exchange, and a charter that explicitly forbade it from lending to the state. There was nothing standing behind it except the promise that there would only ever be 2.4 billion of them in circulation, and that they would always be exchangeable for the old paper marks at a fixed ratio of one trillion to one. The whole apparatus was, on close inspection, a fiction. It was also, by 16 November 1923, the most stable currency in Europe.

To understand why this worked, you have to understand what had actually been driving the inflation, which was not — contrary to the standard story — the Reichsbank simply printing too much money. The Reichsbank had indeed been printing too much money, but it had been doing so under specific political conditions that nobody in Berlin had been able to break for four years. The first condition was the Treaty of Versailles, whose reparations regime required Germany to make payments in hard currency that Germany did not possess. The second was the French and Belgian occupation of the Ruhr, which began in January 1923 after Germany defaulted on coal deliveries. The German government responded with a policy of "passive resistance" — paying the Ruhr workers their full wages while they refused to work for the French — which had to be financed by money creation, because the Ruhr was the country's industrial heart and producing nothing. The third, and decisive, condition was that German industrialists and large landowners were systematically refusing to pay taxes, calculating correctly that any tax bill paid six months late would be worth essentially nothing. The Reichsbank was printing money because the state had no other source of revenue, and the state had no other source of revenue because the wealthiest Germans had figured out that inflation was a giant transfer of wealth from creditors and wage-earners to anyone who held real assets or owed debts in marks.

This is the part of the story that is usually skipped. The hyperinflation was not an accident of monetary policy. It was a class war waged through the printing press, and the people winning it were Hugo Stinnes, Fritz Thyssen, and the great agricultural junkers of East Prussia, who were busy buying up factories, farms, and mines with worthless paper and paying off their pre-war mortgages for the price of a sandwich. By autumn 1923 they had transferred to themselves the largest concentration of industrial assets in German history. The middle class — pensioners, civil servants, anyone whose savings were denominated in marks — had been wiped out. The Reichsbank was not the villain. It was the instrument.

What changed in November was not the monetary mechanics. What changed was that the political conditions producing the inflation were resolved within forty-eight hours of the Rentenmark's introduction, and the resolution was almost entirely political rather than economic. On 26 September, Chancellor Gustav Stresemann had ended passive resistance in the Ruhr — meaning the bottomless wage subsidy was over. On 13 October the Reichstag passed the Enabling Act giving the cabinet emergency powers to legislate by decree. On 15 November, the same day the Rentenmark was issued, the Reichsbank simply stopped — stopped — discounting the Treasury's bills. The state could no longer fund itself by printing money, because the central bank had unilaterally refused to allow it. Two weeks later, on 20 November, Schacht was appointed Reichsbank president and immediately ordered the bank to defend the new exchange rate by any means necessary, including denying credit to the same industrialists who had been gaming the inflation for four years. The reparations question was punted to a committee that would eventually become the Dawes Plan, buying breathing room. The Rentenmark did not end the inflation. The end of passive resistance, the cessation of central bank financing of the state, and the political defeat of the inflationist industrial bloc ended the inflation. The Rentenmark was the symbol that signalled the regime change.

This is why it could be backed by nothing and still work. A currency does not need gold reserves or commodity backing. It needs the credible commitment of the issuing authority not to print too much of it, and credibility, in 1923, meant not the technical features of the new note but the visible political fact that the people who had been forcing the inflation had lost. The Rentenmark was, in technical terms, exactly as worthless as the paper mark it replaced. The difference was that on 15 November 1923 it became visible to every German that the Reich would no longer monetise its deficits and that the great industrialists had run out of ways to bleed the currency. The fiction worked because everyone who needed to believe in it could see, finally, that the alternative had been ended.

There is a lesson in this that has been almost entirely buried under the gold-standard moralism that the episode usually generates. Hyperinflations are not technical failures of monetary economics. They are political crises in which one faction has captured the printing press and is using it against another. They end when that political capture is broken, and they end at the speed at which the capture is broken — which can be, as in November 1923, a single afternoon. The currency that replaces the failed one does not need to be backed by anything tangible. It needs to be backed by the visible defeat of whoever was profiting from the old one. Schacht understood this, which is why the Rentenmark worked. Most of the people who have invoked it since, as a model for hard money discipline, have understood almost nothing about it at all.
