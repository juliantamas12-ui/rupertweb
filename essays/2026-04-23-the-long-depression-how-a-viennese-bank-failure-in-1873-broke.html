The Long Depression: How a Viennese Bank Failure in 1873 Broke the World for Twenty-Three Years

On 9 May 1873, a Friday, the Vienna Stock Exchange opened at its usual hour and by noon had stopped functioning. Prices collapsed so fast that the ticker could not keep up. Within a week, dozens of Austrian banks had failed. Within four months, the panic had crossed the Atlantic, taken down Jay Cooke & Company — the most politically connected investment bank in the United States — and forced the New York Stock Exchange to shut its doors for the first time in its history. The economic crisis that followed lasted, by most contemporary accounts, until 1896. People at the time called it the Great Depression. They stopped calling it that only after 1929 gave them a worse one to name.

We have almost entirely forgotten the Long Depression, and that forgetting is itself revealing. It was the first truly globalised economic catastrophe — the first time a bank failure in Central Europe could bankrupt railroads in Pennsylvania, throw Manchester cotton workers out of their jobs, and reshape the politics of countries that had no particular connection to Vienna. It was also the moment when the nineteenth-century liberal consensus on free trade, the gold standard, and laissez-faire economics began its slow, ugly death, and when the political coalitions that would dominate the twentieth century — protectionist, nationalist, anti-Semitic, welfare-statist — first assembled themselves out of the wreckage.

The immediate trigger was a railway mania. Between 1867 and 1873, Austria-Hungary, Germany, and the United States had together built more track than Europe and America combined had built in the previous forty years. Much of this construction was financed through a specific instrument — the railway mortgage bond — that depended, like all such instruments, on two assumptions: that the land being mortgaged would keep appreciating, and that the railways themselves would eventually carry enough traffic to service their debts. Both assumptions were false. By 1873 the marginal railway being built in Nebraska or Galicia was serving territory that did not yet have enough people to justify it, and the bonds funding those railways were being pyramided through a European banking system that had become, in the jargon of the period, a giant confidence game resting on confidence in Vienna.

When Vienna broke, so did everything that had trusted it. Jay Cooke had bet his firm on the Northern Pacific Railway, which was essentially a speculation on European demand for American land-bond paper. When that demand evaporated in May, Cooke had no exit. His collapse on 18 September 1873 is often dated as the start of the American depression, but in truth he was just the most spectacular of about a hundred interconnected failures. Within two years, roughly a quarter of American railways were in receivership. Unemployment in major Northern cities ran above 25 per cent. There was no unemployment insurance, no deposit insurance, no central bank willing or legally able to act as lender of last resort.

What is genuinely interesting about the Long Depression is not the crash but what it did to politics. Three things happened almost simultaneously, in different countries, and none of them have been undone since.

First, free trade died. Germany, which had been the laboratory of European free trade under Bismarck's early governments, reversed course completely in 1879 with the "iron and rye" tariff — a protectionist coalition of Prussian landlords and Ruhr industrialists that would dominate German politics for the next forty years. France followed with the Méline Tariff of 1892. The United States, which had briefly flirted with tariff reduction, locked in McKinley-era protectionism instead. Britain was the only major industrial economy that clung to free trade, and it did so at the cost of watching its manufacturing base hollow out to Germany and America. The late-nineteenth-century tariff wall that we tend to associate with the 1930s actually dates to the 1880s. The Depression of 1929 inherited a protectionist world; it did not invent one.

Second, and more sinisterly, the Long Depression produced modern political anti-Semitism as a mass movement. The Austrian journalist Hermann Ahlwardt, the German pamphleteer Wilhelm Marr (who coined the word "antisemitismus" in 1879), the French agitator Édouard Drumont — all of them built their careers directly on the argument that the 1873 crash was a Jewish conspiracy. The claim was absurd, but it had a surface plausibility: many of the Viennese banks that had failed were Jewish-owned, and the gold standard that had tightened credit was associated with Rothschild finance. The Long Depression did not create Christian Europe's long tradition of Jew-hatred; it did something more specific and more dangerous. It converted a religious prejudice into an economic ideology, one that blamed a specific group for the opacity and cruelty of the new global financial system. Every subsequent convulsion of European anti-Semitism, up to and including the catastrophe of the 1940s, drew from the well that 1873 had dug.

Third, the Depression permanently discredited the idea that unregulated capitalism was self-correcting. The Social Democratic Party of Germany, founded in 1875, the British Fabian Society, the American Populist movement of the 1890s, the French solidarist tradition — all of them are children of the Long Depression. Before 1873, a serious European politician could defend the proposition that the state should do essentially nothing about the business cycle. After 1896, almost nobody outside a narrow liberal fringe believed that any more. The welfare state, progressive taxation, labour regulation, central banking as a public function — none of these were inevitable, and none of them would have happened on the timetable they did without twenty-three years of stagnation forcing the argument.

The uncomfortable thing about studying the Long Depression now is how familiar the shape of it feels. A credit boom financed by cross-border capital flows. A regulatory framework unequal to the pace of financial innovation. A political class that discovers, only after the collapse, that globalisation has obligations as well as benefits. A surge of nationalist and xenophobic politics feeding on the wreckage. A twenty-year argument about whether the old economic consensus can be patched up or has to be replaced entirely.

Economic historians like to debate whether 1873 was really a "depression" at all, since output eventually recovered and productivity actually grew through the period. This misses the point. A depression is not just a statistical event. It is a political experience — a generation-long loss of confidence in the idea that the existing order can deliver rising prosperity to ordinary people. By that test, 1873 to 1896 was a depression, and its deepest damage was not to GDP but to a particular nineteenth-century faith that markets, left alone, would bend toward justice. That faith did not survive the crash. Nothing since has really restored it.
