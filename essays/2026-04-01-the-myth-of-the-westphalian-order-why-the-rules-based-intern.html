The Myth of the Westphalian Order: Why the "Rules-Based International System" Never Actually Existed

The Myth of the Westphalian Order: Why the "Rules-Based International System" Never Actually Existed

The phrase "rules-based international order" gets invoked constantly — by Western diplomats, think-tank scholars, editorial boards. It is used to describe something worth defending, a structure of norms and institutions that constrains state behaviour and makes the world predictably peaceful. Russia violates it. China threatens it. Rogue states undermine it. The implication is that there is some stable edifice that was built, that functioned, and that is now under assault.

This is mostly a myth. Not entirely — institutions like the UN, WTO, and IMF do real work, and some norms genuinely constrain state behaviour at the margins. But the broader framing is historically illiterate. The "order" being mourned was never universal, never consistently enforced, and was always primarily a tool of the powerful rather than a neutral framework protecting the weak.

Start with Westphalia itself. The 1648 Peace of Westphalia is the origin story. Two treaties ended the Thirty Years' War and supposedly established the foundational principle of state sovereignty: that each state has supreme authority within its own borders, and no external power has the right to interfere. This is taught as the birth of the modern international system.

The problem is that Westphalia did no such thing. Leo Gross popularised the "Westphalian myth" in a 1948 International Organisation article, and historians of the period have been deflating it ever since. The actual treaties dealt mainly with German constitutional arrangements and the religious settlement within the Holy Roman Empire. The Empire continued to be a heterogeneous, layered political entity for another century and a half. The idea of Westphalia as the founding charter of sovereign equality was invented in the twentieth century, projected backwards onto 1648 by scholars who needed a clean origin story. The actual development of state sovereignty was messier, slower, and more contested.

But take the post-1945 order, the version people actually mean when they invoke "rules-based." The UN Charter prohibits aggressive war. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights was adopted in 1948. Decolonisation created dozens of new states. The Bretton Woods institutions built a framework for economic cooperation. This is real, and it matters.

Yet look at who built it and how it operated. The United States invaded or covertly destabilised more than a dozen governments between 1945 and 1990: Iran (1953), Guatemala (1954), the Dominican Republic (1965), Chile (1973), Nicaragua (throughout the 1980s), and so on. These were not aberrations. They were policy. The UN Security Council structure — five permanent members with veto power — was designed explicitly to exempt the great powers from the enforcement mechanisms they imposed on everyone else. The Soviets did the same: Hungary (1956), Czechoslovakia (1968), Afghanistan (1979). The "order" was always a set of rules for the middle powers, not for the strong.

This is not cynicism for its own sake. It matters for how we understand the present moment. When American officials describe China's actions in the South China Sea or Russia's invasion of Ukraine as violations of the rules-based order, they are not wrong — those actions do violate established norms. But the framing is selective in a way that conceals more than it reveals. The United States' own invasion of Iraq in 2003, conducted without Security Council authorisation on the basis of falsified intelligence, was by any coherent standard a violation of the same order. The difference is not legal but political: American power was sufficient to insulate the violation from consequences.

This brings us to the deeper theoretical question, which is whether a genuine rules-based international order is even possible given the anarchic structure of the international system. Realists from Thucydides onward have argued that it is not. In the absence of a world government with coercive power, international law and norms function only when the powerful choose to enforce them — which means they are instruments of power, not constraints upon it. John Mearsheimer's offensive realism holds that states in an anarchic system must always seek to maximise relative power, making genuine legal constraint structurally impossible.

The liberal institutionalist response — developed by Robert Keohane, Joseph Nye, and others — is that even without a world government, institutions can create durable cooperation by reducing transaction costs, facilitating information sharing, and raising the cost of defection. This is partially right. Trade regimes, arms control treaties, and international courts do shape state behaviour in meaningful ways. But they do so primarily among states that share overlapping interests and comparable power. Where interests diverge sharply and power is highly asymmetric, institutions routinely collapse or get ignored.

What we actually have is something more interesting and more honest than the "rules-based order" mythology: a contested, evolving system of norms and institutions that powerful states simultaneously construct, exploit, and violate, that provides real but uneven constraints on behaviour, and that is always a product of political settlements rather than moral progress. The current anxiety about the order's "erosion" is partly real — multipolarity does increase coordination problems — but it is also partly a loss of American hegemonic certainty masquerading as universal institutional decay.

None of this means that international institutions are worthless or that norms don't matter. They do. The prohibition on using nuclear weapons has held for eighty years, which is remarkable. The WTO's dispute resolution mechanism resolved hundreds of trade conflicts that might otherwise have escalated. International criminal tribunals have prosecuted genuine atrocities, even if slowly and selectively.

The point is that defending these things requires honesty about what they are: political achievements, not natural facts; tools that serve interests, not neutral frameworks above politics. Pretending otherwise does not strengthen the order. It weakens it, by making the selective enforcement visible as hypocrisy rather than honest as power politics, and by foreclosing serious thinking about what a more genuinely multilateral system might look like.

The mythology flatters the powerful and misleads the governed. The reality is harder but more useful: international order is built and maintained by specific states with specific interests, and it will only improve if those interests are contested and renegotiated, not if we pretend the edifice was always fair.
