# The Afternoon at Annual That Killed the Spanish Monarchy: 22 July 1921

On the afternoon of 22 July 1921, in a stony Rif valley about ninety kilometres south of Melilla, a Spanish colonial army of roughly twenty thousand men ceased to exist. By nightfall its commanding general, Manuel Fernández Silvestre, was dead — either by suicide or by Berber rifle, the body was never recovered — and a force of perhaps three thousand tribesmen under a former mine clerk named Abd el-Krim was advancing east through the Spanish positions with effectively no opposition. Within three weeks the Rif insurgents had taken back every Spanish post established in the eastern protectorate over the previous twelve years, killed somewhere between nine and thirteen thousand Spanish soldiers — the exact figure was never officially established because the army stopped counting around the eighth thousand — and pushed the frontier of the Spanish zone of Morocco back to the perimeter wire of Melilla itself.

**This is the worst defeat any European colonial power suffered in the twentieth century before Dien Bien Phu, and it is the moment that the Spanish constitutional monarchy of Alfonso XIII began to die.** The line from Annual to the Spanish Civil War, fifteen years later, is shorter and straighter than almost any other causal chain in interwar European history, and it runs through the institutional damage one afternoon did to the relationship between the Spanish army, the Spanish crown, and the Spanish parliament.

The conventional account of Annual treats it as a story of colonial incompetence: Silvestre overextended, his supply lines were impossible, his Moroccan auxiliaries deserted, the Rif terrain favoured guerrilla warfare, the African Army was riddled with corruption and political appointments. All true. But this is the way a defeat is described after it has been absorbed into a larger narrative of imperial decline. What it misses is the precise mechanism by which Annual broke the Restoration political settlement that had governed Spain since 1874.

That settlement, the *turno pacífico*, was a corrupt but stable arrangement under which the Liberal and Conservative parties alternated in office through managed elections, with the king arbitrating between them. It had survived the loss of Cuba and the Philippines in 1898; it had survived the *Semana Trágica* in Barcelona in 1909; it had survived the assassinations of three prime ministers between 1897 and 1921. What it could not survive was a question to which there was no acceptable answer.

That question was: who ordered the advance? Silvestre, an old cavalry comrade of the king, had pushed his lines forward through 1921 against the explicit caution of his immediate superior, the High Commissioner Dámaso Berenguer. He had done so, according to the testimony of his staff and the letters recovered from his headquarters at Melilla, on the strength of personal correspondence with Alfonso XIII himself, who was understood to want a quick and glorious campaign before the autumn. The famous telegram — "Olé tus huevos," roughly "way to go" — that Alfonso was alleged to have sent Silvestre on the eve of the advance was never produced and probably never existed, but the broader pattern of royal encouragement against ministerial advice was documented in detail by the Picasso Commission set up by the Cortes in August 1921 to investigate the disaster.

The Picasso file ran to over two thousand pages. It implicated the king directly in the operational decisions that produced Annual. It was scheduled to be debated in the Cortes on 13 September 1923. On 12 September 1923, General Miguel Primo de Rivera, the Captain General of Catalonia, issued a pronunciamento from his Barcelona headquarters, suspended the constitution, and dissolved parliament. The Picasso file was never debated. Alfonso XIII, instead of resisting the coup, telegraphed his support to Primo on 14 September and asked him to form a Military Directory. The constitutional monarchy of Spain effectively ended that morning, eight years before the king finally went into exile, and the proximate cause of its ending was that the alternative to the coup was a parliamentary debate in which the king would be named, on the record, as personally responsible for the deaths of nine thousand soldiers.

There is a structural lesson here about the failure modes of constitutional monarchies under conditions of imperial crisis. The Spanish system was built around the king as neutral arbiter. As long as the king's hands were clean — as long as the parties could be blamed for whatever went wrong — the system absorbed defeats. Cuba was the fault of the Conservatives; the *Semana Trágica* was the fault of the Liberals. But Annual could not be blamed on any party because the operation had bypassed party control. The king had run his own colonial policy through a personal favourite, and the personal favourite had got an army killed. Once that became the operative question, the only way to save the monarchy was to suspend the constitution that would otherwise have to answer it. Primo's coup was not a usurpation against Alfonso. It was a service Alfonso commissioned.

The longer chain runs through the men who were junior officers in Morocco during the Annual campaign and who built their careers, their fortunes, and their political identities in the colonial war that followed. The Spanish Foreign Legion was raised in 1920, specifically to fight in the Rif, by an officer named José Millán-Astray; his second-in-command and operational successor was a slight, pale, devout young major from Galicia named Francisco Franco, who was promoted to lieutenant colonel in 1923 and to brigadier general in 1926 on the strength of his record in the Moroccan war. The officers who would launch the July 1936 coup were, almost to a man, *africanistas* — veterans of the colonial campaigns whose worldview, methods of warfare, and contempt for civilian politicians had been formed in the long shadow of Annual.

The Republic that replaced Alfonso in 1931 inherited the consequences without the army that had produced them. It tried to reform the officer corps; the officer corps remembered Annual, remembered Picasso, and concluded that it would never again allow civilians to hold it accountable. **Annual did not cause the Spanish Civil War. It created the officer corps that did.** The afternoon in the Rif when an old cavalryman shot himself, or was shot, in a valley he should never have entered taught the Spanish army a lesson it spent the next fifteen years preparing to apply. The lesson was that political authority over the army was negotiable.

The bodies of the Spanish soldiers killed at Annual were left in the open for over a year before a recovery expedition could reach them. By the time the bones were collected, in the autumn of 1922, they had been picked clean. The Picasso Commission's papers, by contrast, were preserved intact, sealed by Primo in 1923, opened briefly by the Republic in 1931, and then quietly resealed when it became apparent that publishing them would have implicated half the senior generals of the new regime. They were finally released to historians in 1976. The dead at Annual outlasted the kingdom that killed them by exactly fifty-five years.
