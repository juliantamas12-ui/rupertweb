# The 55 Days That Killed the Italian Left: Aldo Moro and the Historic Compromise

On the morning of 16 March 1978, in Via Mario Fani in northern Rome, a unit of the Red Brigades ambushed a five-car convoy carrying Aldo Moro, president of the Christian Democrats and the architect of Italy's so-called *compromesso storico* — the historic compromise that was, that very morning, about to bring the Italian Communist Party into the parliamentary majority for the first time in the republic's history. Moro's five-man escort was killed in under ninety seconds with somewhere between forty and ninety rounds. Moro himself was taken alive. He was held for fifty-five days, tried in a "people's court", and on 9 May was shot eleven times in the boot of a red Renault 4 parked, with deliberate symbolism, halfway between the Christian Democrat and Communist Party headquarters in Via Caetani.

The conventional reading is that the Red Brigades, a small Marxist-Leninist terror cell, derailed Italy's evolution toward a left-Catholic governing coalition through a single spectacular act of violence. **This reading is correct in mechanism and wrong in agency.** The Red Brigades pulled the trigger. They did not decide that Moro should die. That decision was made by the Italian state — specifically, by the men who refused to negotiate — and it was made in full knowledge that refusal meant Moro's execution, because Moro himself, in eighty-six letters smuggled from his prison, said so explicitly.

The letters are the key document of post-war Italian politics. Moro wrote them to his wife, to the Pope, to Bettino Craxi, and above all to his own party. Their tone shifts from confused indignation in the first week to pleading in the second to icy clarity by the fourth. By mid-April Moro had grasped that his colleagues had decided he must not return alive. *"You don't want to save me,"* he wrote to the Christian Democrat leadership on 24 April. *"You are choosing not to."* He was, by then, entirely correct. The position adopted by the *fronte della fermezza* — the firmness front — was that the state could not be seen to negotiate with terrorists. The position was articulated by Giulio Andreotti, the prime minister; by Francesco Cossiga, the interior minister; by the Communist leader Enrico Berlinguer; and, with particular vehemence, by the American ambassador Richard Gardner, who communicated to the Italian government, in terms that have since been substantiated by the declassified State Department traffic, that the United States considered any negotiated release of Moro tantamount to a strategic catastrophe.

Why? Because Moro alive was a problem larger than Moro dead. The historic compromise — the project of bringing the largest Communist party in Western Europe into a coalition government with the Christian Democrats — was being negotiated at exactly the moment when the Carter administration, under pressure from a National Security Council dominated by Zbigniew Brzezinski, had decided that any communist presence in the cabinet of a NATO member was unacceptable. The position had been communicated to Rome in January 1978 in the so-called "non-paper" handed by Gardner to Andreotti, the existence of which the State Department denied for two decades and which we now have. The Italian Communist Party had abandoned the Soviet Union in everything but name. It had endorsed NATO. Berlinguer had publicly broken with Moscow over Czechoslovakia. None of this mattered to Washington. **What mattered was that if the PCI entered the Italian government, the French communists would point to Rome and demand the same. So would the Portuguese. The American grip on the southern flank of NATO would unravel within five years.**

Moro understood this. He had been told it directly by Henry Kissinger in 1974, in a conversation his widow, Eleonora, later testified to the parliamentary commission: Kissinger had warned him to abandon the project or "pay dearly". The remark was reported in 1978 to the magistrate Ferdinando Imposimato and dismissed as Kissinger's standard menace. After Moro's death his family treated it as an accusation.

The accusation does not have to be literal to be devastating. The point is not that the CIA murdered Aldo Moro. The point is that the Red Brigades' ideological hatred of the historic compromise — they considered the PCI's parliamentary turn a class betrayal — happened to align perfectly with the American interest in killing the same project, and that the Italian state, in the fifty-five days available to it, chose alignment with Washington over the life of its most senior politician. *Fermezza* was not a principle. It was a foreign policy decision dressed as one.

Consider the alternatives. The Israelis had negotiated with hijackers at Entebbe two years earlier. The West Germans had paid for Peter Lorenz in 1975. The Italian state itself had quietly traded prisoners with the Red Brigades on smaller scales before and would do so after. There was no doctrine of *fermezza* in Italian practice before March 1978 and there has not been one since. The doctrine existed for exactly the duration of Aldo Moro's captivity, and it existed because letting Moro live would have meant letting the historic compromise live.

What happened after 9 May is the proof. The Christian Democrat-Communist parliamentary majority that had been sworn in on the very day of the kidnapping — Andreotti's fourth government, the so-called *governo della non sfiducia* — survived only until January 1979. The Communists, denied any real share of executive power, were forced into formal opposition. By 1980 the PCI's electoral decline had begun. By 1989 the wall came down and the party dissolved itself. The historic compromise, as a serious project of bringing Italy's largest opposition party into government on its own terms, ended in the boot of the Renault in Via Caetani.

**The Red Brigades thought they were striking at the bourgeois state.** They were doing the bourgeois state's work for it. Mario Moretti, the brigatista who personally shot Moro and who is now an old man giving interviews from prison, has said in various forms that he believes this. He is not wrong. The lesson is not that terrorists can change history. The lesson is that terrorists, like everyone else, are sometimes useful idiots, and that the most consequential political assassination in post-war Western Europe was carried out by people who did not understand whose interests they were serving.

The Italian republic spent the next thirty years pretending none of this had happened. Two parliamentary commissions, six trials, three magistrates dead in unexplained circumstances, an entire wing of the Italian state — the P2 lodge, Operation Gladio, the *servizi deviati* — exposed and then quietly buried. Andreotti lived to be ninety-four and was acquitted of everything. Cossiga became president of the republic. The historic compromise became a footnote.

Moro, in his last letter to his wife, wrote: *"I do not wish to be surrounded, in this hour, by men who do not deserve to mourn me."* He got his wish. None of them came to the funeral. The Italian state buried him in private, in Torrita Tiberina, with no eulogy. They knew what they had done.
