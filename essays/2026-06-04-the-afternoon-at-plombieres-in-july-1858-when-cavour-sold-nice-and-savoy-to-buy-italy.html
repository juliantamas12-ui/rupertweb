The Afternoon at Plombières in July 1858 When Cavour Sold Nice and Savoy to Buy Italy

On the afternoon of Wednesday, 21 July 1858, in the spa town of Plombières-les-Bains in the Vosges, the Prime Minister of the Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont, Count Camillo Benso di Cavour, took a four-hour walk with the Emperor of the French, Napoleon III. They walked alone. No advisers, no minute-takers, no aides-de-camp. Cavour wrote up the conversation that evening in a letter to his king, Victor Emmanuel II, in French, fifteen pages, marked secrétissime, sent by special courier rather than the diplomatic bag. The letter is the entire documentary record of what passed between them. It is also one of the most consequential pieces of paper in nineteenth-century European history, because what those two men agreed to during the walk was the manufacture of a war.

The standard account of Italian unification dwells on Garibaldi's red shirts, Mazzini's republican exhortations, and the moral grandeur of the Risorgimento. All of that is real, and most of it is downstream. The thing that actually unified Italy was a cynical bilateral compact between a small north Italian kingdom and the Second French Empire, in which Piedmont agreed to provoke Austria into declaring war, France agreed to come to Piedmont's aid with two hundred thousand men, the two powers would together expel Austria from Lombardy and Venetia, and in return Piedmont would cede to France the County of Nice and the Duchy of Savoy. The cession was the price. The provocation was the mechanism. Everything else — the plebiscites, the volunteers, the Thousand sailing for Marsala — was either consequence or cover.

Consider what Cavour was buying and what he was paying with. He was buying a French army. Piedmont in 1858 had a peacetime strength of around sixty thousand men and a mobilisable strength of perhaps a hundred and forty thousand. The Austrian army in Lombardy-Venetia, under Gyulai, had a peacetime strength of two hundred thousand within Italy alone and could be reinforced from the empire by another two hundred thousand within six weeks. No coalition of Italian states could match these numbers, and no Italian state would join Piedmont in any case: the Two Sicilies were Bourbon and pro-Austrian, the Papal States were Papal and anti-Piedmontese, and the central duchies were Habsburg client states with Habsburg garrisons. Piedmont alone against Austria alone would lose the war in three weeks. Piedmont and France against Austria would beat Austria by autumn. The arithmetic was not complicated, and Cavour, who had been a financial speculator and a railway concessionaire before he was a statesman, did not pretend otherwise.

What he was paying with was the part of his kingdom that was not Italian. Nice was Niçois and spoke an Occitan dialect transitional with Ligurian; Savoy was Savoyard and spoke French. Neither had any plausible claim to belong to a future Italian state defined on linguistic or ethnic grounds. Both, however, were the dynastic heartland of the House of Savoy — Savoy in the strict sense of the word — and ceding them to France meant cutting the king of Piedmont off from the territory that gave his dynasty its name. Victor Emmanuel, when Cavour came back from Plombières and laid out the bargain, reportedly cried. He signed anyway. The treaty formalising the arrangement was concluded at Turin on 24 January 1859, six months after the walk.

The provocation was the hard part, because Napoleon III had told Cavour at Plombières — Cavour records this carefully — that France could intervene only if Austria appeared to be the aggressor. French public opinion would not tolerate an offensive war in Italy on behalf of a small Italian power, and the Catholic right, which Napoleon needed at home, would not tolerate any war that looked as if it were aimed at the Papacy. The casus belli therefore had to be constructed so that Austria fired the first shot. Cavour spent the first four months of 1859 on this single problem. He inflamed the Italian press against Austria. He encouraged volunteers from Lombardy to cross into Piedmont and form units of the Sardinian army, knowing that this was a violation of the Austrian rights of recruitment under the 1815 settlement. He held military reviews in Alessandria that were obviously designed to be seen from Milan. He cultivated rumours, in the British and French papers, that Piedmont was about to invade Lombardy. By March, the Austrian government was demanding that Piedmont demobilise. By 23 April, after a final round of British mediation through Lord Cowley collapsed because Cavour deliberately rejected terms he had himself privately accepted three days earlier, the Austrian ambassador in Turin presented an ultimatum giving Piedmont three days to demobilise or face war. Cavour rejected it within an hour of receiving it. Austrian troops crossed the Ticino on 29 April. The casus belli existed. France declared war on 3 May. The plan from Plombières was now operational.

The war ran for ten weeks. The French and Piedmontese won the battles of Magenta on 4 June and Solferino on 24 June, the latter being the bloodiest single day of European combat between Waterloo and the Somme — forty thousand casualties across roughly three hundred thousand engaged — and the proximate occasion for Henri Dunant's founding of the Red Cross four years later. By the first week of July, Austrian forces had been pushed back to the Quadrilateral fortresses on the eastern edge of Lombardy. The road to Venetia was open. At this point, on 11 July, Napoleon III met the Austrian Emperor Franz Joseph at Villafranca and signed, without consulting Cavour and without informing the Piedmontese command, a preliminary peace under which Austria ceded Lombardy but kept Venetia. The Plombières plan had been carried out two-thirds of the way and then unilaterally truncated.

The reasons for the truncation are well documented. Casualties at Solferino had been higher than Napoleon had stomach for; Prussia had begun mobilising along the Rhine and Napoleon was afraid of a war on two fronts; the prospect of a strong unified Italian state on the French southern border was beginning to look less attractive than a weak federation he could play off against Austria; and the French Catholic right had begun threatening to break with the regime over the threat to the Papal States. Cavour, when he heard the news at Monzambano on the evening of 11 July, resigned the next morning. He was back in office by January 1860.

What is interesting about the period that followed is that the Plombières framework — provoke, intervene, cede — kept generating Italian unification even after Plombières itself had been abandoned by one of its signatories. The central Italian duchies of Tuscany, Modena, Parma, and the Romagna, whose Austrian-backed dukes had fled during the war, held plebiscites in March 1860 and voted overwhelmingly to join Piedmont. France permitted this in exchange for an accelerated handover of Nice and Savoy under treaties signed at Turin on 24 March. Garibaldi's Thousand sailed from Quarto on 5 May 1860 with the half-knowledge and half-consent of Cavour, conquered Sicily by August and Naples by September, and were absorbed into the Piedmontese system at Teano on 26 October. The Kingdom of Italy was proclaimed on 17 March 1861. The whole thing took thirty-two months from the walk in the Vosges.

What Plombières demonstrates, and what most accounts of the Risorgimento prefer not to dwell on, is that nineteenth-century state-making was not primarily a process of national awakening. It was a process of bilateral bargaining between professional cabinets, in which national feeling was a useful pretext, popular enthusiasm was a useful tool, and territory was the currency. Cavour was not a romantic. He referred to Mazzini privately as a fanatic and to Garibaldi as a useful instrument. He did not believe that the inhabitants of Naples and the inhabitants of Turin were a single people in any meaningful pre-political sense, and he was right not to believe it: the brigantaggio wars in the south after 1861 killed more Italians than the wars of independence had killed Austrians. He believed that he could, by careful management of foreign cabinets, assemble a north Italian state with a French alliance and then use the resulting prestige to absorb the rest. He was correct, and he was correct because he treated his own population as one of several variables in an equation whose primary terms were French divisions and Austrian fortresses.

The afternoon at Plombières is interesting now, in 2026, for a reason that has nothing to do with the moral status of the Risorgimento. It is interesting because it is one of the cleanest historical examples of a small state successfully outsourcing its security to a large one while retaining the political initiative — a thing that small states usually do not manage and that the literature on alliance theory generally says they cannot manage. Cavour managed it by being willing to pay in advance, in territory rather than promises, and by being willing to manufacture the war on his own timetable rather than wait for one to arrive. The cost was the part of his kingdom that he could most plausibly do without. The return was the rest of the peninsula. Whether it was worth it is a question on which the inhabitants of Nice, who lost their kingdom, and the inhabitants of Lombardy, who gained one, have always disagreed. The bookkeeping, from Cavour's chair, balances. Most state-makers do not get balances that clean, and Cavour got his because he understood, four hours into a walk in a Vosges spa town, exactly what he was buying and exactly what he was selling.

He died on 6 June 1861, eleven weeks after the proclamation, of a fever contracted in the Po valley. He was fifty. His last recorded words were not the famous ones about a free church in a free state, which are an invention of his secretary, but a quieter remark to his physician about whether the Romagna tax assessments had been finalised. The man was, to the end, a bookkeeper. Italy was a balance sheet that closed.
