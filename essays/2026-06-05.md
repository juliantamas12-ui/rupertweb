# The Afternoon Aboard the Polar Star at Björkö in July 1905 When the Kaiser Got the Tsar to Sign Away the French Alliance and the Russian Cabinet Quietly Tore It Up

On the afternoon of Monday, 24 July 1905, in the lee of a granite islet in the Gulf of Finland called Björkö, two imperial yachts lay at anchor about a cable's length apart. The *Hohenzollern* belonged to Wilhelm II of Germany. The *Polar Star* belonged to Nicholas II of Russia. Between them, on the deck of the *Polar Star*, in the presence of exactly one witness on each side — Heinrich von Tschirschky for Germany, Admiral Alexei Birilev for Russia — the two emperors signed a treaty of mutual defence. It bound Germany and Russia to come to one another's aid if either was attacked by a European power. It was, in its operational meaning, a unilateral repudiation of the Franco-Russian Alliance of 1894, the cornerstone of the European state system. Wilhelm wept when Nicholas signed. He wrote that night in his diary that a great weight had lifted from him and that the dead would rise from their graves in joy. By the second week of October the treaty was dead. It had been killed not by France, not by Britain, not by any foreign chancery, but by two Russian ministers — Vladimir Lamsdorf at the Foreign Ministry and Sergei Witte, just returned from Portsmouth — who refused to countersign. Nicholas, after a fortnight of trying to insist, gave way. The treaty was never registered, never ratified, never enforced. It is one of the cleanest examples in the modern record of a sovereign signing his name to a document that turned out to be worth nothing because the ministerial apparatus he depended on declined to validate it.

The standard textbook treatment of the pre-1914 alliance system runs through the obvious chain: Bismarck's Reinsurance Treaty lapses in 1890, France and Russia sign in 1894, the Anglo-Japanese Alliance in 1902, the Entente Cordiale in 1904, the Anglo-Russian Convention in 1907, the camps are locked in by 1908, the rest is bookkeeping until Sarajevo. **What this chain conceals is that the entire system almost reversed itself in the summer of 1905, and that the reason it did not was a quirk of Russian constitutional practice that did not appear in any law book.** No statute required the Tsar to obtain a ministerial countersignature on a treaty. Custom required it. The custom held. The system held with it. Without the custom, the rails of European history fork.

Consider what each man thought he was doing aboard the *Polar Star*. Wilhelm had been pursuing Nicholas, in person and by letter, since the Russo-Japanese war began in February 1904. The *Willy-Nicky* correspondence, eventually some seventy-five letters in execrable English, is one of the strangest documents of the period: a German emperor coaching a Russian one, in the second person, on how to detach himself from his French alliance, on how to view Britain as the real enemy, on the racial necessity of a Continental bloc against the Anglo-Saxons. Wilhelm believed, with some justification, that Russia's catastrophe in the Far East — Port Arthur fallen, the Baltic Fleet annihilated at Tsushima in May, revolution flickering through Russian cities — had broken France's confidence in its ally and broken Russian confidence in France. France had refused to lend Russia the money to continue the war on terms Russia could stomach. France had refused diplomatic cover at Algeciras. France was, in Wilhelm's reading, finished as a Russian partner, and the moment to detach Russia was now, before Witte returned from negotiating peace with Japan at Portsmouth and reasserted his pro-French line at the Foreign Ministry.

Nicholas's reading was simpler, and more characteristic of him. He liked Wilhelm. He was tired. He had been at sea on his yacht for two weeks to escape Tsarskoye Selo and the news from Manchuria. Wilhelm steamed up to Björkö on a courtesy visit that had been arranged weeks in advance, came aboard the *Polar Star* for lunch on the afternoon of the 24th, and produced from his pocket a draft treaty that he had had Bernhard von Bülow's chief assistant draft in Berlin a fortnight earlier. The text was four articles long. Article I bound each empire to come to the aid of the other if attacked in Europe. Article II forbade either from concluding a separate peace. Article III committed both to invite France to accede once the treaty was in force. Article IV deferred its operation until the end of the Russo-Japanese war. **Wilhelm had drafted it precisely so that Nicholas could tell himself he was not abandoning France: he was, on paper, inviting France into a new tripartite arrangement.** This was a fiction. France could not accede to a treaty whose entire point was to neutralise her existing arrangement with Russia, and Wilhelm and Bülow knew it. The Article III clause was decoration. It was there so that Nicholas could sign without feeling that he was betraying anyone.

He signed at about four o'clock. He signed first, in pencil, then in ink. Wilhelm signed after. Tschirschky and Birilev countersigned as witnesses. None of the four men present had the authority to bind a sovereign state to a treaty without ministerial ratification, but the treaty contained no such clause, and Wilhelm took the position that it was operational from the moment of signature. He went back to the *Hohenzollern* in tears, embraced his entourage, telegraphed Bülow in coded German that the morning of 24 July 1905 had been a turning point in European history, and ordered a hundred-and-one-gun salute that he then, on reflection, countermanded for fear of giving the game away. The treaty existed. The Russian Empire was, for a few weeks in the summer of 1905, on paper, a German ally.

What happened next is the actual mechanism of the story. Nicholas returned to Peterhof on 27 July. He did not at first tell Lamsdorf, the Foreign Minister, what he had signed. He waited until he was back in his own study to mention, in passing, that Wilhelm had given him an interesting paper at Björkö. He produced the text. Lamsdorf read it through once and went white. He read it again and asked the Tsar whether the document had been shown to anyone at the Foreign Ministry before signature. The Tsar said it had not. Lamsdorf, who was a quiet, careful, professional diplomat who had spent twenty years building the French alliance into the load-bearing wall of Russian foreign policy, asked permission to consider his response and withdrew. He did not resign. He went to his office, summoned the senior counsellors of the ministry, locked the text in his safe, and spent the next four days writing a memorandum.

The memorandum, which survives in the Russian Foreign Ministry archives at Moscow and was published in part in the Soviet collection *Mezhdunarodnye otnosheniya v epokhu imperializma* in 1937, makes three arguments. First, the Björkö treaty was incompatible with the Franco-Russian Alliance of 1894 because the 1894 alliance specifically committed Russia to come to France's aid if France were attacked by Germany, and the Björkö treaty committed Russia to come to Germany's aid if Germany were attacked by a European power, which manifestly included France. The two obligations could not be simultaneously performed. Second, Russia could not afford to repudiate the 1894 alliance because Russia had borrowed approximately ten billion francs from French capital markets between 1888 and 1905, the French government had quietly underwritten the entire programme, and any rupture would precipitate a collapse of Russian credit on the Paris bourse at the precise moment when Russia needed further loans to stabilise the currency after Tsushima. **The Björkö treaty would, within six months, bankrupt the Russian state.** Third, the treaty was procedurally invalid because it had been signed without ministerial countersignature, which was not a legal requirement but a settled custom and an indispensable practical one: no Russian ministry would administer a treaty it had not approved, and the Foreign Ministry, the Finance Ministry, and the War Ministry would all decline.

Lamsdorf showed the memorandum to Witte on 1 September, the day after Witte returned from Portsmouth with the Japanese treaty in his pocket and the prestige of having extracted from Theodore Roosevelt's mediation a peace much better than Russia had any military right to expect. Witte read the Björkö text and remarked, drily, that the Tsar had been outmanoeuvred by a German amateur. He concurred with Lamsdorf's analysis in every particular and added a fourth argument of his own, on the financial side: not only would the treaty crash the bourse, but it would crash the rouble on a timetable that would make the survival of the autocracy itself uncertain through the revolutionary winter of 1905–06. The two ministers presented the memorandum jointly to Nicholas at Peterhof on the morning of 7 October.

Nicholas resisted for almost three weeks. He wrote to Wilhelm on 7 October — a long, embarrassed letter, in English — proposing to add a clause specifying that the treaty did not apply if Germany were at war with France. Wilhelm replied, furiously, that this was the equivalent of tearing it up. He was correct. Nicholas wrote again on the 23rd, this time confessing that his ministers would not countersign and that without their countersignature he could not bind Russia. Wilhelm replied on the 27th with one of the most extraordinary letters ever sent by one sovereign to another: he reminded Nicholas that what they had signed had been signed *before God*, that no constitutional formality could dissolve such an obligation, and that he, Wilhelm, would hold his cousin to it on Judgement Day. **The letter was filed at the Russian Foreign Ministry without reply. The treaty was dead. The custom had won. The French alliance held.**

The Björkö episode is interesting now because it puts a measurable price on a procedural norm. Russian autocracy had no written constitution in 1905. The Tsar's word was, in formal theory, the law. In practice, sovereignty was distributed across a ministerial apparatus that the Tsar could not run without and that he could not override on a matter requiring administrative execution. The cost of the norm, to Wilhelm, was the encirclement of Germany — which proceeded on schedule, was completed by the Anglo-Russian Convention of August 1907, and produced, on a nine-year delay, the configuration that destroyed his throne. The cost of the norm, to Nicholas, was the loss of an exit that might — and the counterfactual is not absurd — have kept Russia out of the Great War. The cost of the norm, to the international system, was the locking-in of the bloc structure that detonated in 1914.

The deeper point is that constitutional structures, in the period before 1914, were not the formal documents that constitutional lawyers studied. They were settled administrative customs, often unwritten, sometimes barely noticed by the people upholding them. The Russian Empire was held together in foreign policy by the fact that Lamsdorf would not countersign a piece of paper he had not seen drafted. That is a thin reed on which to hang a continental order. **It held until July 1914, when the same ministerial apparatus, having spent nine years investing in the French alliance, marched Russia into the war it had been preserving the alliance to prevent.** The men who saved the alliance at Björkö killed the empire at Petrograd. They were the same men. The norm that worked one time worked the other.

What Wilhelm never grasped, and what Nicholas grasped too late, was that signing one's name to a paper is not the act of binding a state. The act of binding a state is the alignment of the apparatus that will execute the paper. Wilhelm had got the signature. He had not got the apparatus. The apparatus, in autocratic Russia in 1905, lived in two adjoining offices on the Palace Embankment, and it said no.
