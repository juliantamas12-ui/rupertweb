The Morning the Bundesbank Killed Bretton Woods: 5 May 1971

On Wednesday morning, 5 May 1971, at approximately 9:35 in Frankfurt, the Bundesbank's foreign exchange desk stopped quoting a price for the United States dollar and walked away from the market. By 10:30, four other European central banks — the Dutch, Belgian, Swiss, and Austrian — had done the same. By the end of the trading day, the gold-dollar standard that had organised the world economy since the Bretton Woods conference of July 1944 had effectively ceased to function, though it would take President Nixon another fifteen weeks to publicly recognise this and close the gold window. The decision to stop intervening was taken in a small back room of the Bundesbank's Frankfurt headquarters by perhaps eight men, the most senior of whom was Karl Klasen, the bank's president. There was no announcement. There was no consultation with the Federal Republic's main creditors. There was barely a consultation with the Federal Chancellery in Bonn. The Bundesbank simply stopped buying dollars, and a global monetary order ended.

This is the moment the long peace of the post-war financial settlement died. Not 15 August 1971, when Nixon went on television and announced the closure of the gold window, the import surcharge, and the wage-price freeze. Not December 1971 at the Smithsonian. Not March 1973, when the major currencies finally floated. The system died on a Wednesday morning in Frankfurt, when the central bank of America's most important Cold War creditor decided that defending the dollar at one to 3.66 Deutschmarks was no longer compatible with German monetary sovereignty.

The standard account treats August 1971 as the inflection point — the "Nixon Shock," named, in a piece of historical projection, after the Japanese term Nikuson shokku. This is wrong in the same way that treating the abdication of the Kaiser as the end of the German Empire is wrong: it confuses the public funeral with the private death. By August 1971, the corpse had been on the slab for three months. What Nixon did on the evening of the 15th was confirm a fact already established in the Frankfurt market by Karl Klasen and Otmar Emminger in the first week of May.

The pressure had been building for two years. The Federal Reserve under William McChesney Martin and then Arthur Burns had run an extraordinarily loose monetary policy through 1970 and into 1971, partly to finance the Vietnam War and the Great Society programmes simultaneously without raising taxes, partly because Burns had been appointed by Nixon with the explicit understanding that re-election in 1972 required cheap money. The American current account had deteriorated from a surplus of $2.3 billion in 1964 to a deficit of $1.4 billion in the first quarter of 1971; the capital account was haemorrhaging dollars at a rate that would reach $20 billion for the year. Eurodollar deposits — dollar liabilities held outside the United States and therefore outside the reserve requirements of the Federal Reserve — had grown from $9 billion in 1964 to $57 billion by the end of 1970. Every one of those Eurodollars was a claim on the United States gold stock at $35 the ounce. The United States gold stock was $10.5 billion.

The Germans understood this with greater clarity than anyone else for a specific institutional reason. The Bundesbank had been established in 1957 with a statutory independence drafted explicitly in reaction to the two hyperinflations of 1923 and 1948. Its mandate was the stability of the currency, and that mandate was constitutional in the precise sense that the bank's officials understood themselves to be defending the social contract of the Federal Republic against any threat — including the threat of imported American inflation. By the spring of 1971, German consumer prices were rising at 4.5 per cent year-on-year, an extraordinary number by the standards of the Wirtschaftswunder, and the Bundesbank's economists had concluded that approximately two-thirds of the increase could be traced directly to the monetary consequences of dollar inflows.

The mechanism was simple and visible to anyone who could read a balance sheet. Every dollar the Bundesbank bought to defend the parity at 3.66 was a dollar added to its reserves and a Deutschmark added to the German monetary base. In the first four months of 1971, the Bundesbank had been forced to absorb $7.5 billion at the official rate — more than the entire intervention of the previous five years combined. On the morning of 5 May alone, between the opening of the market and 9:35, the bank had bought $1 billion in forty minutes. At that pace, the inflationary consequences for the German economy in 1972 would have been catastrophic, and the Bundesbank's directorate calculated — with the cold institutional clarity that is the bank's signature — that the political cost of breaking the parity was now lower than the political cost of maintaining it.

The decision to walk away from the market was therefore not, in any meaningful sense, an emergency. It was the execution of an analysis that the bank's research department had been refining for eighteen months. What was extraordinary was the unilateralism. Klasen did inform Chancellor Brandt's office on the evening of 4 May, but neither Brandt nor Economics Minister Karl Schiller — who was opposed to floating — was consulted on the timing. The IMF was not consulted. The US Treasury was not consulted. Connally, Nixon's Treasury Secretary, learned of the suspension from a wire service report. The Bundesbank, in defending its statutory mandate, had unilaterally rewritten the rules of an international monetary system to which the Federal Republic was a signatory.

The deepest consequence is not the end of fixed exchange rates. It is the establishment of a precedent that would govern the next fifty years of European monetary politics. A national central bank, acting in defence of price stability against the inflationary pressure of a fiscally dominant partner, could and would break a multilateral commitment when the cost of compliance became unacceptable. This is the institutional DNA of every subsequent German position in the European Monetary System, in the negotiations over the Maastricht convergence criteria, in the Bundesbank's resistance to the Italian re-entry to the ERM in 1996, in the Karlsruhe court's rulings on the European Stability Mechanism, and — most consequentially — in the constitutional posture of the European Central Bank itself, whose mandate is a direct lineal descent from the Bundesbank statute of 1957. The European monetary order of the twenty-first century was, in its essentials, designed in Frankfurt on a Wednesday morning in May 1971, when a small group of central bankers decided that no international obligation could override the duty to defend the value of the currency.

There is a final twist that the conventional histories tend to elide. Karl Klasen, the man who made the call, had spent the war as a Wehrmacht artillery officer on the Eastern Front and the subsequent twenty years as a senior banker at the Deutsche Bank. He had lived, personally, through the destruction of two German currencies in his lifetime. When he was asked, years later, why the Bundesbank had acted without informing Washington, he replied that the question had not occurred to him. The Bundesbank's duty was to the holders of Deutschmarks. The dollar's problems, he said, were America's problems. It is the most concise summary ever produced of the institutional philosophy that would, within a generation, reshape the European continent.

  • Harold James, International Monetary Cooperation Since Bretton Woods (IMF/Oxford, 1996) — the standard institutional history of the system's design, operation, and collapse
  • Otmar Emminger, D-Mark, Dollar, Währungskrisen: Erinnerungen eines ehemaligen Bundesbankpräsidenten (DVA, 1986) — first-person account by the man who was at Klasen's side on 5 May, indispensable for the internal dynamics

  • Allan H. Meltzer, A History of the Federal Reserve, Volume 2, Book 2: 1970-1986 (Chicago, 2009) — the American side of the policy failure, including the Burns-Nixon relationship
  • Carl-Ludwig Holtfrerich, "Monetary Policy in Germany Since 1948" in Monetary Policy in the United States and Germany (Macmillan, 1999) — for the institutional and statutory framework of Bundesbank independence
  • Deutsche Bundesbank, Monatsbericht, May and June 1971 — the contemporaneous official documentation of the suspension and its rationale
