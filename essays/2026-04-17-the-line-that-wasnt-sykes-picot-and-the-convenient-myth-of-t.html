The Line That Wasn't: Sykes-Picot and the Convenient Myth of the Artificial Border

The Line That Wasn't: Sykes-Picot and the Convenient Myth of the Artificial Border

Ask almost anyone why the Middle East is unstable and you will hear the same answer delivered with great confidence: the Sykes-Picot Agreement. In 1916, the story goes, two imperial bureaucrats — a British diplomat named Mark Sykes and a French one named François Georges-Picot — sat down, drew arbitrary lines through the desert, carved up Arab lands with no regard for ethnic or tribal realities, and bequeathed a century of misery to the people living there. It is a tidy explanation. It is also, in almost every important particular, wrong.

This is worth taking seriously, because the Sykes-Picot myth is not merely a historical error. It is an actively harmful intellectual habit — one that assigns agency to a dead agreement rather than to the living decisions of governments, movements, and leaders who had every opportunity to build something different and often chose not to.

What Sykes-Picot Actually Said

The agreement, signed in May 1916, divided the Arab territories of the collapsing Ottoman Empire into British and French spheres of influence. The French were to control a coastal zone running from Cilicia down through Lebanon and Syria. The British would control Mesopotamia and the area around the Persian Gulf. Palestine would be placed under international administration. The rest — a vast inland territory — was to be nominally Arab states under British and French "protection," which is to say, manipulation.

Here is the problem with blaming this agreement for the map that emerged: the borders of the modern Middle East do not follow Sykes-Picot. The actual states — Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Jordan, Palestine — were drawn between 1920 and 1922 through a series of subsequent agreements, League of Nations mandates, and bilateral deals that differed substantially from what Sykes and Picot envisioned. France got Syria but lost the inland buffer zone. Britain got Palestine in its entirety rather than sharing it. The 1920 Treaty of Sèvres, the 1920 San Remo Conference, the 1921 Cairo Conference, and the 1922 League of Nations Mandate charters were the operative documents. The borders you see on a map today are the product of these later decisions, not of a single secret wartime accord.

David Fromkin's exhaustive history of the period, A Peace to End All Peace (1989), makes this plain. The Sykes-Picot Agreement was a wartime contingency plan that was partially superseded almost immediately by the military realities of the Arab Revolt, the Balfour Declaration, and the collapse of the Ottoman army. It was a starting point, not an end state.

The Incoherence of "Artificial"

But suppose we grant, for the sake of argument, that the British and French did draw the lines. Does that make those lines uniquely illegitimate?

Every border in the world is artificial. The borders of France were drawn through centuries of dynastic conquest and military expansion that had nothing to do with the preferences of Basques, Alsatians, or Bretons. The borders of Germany were redrawn twice in the twentieth century by the victors of two world wars. The borders of the United States were drawn through a combination of purchase, treaty, and violent dispossession of indigenous peoples. When we call Middle Eastern borders "artificial," we are applying a standard to one region that we do not apply to any other, and this asymmetry should make us suspicious about whose interests the argument serves.

The political scientist Avi Shlaim, writing about the Arab-Israeli conflict, noted that the problem in the region was never primarily that borders were drawn in the wrong places. It was that the political entities created within those borders — whether the British Mandate in Palestine, the French Mandate in Syria, or the artificially accelerated state of Iraq — were governed in ways designed to prevent, rather than foster, the development of genuine political institutions. Divide-and-rule was not an accident of poor cartography. It was deliberate policy.

What Was Actually Built — and Deliberately Not Built

When Britain took control of Mesopotamia after 1918, it was governing a territory with real administrative traditions: Ottoman-era bureaucracies, tribal structures, urban merchant classes, and Shia clerical networks that had their own forms of authority and legitimacy. The British looked at this complexity and chose, almost immediately, to simplify it by installing a foreign Hashemite king — Faisal I, a man from the Hejaz with no prior connection to Iraq — and constructing a state around Sunni Arab officers who had served in the Ottoman military. The Shia majority was systematically excluded from power. Kurdish regions were never meaningfully integrated. This was not a mistake of geography. It was a choice about who would be governable.

In Syria, the French dismembered the territory into confessional mini-states — the State of Greater Lebanon, the State of Syria, the State of Jabal Druze, the Alawite State — explicitly to prevent the emergence of a unified Arab political entity that might resist French control. Eugene Rogan's The Arabs: A History (2009) documents how the French systematically favored minority communities, armed irregular forces against Arab nationalist movements, and suppressed the Syrian Revolt of 1925-1927 with violence that leveled entire neighborhoods of Damascus.

The "artificial borders" narrative transforms this active political engineering into passive geographic bad luck. It lets France and Britain off the hook. But it also lets subsequent Arab governments off the hook, which is a more uncomfortable point to press.

The Myth's Political Uses

The Sykes-Picot narrative is popular for a reason: it is useful to almost everyone. Arab nationalist movements used it to explain their failures without interrogating them. ISIS, when it declared its caliphate in 2014, literally bulldozed a checkpoint between Syria and Iraq and released a video called "The End of Sykes-Picot" — treating a century-old agreement as though it were still the primary political fact of the region. Western liberals use it to explain Middle Eastern instability as a uniquely European sin, which is self-flagellating but also conveniently simple. Repressive Arab governments use it to argue that state borders are illegitimate and that therefore the nation-state itself is illegitimate, which handily undermines citizenship-based demands for accountability.

Everyone finds Sykes-Picot useful. That should make you trust it less.

The harder questions — about why authoritarian consolidation took the forms it did in post-independence Iraq, Syria, and Egypt; about why pan-Arab nationalism failed on its own terms; about the relationship between oil wealth and the arrested development of civic institutions — do not resolve into a story about two men drawing a line. But they are the questions that actually explain what you are looking at when you look at a map of the contemporary Middle East.
