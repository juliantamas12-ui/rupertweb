The Sixty Hours in August 1991 When the Soviet Coup Lost the Television War

On the morning of Monday, 19 August 1991, at 06:00 Moscow time, Radio Moscow interrupted its scheduled programming and read out, twice, a document called Statement of the Soviet Leadership. It announced that Mikhail Gorbachev was unable to perform his duties on grounds of health and that a State Committee for the State of Emergency — the Gosudarstvenny Komitet po Chrezvychainomu Polozheniyu, abbreviated as GKChP — had assumed his powers. The committee consisted of eight men: the Vice-President Gennady Yanayev, the Prime Minister Valentin Pavlov, the Defence Minister Dmitry Yazov, the KGB Chairman Vladimir Kryuchkov, the Interior Minister Boris Pugo, the chairman of the Soviet of the Union Anatoly Lukyanov in a quasi-formal capacity, and two industrial association heads who were added largely to give the list a non-uniformed look. The statement was followed by Decree No. 1, which banned demonstrations, closed independent newspapers, imposed a six-month state of emergency, and ordered the army back to barracks readiness across the entire Soviet Union. By 07:00, columns of the Tamanskaya and Kantemirovskaya armoured divisions were rolling into Moscow from the south and west. By 09:00, four tank battalions had taken up positions around the Kremlin, the Lubyanka, the Ostankino television tower, the central telegraph office, and — almost as an afterthought — the building of the Supreme Soviet of the Russian Federation, known then and since as the White House. By midday, on paper, the Soviet Union was under military government.

Sixty hours later, on the evening of Wednesday 21 August, Yanayev was drunk and weeping in his Kremlin office, Pugo had shot himself and his wife, Kryuchkov was on a plane to Foros being flown there under arrest to apologise to a furious Gorbachev, and Boris Yeltsin was on the balcony of the White House addressing a crowd of perhaps a hundred and fifty thousand people who had spent two nights camped in the rain on Krasnopresnenskaya Embankment. The Soviet Union had two more days of unitary existence after the coup collapsed — until the Ukrainian declaration of independence on 24 August, after which the unwinding was procedural. The standard explanation for what happened in those sixty hours is that the army refused to fire on the crowd, that Yeltsin's tank-top speech crystallised popular resistance, and that the plotters were too drunk and incompetent to follow through. All three are true. None of them is the actual mechanism.

The actual mechanism was that the GKChP lost the broadcast.

Consider what the plotters did with television. At 09:25 on 19 August, Gostelradio cancelled all scheduled programming on all four central channels and began broadcasting, on a loop, the Statement of the Soviet Leadership, the decrees, classical music — predominantly Tchaikovsky's Swan Lake, which entered Russian folk memory thereafter as the music of failed putsches — and a long pre-recorded interview with Gennady Yanayev. The interview, given to the Vremya news programme at 17:00 on the 19th, is now the single most famous piece of footage of the coup. Yanayev is visibly drunk. His hands shake throughout. He sweats. He claims that Gorbachev is resting in Crimea and being looked after by competent doctors. He cannot answer a question from the CBS correspondent Stuart Loory about whether the Supreme Soviet had authorised the state of emergency. He concludes by saying that he hopes Mikhail Sergeyevich will be back at work soon. The footage was broadcast at peak viewing hour to an audience of approximately 180 million people. It was, by 18:00 on the first evening, the only public statement any member of the GKChP had made on camera, and it had communicated, with perfect clarity, that the men who claimed to have replaced Gorbachev were terrified of what they had done.

By contrast, consider what Yeltsin's people did. At 12:30 on 19 August, six and a half hours after the radio announcement, Yeltsin arrived at the Russian Supreme Soviet building from his dacha at Arkhangelskoye in an unarmoured Volga. He was not arrested on the way, which is the single most consequential failure of the coup: Kryuchkov had ordered the KGB's Alpha Group to detain Yeltsin at the dacha at 04:00 that morning, and Major-General Viktor Karpukhin, the Alpha commander, had stalled, kept the unit at its base in Kuntsevo, and eventually told Kryuchkov that the operation was being prepared, when in fact it never was. Yeltsin's car drove past Alpha checkpoints that had been ordered to stop it and were not stopped. He arrived at the White House. He went straight up to the office of the Russian Vice-President Alexander Rutskoi. At 12:45 he wrote, in longhand, on a sheet of foolscap, an Address to the Citizens of Russia in which he declared the GKChP unconstitutional and called for a general strike. At 13:00, accompanied by Rutskoi, Burbulis, Khasbulatov, and a Russian television cameraman named Sergei Skvortsov who happened to have brought a Betacam from the Russian state television studio that morning, he walked downstairs and out of the front door of the building, climbed onto the hull of Tank No. 110 of the Tamanskaya Division's 12th Battalion, and read the address aloud.

The reading took four minutes. Skvortsov filmed it. The tape was driven by motorcycle courier to the British embassy on the Sofiyskaya Embankment, which had a satellite uplink to Reuters in London by 14:30. By 15:00 Moscow time — two hours after the reading and six hours before Yanayev's Vremya interview — the footage was being broadcast on CNN, BBC, ARD, NHK, and every other major Western network. By 17:00 it had been re-imported into the Soviet Union by Radio Liberty's Russian Service from Munich, which was being listened to in clear by an estimated forty per cent of the urban Soviet population because the KGB jamming stations had been ordered switched off in 1988 and never reactivated. By 19:00, when Yanayev's interview aired on Vremya, the same Soviet citizens who watched it had already heard or seen, via foreign broadcast, a thirty-two-year-old grandson of a kulak standing on a tank in front of their own parliament telling them that the men they were now watching on Vremya were criminals.

The plotters had a television monopoly inside the Soviet Union for sixty hours and could not use it, because what they had to broadcast was Yanayev sweating, Swan Lake, and decrees nobody believed. Yeltsin had four minutes of footage and the entire foreign broadcast ecosystem of 1991 — CNN's first full-scale international crisis coverage, the post-glasnost short-wave market, the Western satellite networks that the perestroika-era Soviet population had learned to watch via VCR copies and dish receivers in dacha settlements outside the major cities — and he used those four minutes to define the meaning of the entire event before the plotters had defined anything at all.

The army defection on the night of the 20th, the storming of the White House that did not happen because the Alpha Group again refused the order, and the collapse of the plotters' nerve on the morning of the 21st are all real, and all flowed from the same source. The men in the tanks around the White House were watching foreign satellite feeds on the colour televisions in the apartments whose windows they were parked under. The Alpha officers were watching the same feeds in their barracks. The plotters themselves, by the morning of the 21st, had taken to watching CNN in the Kremlin because their own broadcasts could no longer be believed even by themselves. The asymmetry of conviction in those sixty hours — between a regime broadcasting on every domestic channel and being disbelieved, and a counter-regime broadcasting nothing domestically and being believed — was the asymmetry that ended the Soviet Union.

It is worth saying what this episode did and did not prove. It did not prove that television had become more powerful than tanks, which is the cliché that emerged from it. The tanks were never used. It proved something narrower and more interesting: that a regime claiming legitimacy in front of cameras cannot afford to look frightened on them, because the medium will transmit fear faster than it transmits any text. Yanayev's shaking hands at 17:00 on 19 August were the most authoritative piece of information any Soviet citizen received that day. The coup did not fail because the plotters were drunk. The coup failed because they were drunk on television, and the man on the tank was sober.

The republics that declared independence in the following ten days — Estonia, Latvia, Ukraine, Belarus, Moldova, Azerbaijan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Armenia, Turkmenistan, Kazakhstan, in that order — were not responding to a successful resistance. They were responding to a transmitted humiliation. The Union dissolved because everyone had watched it lose its nerve in real time, and there is no constitutional mechanism for governing a country whose officials have been seen on the evening news being too afraid to govern it. Gorbachev returned to Moscow on the morning of 22 August to find that he was still President of the Soviet Union but no longer the head of anything: the Russian government had taken his archives, his security detail, his communications, and within four days his party. He resigned on 25 December. The flag came down at 19:32. By then the actual decision had been four months old. It had been taken not in any meeting room but on a tank, in four minutes, on 19 August at 13:00.
