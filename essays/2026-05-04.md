# The Bluff at Portsmouth: How Sergei Witte Won a Peace Russia Had Already Lost

**4 May 2026**

By the summer of 1905 the Russian Empire had no business being at the negotiating table on equal terms. Its Pacific fleet had been sunk at Port Arthur, its Baltic fleet had sailed halfway around the world to be annihilated at Tsushima in forty minutes, its army had been driven out of Mukden in the largest land battle in human history to that point, and St Petersburg was in open revolution following Bloody Sunday. By any rational reading of the military map, Japan had won — comprehensively, in less than eighteen months, against a great power that outnumbered her three to one in population and many times that in industrial base. When Theodore Roosevelt convened the peace conference at the Portsmouth Navy Yard in New Hampshire in August 1905, the assumption in every chancellery in Europe was that Russia would be forced to pay an indemnity, cede Sakhalin, evacuate Manchuria, and acknowledge a Japanese sphere of influence stretching from Korea to the Liaodong Peninsula.

None of that happened. Russia paid no indemnity. She kept the northern half of Sakhalin. She acknowledged Japanese predominance in Korea — which she had already lost on the battlefield — and surrendered the lease on Port Arthur, which she no longer occupied. She walked out of Portsmouth having conceded almost nothing she had not already lost in fact, and Japan walked out with riots in Tokyo, because the Japanese public had been promised a Carthaginian peace and got something closer to a draw. The Hibiya riots in September 1905 killed seventeen people and burned down two-thirds of the city's police boxes. The Japanese cabinet fell. The man responsible for this extraordinary inversion of the military verdict was Sergei Witte, the Tsar's former finance minister, sent to Portsmouth precisely because nobody else wanted the job. **What Witte did at Portsmouth was the most successful diplomatic bluff of the twentieth century, and the mechanics of how he pulled it off are worth understanding because they tell you something durable about how peace settlements actually work.**

The Japanese position at the conference looked unanswerable on paper. Komura Jutaro, the Japanese foreign minister, arrived with twelve demands: recognition of Japanese paramountcy in Korea, evacuation of Manchuria, transfer of the Port Arthur lease, transfer of the South Manchurian Railway, cession of Sakhalin in full, an indemnity of 1.2 billion yen, surrender of Russian warships interned in neutral ports, limitation of Russian naval forces in the Pacific, and fishing rights in Russian waters. Witte was instructed by Tsar Nicholas II to concede nothing on indemnity and nothing on territory — instructions that, taken literally, meant Witte was being sent to fail. The Tsar's view was that any settlement requiring payment would be a humiliation Russia could not survive domestically, and that he would rather continue the war than sign one. This was, given the actual military situation, delusional. It was also Witte's instrument.

Witte's bluff rested on three things, only one of which was true. The first was that Russia had reserves of manpower and territory that could absorb any further Japanese advance — *true*, in the sense that Japan could never have marched on Moscow, but *misleading*, in that Russia could not have continued the war either, given the revolution at home. The second was that Russia would walk away from the table and resume the war if pressed too hard — *false*, because Witte knew, as Komura did not, that the Tsar's military advisers had told him a renewed offensive in Manchuria would require eighteen months of mobilisation that the regime did not have. The third was that the Japanese were running out of money — *true*, and *decisive*, because Witte had spent the previous decade as finance minister and understood Japanese public finance better than the Japanese delegation realised.

This last point is the one that matters. Japan had financed the war through four enormous loans floated in London and New York, arranged largely by Jacob Schiff of Kuhn, Loeb — about £82 million in total, roughly half the cost of the war. By August 1905 the Japanese government's debt-to-GDP ratio had passed seventy percent, the loans were being rolled at increasingly punishing rates, and a fifth tranche under negotiation was contingent on the war ending. **Witte knew the loans were running out. Komura knew the loans were running out. The difference was that Witte knew Komura knew, and Komura did not know that Witte knew.** Witte had been briefed by Russian agents in the City of London on the exact terms of the pending Japanese tranche, and he had been told — correctly — that Schiff had privately informed Tokyo that a sixth loan would not be available on any terms. This was the asymmetry that decided Portsmouth.

Witte played it with theatrical confidence. He rejected the indemnity outright on the second day of negotiations and never moved from that position. He offered the southern half of Sakhalin — south of the fiftieth parallel — but refused the northern half, calculating, correctly, that Japan would not restart a war over latitude. He gave Roosevelt, who was mediating, the impression that Russia was prepared to fight indefinitely, while privately encouraging the American press to portray Japanese demands as greedy. He was, by all accounts of the participants, magnificent at this — large, blunt, aristocratic in a way that played well with American newspaper readers, deliberately courting reporters in a manner that no Japanese diplomat of the period could have matched. He also, crucially, gave the Japanese delegation no exit. Komura had been instructed in Tokyo that he could not return without an indemnity. Witte made sure he had no path to one. By the final week of the conference, Komura was facing a choice between accepting Witte's terms and presiding over the collapse of Japanese state finances. He accepted.

The settlement was signed on 5 September 1905. Roosevelt got the Nobel Peace Prize. Komura returned to Tokyo to face mobs throwing stones at his train. Witte returned to St Petersburg to be made a count and then, within months, dismissed by a Tsar who never forgave him for being right. *The settlement held for thirty-six years*, until the Japanese decided in 1941 that they no longer needed to honour the part about Russian territory. **What Portsmouth proves is that the loser of a war is not necessarily the country whose army has lost. It is the country whose nerve breaks first at the table — and nerve, at the table, is mostly about knowing what the other side cannot afford.** Witte could not have won at Portsmouth without the Japanese debt position, but Komura, with the same information, could not have lost at Portsmouth without the Russian one. The asymmetry was not in the facts. It was in who had read the books.

The lesson is unfashionable in an age that likes to talk about peace settlements in moral terms — just wars, war crimes, accountability, all of which are real categories but none of which were operative at Portsmouth. The lesson is that diplomatic outcomes after wars are decided by the side that understands the loser's domestic constraints better than the loser does. *Witte won a peace Russia had already lost because he knew, more clearly than the Japanese knew, that Japan could not afford to keep winning.* Most of the great peace settlements of the modern era — Brest-Litovsk, Versailles, Panmunjom, Dayton — turn out, on inspection, to follow the same logic. The military verdict gets you to the table. What happens at the table is decided by something else entirely.
