# The Imperial Conference of 6 September 1941 That Made Pearl Harbor a Bookkeeping Problem

On the morning of 6 September 1941, in a long room on the second floor of the Imperial Palace in Tokyo, eleven men sat down on either side of a brocaded table in front of the Showa Emperor and approved a document, four paragraphs long, called the *Teikoku Kokusaku Suikō Yōryō* — "Essentials for Carrying Out the Empire's Policies." The document said that Japan would complete preparations for war against the United States, Britain and the Netherlands by the end of October; that diplomatic negotiations with Washington would continue in parallel; and that if no acceptable settlement had been reached by approximately 10 October, Japan would immediately decide on war. The Emperor said almost nothing. He read aloud a tanka his grandfather, the Meiji Emperor, had written in 1904 about all the seas being brothers — a hint, transmitted through a wall of court protocol, that he disliked what was being decided in his name. Then he gave his sanction. The meeting lasted under two hours. Pearl Harbor was three months and a day away.

**The Liaison Conference system did not produce a decision for war at Pearl Harbor. It produced a timetable, and the timetable produced the war.** This distinction is not pedantic. It is the key to why a country whose senior naval planners knew, in writing, that it could not defeat the United States nonetheless attacked the United States. Japan in 1941 was not a state with a war policy. It was a state with a deadline policy, and the deadline policy was generated by an institutional procedure that, once activated, could not be reversed by any actor inside the system, including the Emperor.

The Liaison Conference (*Renraku Kaigi*) was a wartime improvisation that had become a permanent feature of policymaking after 1937. It was the only forum in which the army, the navy, the cabinet and the throne could deliberate together. Its decisions, once ratified by an Imperial Conference in the Emperor's presence, acquired a quasi-sacral status: they could be revised only by another Imperial Conference, which required unanimous prior agreement by the Liaison Conference, which in turn required the consent of the army general staff, the navy general staff, the war minister and the navy minister. Any one of those four could veto revision. None of them, by the autumn of 1941, was willing to.

The document of 6 September was drafted by middle-ranking officers in the Army General Staff's War Guidance Section and the Navy General Staff's First Bureau, working at the *kachō* (section chief) level over the course of the last week of August. The two services had been quarrelling for two years about whether Japan's next war should be against the Soviet Union (the army's preferred option) or against the southern resource area held by Britain, the Netherlands and the United States (the navy's preferred option). The American oil embargo of 1 August 1941 — which the Roosevelt administration had not quite intended to be a total embargo but which the Treasury Department under Dean Acheson had quietly converted into one by freezing the licences — collapsed this dispute. Without American oil, the Japanese navy had between eighteen months and two years of fuel reserves. Without fuel, the navy was a collection of expensive scrap metal. Without the navy, the army's bargaining position inside the Japanese state collapsed. The army therefore had to agree to a southern war it had spent two years opposing, on a timeline set by the navy's tankage rather than by any analysis of the political situation.

This is the part that gets lost in retellings that treat the war as a decision. It was not a decision. It was an inventory problem. The Navy General Staff's logistics section had calculated, in late August, that if war began in early December 1941 the navy could fight for approximately twenty-four months. If war began in early March 1942, the navy could fight for approximately sixteen months. Every month of delay subtracted one month from operational endurance, because peacetime consumption was running at near-wartime rates. The 6 September document encoded this calculation into a procedural deadline. Diplomacy had until approximately 10 October to produce a settlement. After that, the calendar took over.

Konoe Fumimaro, the prime minister, understood what had happened and tried, between 6 September and 16 October, to reverse it. He attempted to arrange a face-to-face summit with Roosevelt in Honolulu or Juneau, on the theory that direct conversation between principals could cut through the cable traffic in which the Hull–Nomura negotiations had become trapped. Roosevelt was initially interested. Hull, who did not trust the Japanese position on China and who suspected (correctly) that any agreement Konoe could deliver in Tokyo would be sabotaged by the army before it could be implemented, talked him out of it. By the second week of October, Konoe knew that no diplomatic break was coming. He went to see Tojo Hideki, the war minister, and asked the army to accept a withdrawal from China as the price of peace with America. Tojo refused. Konoe resigned on 16 October.

The Emperor's senior advisers — Kido Koichi the Lord Keeper of the Privy Seal, the *jushin* council of former prime ministers — then made the decision that has puzzled historians ever since. They recommended Tojo, the architect of the army's intransigence, as Konoe's replacement. The reasoning was institutional rather than ideological. Only Tojo could control the army; only an army-controlled cabinet could revise the 6 September decision; and Tojo, having been personally instructed by the Emperor at his investiture to wipe the slate clean (*hakushi kangen* — "return to blank paper"), would, it was hoped, do exactly that.

He did not. The Liaison Conference under Tojo reviewed the 6 September decision between 23 October and 1 November in a series of sessions that lasted, in total, more than fifty hours. The reviewers reached the same conclusion the drafters had reached. The navy still needed to fight before its oil ran out; the army still would not leave China; the United States still would not lift the embargo without a withdrawal from China. The procedural deadline, when reopened, did not move. It hardened. On 5 November, a second Imperial Conference confirmed that war preparations would be completed by the end of the month and that, absent an American capitulation on the China question by 30 November, hostilities would begin in early December. Yamamoto's carrier strike force had already begun assembling at Hitokappu Bay in the Kuriles. On 26 November, Hull delivered the so-called Hull Note, which the Japanese government read as an ultimatum demanding total withdrawal from China and Indochina as a precondition for negotiation. Hull, in private, told Stimson he had washed his hands of the matter; it was now in the hands of the army and the navy.

**The deepest lesson of 6 September 1941 is that bureaucracies can manufacture inevitabilities.** No individual Japanese leader in the autumn of 1941 wanted war with the United States. The Emperor did not want it; Konoe did not want it; Tojo, by his own postwar testimony and by the corroborating testimony of his cabinet secretary Hoshino Naoki, did not want it. The Navy Minister Oikawa did not want it. The Foreign Minister Togo did not want it. They all believed, with varying degrees of clarity, that Japan would probably lose. And they all, in the end, ratified the war because the procedural machinery they had inherited produced a deadline none of them could individually move, and the deadline produced operational orders, and the operational orders produced 7 December.

The Showa Emperor's tanka, recited in the long room above the moat on a September morning, was the only moment in the entire sequence when anyone with formal authority said, in public, that the policy might be wrong. Nobody at the table answered him. The minutes record a silence. Then the meeting resumed.
