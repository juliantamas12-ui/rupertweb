# The Weekend in June 1970 When the Federal Reserve Invented Too Big to Fail and Refused to Admit It

On the morning of Sunday, 21 June 1970, the Penn Central Transportation Company filed for bankruptcy in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania. It was, at $6.3 billion in assets, the largest corporate failure in American history up to that point, larger than every previous bankruptcy combined. Penn Central was the sixth-biggest non-financial corporation in the country. It ran the passenger services into New York, Boston, Washington, and Chicago. It employed ninety-six thousand people. It owned the Pan Am Building, the Waldorf Astoria, and most of the developable land in midtown Manhattan. It had been created two years earlier by the merger of the Pennsylvania and the New York Central, the two most storied railroads in the United States, in a deal the Interstate Commerce Commission had approved on the explicit theory that the combined entity was too important to be allowed to fail. On 21 June 1970 it failed.

**What the Federal Reserve did over the seventy-two hours preceding that filing was unprecedented in American central banking, was never publicly described as what it was, and quietly became the template for every subsequent crisis intervention through Continental Illinois in 1984, LTCM in 1998, Bear Stearns in 2008, and Silicon Valley Bank in 2023. The Federal Reserve, between Thursday 18 June and Saturday 20 June 1970, decided that the commercial paper market — not Penn Central — was too big to fail, and bailed it out without using the word, without congressional authorisation, and without telling the public what it had done until the relevant minutes were declassified more than a decade later.**

The mechanism is worth understanding because the public memory of 1970 has it almost exactly wrong. Penn Central had been insolvent for at least a year. Its commercial paper — short-term unsecured IOUs sold mainly to corporate treasurers and money market funds — totalled $200 million, an amount that had been rolled over week by week through a single dealer, Goldman Sachs, on the assumption that Penn Central was a railroad and railroads do not default. By early June the assumption had collapsed. Goldman was unable to place new paper. The Nixon administration, through Treasury Secretary David Kennedy and Federal Reserve Chairman Arthur Burns, spent the first three weeks of June trying to assemble a $200 million federally guaranteed loan under the Defense Production Act, on the theory that Penn Central's passenger services were essential to national security. The House Banking Committee, on the morning of Thursday 18 June, refused to clear the guarantee. Penn Central had until Monday morning.

What concentrated minds in Washington was not the railroad. It was the realisation, arrived at over the course of Thursday afternoon, that the commercial paper market was about to discover that commercial paper could default. The outstanding stock of commercial paper in the United States in June 1970 was $40 billion. Most of it had been issued by industrial corporations to bypass the regulated banking system, which under Regulation Q could not pay competitive rates on short-term deposits. The market had grown roughly fivefold in the previous five years. Nobody in the Federal Reserve had ever seen a commercial paper default. The standard reference handbook on the instrument, published in 1968 by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, contained the sentence: *defaults are exceedingly rare, and total losses on prime commercial paper since 1920 have been negligible*. The treasurers of every Fortune 500 company in the country had been told by their banks that commercial paper was, in effect, money. They had treated it accordingly. They were, on Thursday 18 June, all looking at their Penn Central paper and wondering whether the rest of their paper was money or not.

Burns and Kennedy understood the implication. If Penn Central defaulted on Monday and the commercial paper market panicked on Tuesday, every industrial corporation in the United States would discover simultaneously that its short-term funding had evaporated. Chrysler, which had $200 million in paper outstanding, was the immediate worry. So was Sears, with $400 million. So were perhaps two hundred other companies whose commercial paper was held by perhaps two thousand money market funds and corporate treasuries that had no obligation to roll it over. The credit channel of American industry was about to seize. The Federal Reserve's only tool against this was the discount window: the standing facility by which member banks could borrow from their regional Federal Reserve Bank against eligible collateral. In normal times the discount window was a punishment instrument, used reluctantly, at a penalty rate, by banks that had mismanaged their reserves. **What Burns did over the weekend of 19–20 June 1970 was to telephone the chief executive of every major money centre bank in New York and Chicago and tell them, in language that was never put in writing, that the discount window was now open without limit, at the prevailing rate, against any commercial paper they cared to bring in. They were to lend, freely, to any industrial corporation whose paper they held, on terms that would allow the corporation to retire the paper. The Federal Reserve would stand behind the banks. The banks would stand behind the corporations. Nobody would stand behind Penn Central, which would be allowed to fail on Monday morning as a sacrifice to the principle that the central bank had not just bailed out the commercial paper market.**

The operation worked exactly as designed. Penn Central filed on Sunday. The commercial paper market shrank by $3 billion the following week as the weakest issuers were quietly retired through the banking system. There was no panic. The money market funds held. Chrysler survived; Sears never had a problem. By the end of July the commercial paper market was functioning again, slightly smaller and slightly more discriminating. The Federal Reserve's discount window lending peaked at $1.7 billion in the week of 24 June — a sum that, at the time, represented the largest weekly discount window operation since the Second World War — and returned to normal by mid-August. The minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee for the meeting of 23 June 1970, which were not published in detail until 1996, record Chairman Burns describing the previous weekend's operations in language so opaque that the transcript reads, in places, as if it were referring to a different country.

The doctrine the Federal Reserve invented that weekend has three components, all of which remain in force. The first is the distinction between rescuing an institution and rescuing a market: Penn Central was allowed to fail because the operation that mattered was the rescue of the commercial paper market, not of the borrower. The second is the use of the discount window as a market-making instrument of last resort, lending to solvent intermediaries against the obligations of distressed issuers, on terms intended to extinguish the distressed issue without monetising it. The third, and most consequential, is the doctrine of strategic silence: that a central bank intervention of this kind works only if it is not publicly described as a bailout, because the moment market participants understand that the central bank will always intervene to prevent contagion, they price out the risk premia that make the system stable in the first place. **Moral hazard, in the framework Burns improvised over a weekend in June 1970, is managed not by refusing to intervene but by refusing to admit that one has intervened.**

Every major Federal Reserve operation since has followed the template. When Continental Illinois failed in 1984, the FDIC explicitly guaranteed all depositors and creditors, but Chairman Paul Volcker insisted the term *bailout* never appear in any official statement. When LTCM was unwound in 1998, William McDonough of the New York Fed organised the rescue in a private dining room and described the operation, in his subsequent congressional testimony, as a *facilitation*. When Bear Stearns was sold to JPMorgan in March 2008, Chairman Bernanke described the $29 billion of Federal Reserve credit extended against Bear's collateral as a *liquidity backstop*. When Silicon Valley Bank failed in March 2023, the Treasury, the FDIC, and the Federal Reserve issued a joint statement on Sunday evening declaring that all depositors would be made whole through a *systemic risk exception*, while the Bank Term Funding Programme launched the next morning offered the entire banking system par-value loans against underwater Treasuries — a discount window operation on the scale Burns would have recognised immediately.

The interesting question is whether the doctrine can survive being understood. Burns operated on the assumption that markets price moral hazard out of the system the moment they perceive a guarantee. The evidence of the last fifty-five years suggests, on the contrary, that markets price the guarantee in, demand it, lobby for its extension, and treat its absence as a policy failure. The commercial paper market in 1970 was $40 billion. The commercial paper market in 2023 was $1.3 trillion, of which perhaps three-quarters depends, implicitly, on the doctrine that the Federal Reserve invented over a weekend fifty-three years before to deal with a railroad nobody under sixty now remembers. **The system Burns built was meant to be invisible because it would not work if it were visible. The system that exists today is visible, expected, and demanded, and the question of whether it still works is the question of what the Federal Reserve does the next time a Penn Central appears and the discount window is no longer large enough to absorb the contagion.**

It is large enough now. The interesting question is for how much longer.
