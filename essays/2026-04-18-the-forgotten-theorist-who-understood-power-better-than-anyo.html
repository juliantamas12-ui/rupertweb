The Forgotten Theorist Who Understood Power Better Than Anyone

The Forgotten Theorist Who Understood Power Better Than Anyone

In 1377, a North African scholar named Ibn Khaldun sat in a remote fortress in what is now Algeria and wrote what he called a mere introduction to a larger history. That introduction — the Muqaddimah — turned out to be one of the most extraordinary works of social and political theory ever produced, anticipating sociology, economics, historiography, and political science by centuries. It is also almost entirely absent from Western political education, which is a genuine intellectual loss.

Ibn Khaldun's central concept is asabiyyah — usually translated as "group feeling" or "social cohesion." It is the binding force that allows groups to act collectively, to sacrifice for one another, to project power outward. Without it, nothing gets built. With it, empires rise. The tragedy, in Ibn Khaldun's telling, is that asabiyyah is inherently self-destructive: the very success it produces corrodes it.

The cycle goes like this. A group on the periphery — desert nomads, mountain clans, hungry outsiders — possesses strong asabiyyah born of necessity. They cooperate because they must. They fight because survival demands it. They are frugal, tough, and morally coherent because luxury hasn't reached them yet. They overthrow the decadent ruling group, whose asabiyyah has collapsed under generations of wealth and security. The outsiders become the new rulers. And then the decay begins.

Three or four generations, Ibn Khaldun estimates. The first generation made the conquest. The second remembers the stories. The third has only heard them. The fourth has grown up in palaces. By that point, asabiyyah has collapsed: the elite is fractured by internal competition, the state apparatus bloated and venal, military power outsourced to mercenaries, the tax base eroded by exemptions and corruption. A new hungry group on the periphery starts looking at the capital.

This is not metaphor. Ibn Khaldun documented it empirically across Berber dynasties, Arab caliphates, and the remnants of Rome — comparing the Almoravids, Almohads, and Marinids in North Africa and tracing how each followed the same arc with near-mechanical regularity. He was doing comparative historical sociology six centuries before Weber.

What makes this framework remarkable — and criminally underused — is how well it maps onto patterns that modern political science struggles to explain with its preference for institutional and procedural accounts.

Take the Roman Republic's collapse. The standard story is institutional: Caesar crossed the Rubicon, the Senate failed to adapt, Octavian outmaneuvered his rivals. But what enabled all of this was prior social dissolution. The old Roman virtues — frugality, civic duty, the willingness to die for the res publica — had been dissolving since at least the Gracchi a century earlier. By Caesar's time, Roman citizenship meant entitlement to grain doles, not obligation to serve. Legions owed loyalty to their generals, who paid them, not to the republic, which didn't. Asabiyyah had collapsed; the institutional crisis was symptom, not cause.

The same structural sequence appears in the late Ottoman Empire, the Romanov dynasty, the Qing, and Weimar Germany. In each case: long success breeds stratification and detachment; elites become more extractive and less cohesive; peripheral or excluded groups develop their own solidarity; the center cannot hold because it no longer wants to badly enough. The specific institutions, ideologies, and personalities differ. The underlying dynamic is the same.

Ibn Khaldun's framework also has something sharp to say about contemporary politics that more fashionable theories don't. The rise of populist movements across Europe and America is typically explained as economic anxiety, cultural backlash, or elite failure — all real, but all missing the structural dynamic underneath. What you're actually watching in many cases is a group on the periphery — economically, geographically, culturally — that still possesses something like asabiyyah, colliding with an elite that has largely lost it. The professional class in major cities is cosmopolitan, mobile, and fractured across competing identity vectors. The rural and exurban working class is not. It has grievances, but more importantly, it has cohesion. Ibn Khaldun would not be surprised by how that collision has been going.

This is not an endorsement of populism. Ibn Khaldun's cycle doesn't produce better regimes — it produces replacement regimes that will themselves eventually decay. His theory is descriptive and cyclical, not progressive. There is no telos, no arc bending toward justice. Just the wheel turning.

That may be the most intellectually honest part of his framework. Most Western political theory — liberal, Marxist, even conservative — has some version of history going somewhere: toward freedom, toward class resolution, toward tradition restored. Ibn Khaldun had no such comfort to offer. He had seen too many dynasties rise and fall across too many centuries to believe that any of them were special or final. The best you could do was understand the cycle and perhaps slow the decay through deliberate cultivation of asabiyyah — through shared purpose, common sacrifice, institutions that demanded something from people rather than simply delivering things to them.

Whether that is possible in modern mass societies is a genuinely open question. Liberal democracies have no agreed account of what would constitute sufficient civic solidarity, and their procedural architecture is specifically designed to avoid imposing one. That may be the correct tradeoff — coerced solidarity has its own disaster modes. But it does mean that the Khaldunian decay mechanism runs largely without check, and that the people defending liberal institutions tend to be precisely the group whose asabiyyah has already thinned most.

The Muqaddimah has never been out of print. It was translated into English by Franz Rosenthal in 1958 and remains fully accessible. The fact that it sits outside the Western canon — that undergraduates study Locke, Rousseau, and Marx but almost never Ibn Khaldun — is not because it is less rigorous or less relevant. It is because the canon is geographically parochial in ways that cost us real analytical power.

Read it. Not because it offers answers, but because it asks the right questions and asks them with a clarity that most modern political theory, dressed up in the language of datasets and regression coefficients, has largely stopped being willing to match.
