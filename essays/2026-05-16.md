# The Four Sick Old Men Who Killed the Soviet Union: The Politburo Session of 12 December 1979

On the afternoon of 12 December 1979, four men met in a small room next to Leonid Brezhnev's office on the third floor of the Central Committee building on Staraya Square in Moscow. Brezhnev was seventy-two and barely lucid, sustained on sedatives and amphetamines by a personal physician who had been instructed never to leave his side. Yuri Andropov, the KGB chairman, was sixty-five and already showing the kidney failure that would kill him four years later. Dmitri Ustinov, the Defence Minister, was seventy-one. Andrei Gromyko, the Foreign Minister, was seventy. Between them they decided to send forty thousand Soviet soldiers across the Amu Darya into Afghanistan. They did not inform the full Politburo, which met formally only after the decision had been taken and the orders cut. They did not inform the Soviet General Staff's operations directorate, whose chief, Marshal Ogarkov, had spent the previous month begging them not to do it. They did not inform Alexei Kosygin, the Prime Minister, who learned about the decision from a brief telephone call after he had already gone to bed. The piece of paper they signed — a single page, handwritten by Konstantin Chernenko because no typist could be brought into the room — was filed under the deliberately vague title *Concerning the Situation in A.* The "A" was for Afghanistan. Even inside the Central Committee they were not willing to write the country's name.

The conventional reading is that this was an act of Brezhnev-era expansionism, the high tide of Soviet ambition, the moment the USSR overreached and met its imperial graveyard. **This reading inverts cause and effect.** The Soviet Union did not invade Afghanistan because it was strong and confident. It invaded because four old men, frightened of what was happening on the southern frontier of their state, made a decision that the institutions designed to restrain them no longer functioned well enough to prevent. The Politburo session of 12 December was not the apex of Soviet power. It was the moment Soviet power discovered that it could no longer think.

The trigger was Iran. In February 1979 the Shah had fallen, and within months the new Islamic Republic had begun broadcasting in Tajik, Uzbek, and Turkmen — the languages of the five Soviet Central Asian republics that contained, between them, roughly fifty million Muslims. Andropov, whose entire career had been built on the suppression of nationalism and religion inside the Soviet Union, read the Tehran broadcasts with the attention of a man who had seen Hungary in 1956 and understood what an unsuppressed Islamic revival on a Soviet border could do. The KGB's First Chief Directorate had begun, by summer 1979, to file reports warning that the Afghan Communist regime of Nur Muhammad Taraki was collapsing, that its replacement Hafizullah Amin was negotiating with the Americans behind Moscow's back, and that the Islamist insurgency spreading from Afghan villages would, within months, reach the Tajik frontier. Almost none of this was true. Amin was not negotiating with the Americans — he was, in fact, an old graduate of Columbia who detested them. The Islamist insurgency was real but its strategic direction in 1979 was eastward toward Pakistan, not northward toward the USSR. The reports were Andropov's projections, dressed in intelligence assessments. He was telling himself what he was afraid of, and the bureaucracy he had built was no longer capable of telling him he was wrong.

The institutions that should have stopped him had been dismantled by precisely the men now making the decision. Brezhnev had spent the 1970s replacing every plausible internal critic in the Politburo with personal loyalists. Ustinov had been Brezhnev's drinking companion since the war and owed his Defence Ministry to Brezhnev's personal favour. Gromyko, the longest-serving foreign minister in modern European history, had survived by never offering an opinion that contradicted the General Secretary's first instinct. The Politburo's collective leadership — the system that, after Khrushchev's fall in 1964, had been carefully designed to prevent any single member from taking the country to war on his own — had decayed into a kitchen cabinet of four men who could no longer hear bad news. Marshal Ogarkov was summoned to the Politburo on 10 December, three days before the invasion order. He told Ustinov, in front of Brezhnev, that the operation could not be limited, that forty thousand men would not be enough, that they would still be there in five years and would have to fight a guerrilla war they could not win. Ustinov told him to be quiet and remember that his job was to carry out political decisions, not to make them. Ogarkov walked out. He was correct on every count, and he had no further influence on the decision.

What is striking, reading the surviving documents — most of them released in the 1990s through the Volkogonov collection and the Cold War International History Project — is how little discussion there was. The single typed page that records the Politburo's formal ratification on 27 December, after the invasion was already underway, contains four sentences. There is no analysis of the strategic situation. There is no estimate of likely costs. There is no contingency plan for failure. There is no specified objective beyond the replacement of Amin, who was duly murdered by KGB special forces in Kabul on the evening of 27 December and replaced by Babrak Karmal, a man who had spent the previous year in a Czechoslovak safe house drinking heavily and who would prove incapable of governing a village, much less a country at war. The men who made the decision had stopped expecting their decisions to be scrutinised. They had been the supreme authority for so long that they had forgotten what it felt like to be challenged, and the bureaucracy beneath them had learned to provide them with whatever conclusions they had already reached.

The consequences are well known. Ten years, fifteen thousand Soviet dead, perhaps a million Afghan dead, a humiliation that broke the Soviet army's faith in its political masters, an economic burden the USSR could not sustain at the moment when oil prices were collapsing and Reagan was forcing it to compete in arms it could no longer afford. By the time Gorbachev pulled the last Soviet soldier across the Friendship Bridge at Termez in February 1989, the regime that had ordered the invasion was dead — Brezhnev in 1982, Andropov in 1984, Ustinov in 1984, Chernenko in 1985 — and the state itself had less than three years to live. The connection between Afghanistan and the collapse of the USSR was not mechanical. It was structural. The same decay that had produced the invasion produced everything else: the inability to reform an economy whose distortions were obvious, the inability to negotiate with Solidarity in Poland, the inability to permit the East German leadership to fall when it should have fallen in 1988 rather than 1989. **A system that could not stop four old men from invading Afghanistan in December 1979 could not stop itself from doing anything else either.**

The lesson is not that Andropov was wrong about the Islamic revival, though he was — the Soviet Central Asian republics turned out to be the calmest part of the country during the collapse, and the Tajik civil war when it came in 1992 was driven by post-Soviet clan politics rather than imported Islamism. The lesson is that authoritarian states do not, in the end, fail because their ideologies are exposed as false or their economies prove unworkable. They fail because their elites grow old together, replace honest subordinates with flatterers, and arrive at a point where the people authorised to make decisions can no longer hear what their own intelligence services and general staffs are telling them. The Politburo of 12 December 1979 was not the strongest state in Eurasia making an aggressive move. It was the weakest possible version of itself, four men in failing health who could not face down the youngest of them. Empires die many ways. The Soviet Union died in that room.
