# The Morning of 20 July 1936 at Estoril When General Sanjurjo's Suitcase Killed Him and Made Franco the Caudillo of Spain by Default

On the morning of Monday, 20 July 1936, at approximately 11:00 local time, a Puss Moth biplane piloted by the Falangist aviator Juan Antonio Ansaldo took off from a small grass strip outside Estoril, Portugal, bound for the Spanish nationalist headquarters at Burgos. Its passenger was General José Sanjurjo y Sacanell, the Marqués del Rif, the man designated by the conspirators of the Unión Militar Española to lead the military uprising that had begun two days earlier against the Spanish Republic. The Puss Moth was overloaded. Sanjurjo had insisted on bringing two heavy leather suitcases containing, by various accounts, his dress uniforms, his decorations, and the ceremonial wardrobe in which he intended to enter Madrid as head of state. Ansaldo had warned him that the field was short, the engine was modest, and the cypress trees at the end of the strip were tall. Sanjurjo had answered, according to Ansaldo's later testimony, that "a general of Spain must enter the capital correctly dressed." The Puss Moth lifted, stalled at the tree line, clipped the cypresses, and burned. Ansaldo crawled out with burns. Sanjurjo did not crawl out at all. By noon the designated leader of the Spanish nationalist rising was a charred corpse in a Portuguese field and the rising itself, three days old, was without a head.

The standard account of the Spanish Civil War treats Franco's emergence as caudillo as the inevitable product of his military reputation, his Moroccan command, and his political cunning. There is some of each in the story. None of it accounts for the specific fact that on the morning of 18 July 1936, when the rising began, Franco was the fourth-ranking figure in the conspiracy at best, was distrusted by the Carlists, was disliked by Mola, and was regarded by Sanjurjo himself as a cautious careerist who had hedged his commitment to the coup until the last possible moment. **Franco became caudillo because the three men who outranked him in the conspiracy — Sanjurjo, Goded, and Fanjul — were dead or in prison within eight weeks of the rising's outbreak, and because the fourth, Mola, was the only one who survived long enough to be killed in another aircraft accident at exactly the moment Franco needed him gone. The Spanish nationalist state that emerged from the war was the product of an actuarial accident.**

To see this one has to understand what the conspiracy of June and July 1936 actually was. It was not a Francoist movement. It was a coalition of four overlapping factions: the monarchist officers around Sanjurjo, in exile in Portugal since his failed 1932 coup; the conspirators in metropolitan Spain organised by General Emilio Mola from his garrison in Pamplona; the Carlist requetés in Navarre, with their own command structure under Fal Conde; and the Falangists, whose leader José Antonio Primo de Rivera was in republican custody at Alicante. The agreed leader was Sanjurjo. Mola was the operational director — *El Director*, as the conspirators called him — but Mola accepted Sanjurjo's primacy and Sanjurjo accepted Mola's planning. Franco's role in the conspiracy until 12 July was deliberately ambiguous. He had been transferred by the republican government to the Canaries in February 1936 precisely to put him out of the way. He had refused to commit to the rising in writing until two weeks before it broke out. The famous Dragon Rapide that flew him from Las Palmas to Tetuán on 18 July was chartered in London by monarchist sympathisers because the conspirators were not certain, even in early July, that Franco would board it.

What changed this within three weeks was a sequence of deaths and arrests so precise that one is tempted to call it providential, except that providence in Spain in 1936 was working for nobody in particular. **General Manuel Goded, who had taken Mallorca for the nationalists on 19 July and then flown to Barcelona to lead the rising in Catalonia, was captured by republican forces on the afternoon of 19 July when the Barcelona garrison failed to rise.** He was shot on 12 August. General Joaquín Fanjul, who had been designated to lead the rising in Madrid, holed up in the Montaña barracks with about two thousand troops and was overrun by republican militias on 20 July. He was shot on 18 August. **Sanjurjo died on the morning of 20 July at Estoril.** Within seventy-two hours of the rising's outbreak, the conspiracy had lost its designated leader, its Madrid commander, and its Catalan commander. Only Mola in the north and Franco in Morocco remained.

The substance of the next eight weeks was the question of who would inherit. Mola was the senior figure by experience and by his role as operational director. He commanded the northern army, controlled Navarre and Old Castile, and had the support of the Carlists. Franco commanded the Army of Africa — the only fully professional combat force on the nationalist side — but he was stuck in Morocco until he could ferry his troops across the Strait of Gibraltar, which required transport aircraft he did not possess. The decisive intervention came not from Spanish politics but from Berlin and Rome. On 25 July, Franco's emissaries reached Adolf Hitler at Bayreuth during the Wagner festival and, after a long evening of *Siegfried*, Hitler agreed to provide twenty Junkers Ju 52 transports. Within days Italian Savoia-Marchetti SM.81s were flying the same route. By mid-August the Army of Africa was on the Spanish mainland, advancing north through Extremadura, and Franco — by virtue of his command of the only serious fighting force the nationalists possessed and the only general with a direct line to the two fascist powers — had become the indispensable military figure of the rising.

This is where the actuarial accident closed. On 21 September 1936, at a meeting at an airfield near Salamanca, the nationalist generals voted to make Franco *Generalísimo*. The vote was not unanimous and the decision was framed as provisional — Franco was to be commander-in-chief for the duration of military operations, with the question of permanent political authority deferred. On 28 September the same group, meeting again at Salamanca, dropped the qualifications. Franco was now *Jefe del Estado*, head of state, with no time limit and no constitutional check. Mola accepted the decision with a public courtesy that masked, according to several of his close associates, a private fury. He had been the conspiracy's director. He had organised the rising. He commanded the northern army through the autumn of 1936 and the spring of 1937, in the campaigns that took Irún, San Sebastián, and finally Bilbao on 19 June 1937. He was, in the standard view of the conspirators, the man who would in due course balance Franco politically and prevent the consolidation of personal dictatorship.

**On the morning of 3 June 1937, less than two weeks before the fall of Bilbao, Mola boarded a Spanish Airco DH.9 at Pamplona for a flight to Valladolid. The aircraft crashed into a hillside near Alcocero in Castile. Mola was killed instantly.** The aircraft was examined; the cause was never definitively established; the accounts diverge between accident and sabotage, with no archival evidence so far produced that resolves the question. What is not in doubt is the result. Within eleven months of the rising's outbreak, all four of Franco's senior rivals in the conspiracy were dead or shot. Goded on 12 August 1936. Fanjul on 18 August 1936. Sanjurjo on 20 July 1936. Mola on 3 June 1937. **The man who entered the rising as its fourth-ranked figure left it, before the war was even half over, as its sole surviving founder.**

The downstream consequence of this concentration was the specific shape of the Spanish state that emerged from victory in 1939. Sanjurjo had been a monarchist who intended to restore Alfonso XIII or his son Don Juan within months of taking Madrid. Mola was a republican-by-temperament authoritarian who envisaged a directorate of generals and an eventual return to constitutional government. Goded and Fanjul, had they survived, would have shifted the conspiracy's centre of gravity towards the army's traditional pluralism. None of them would have produced the personal regime of Francisco Franco — a regime in which one man, with no clear successor, ruled for thirty-six years and refused to settle the succession until 1969. The Spain of the Civil Guard at Carrero Blanco's funeral, of the executions of September 1975 three months before Franco's own death, of the slow and uncertain transition that Adolfo Suárez had to negotiate against the dead hand of a regime that had no living architect to call it off — that Spain was produced by the cypress trees at Estoril and the hillside at Alcocero. Take either accident out of the record and the war is still won, perhaps, but the peace that follows is somebody else's peace.

Sanjurjo's suitcase was, in the end, an instrument of historical compression. It killed the only nationalist leader whose political instincts and personal authority were sufficient to restore the Spanish monarchy in 1936, and it killed him at the precise moment when the rising required a head and no other candidate possessed one of comparable weight. The further accidents followed in their own time and from their own causes, but the trajectory was set on the morning of 20 July at Estoril, when a vain man insisted on his dress uniforms and an underpowered aircraft confirmed his vanity at the cost of his life. **Spain became Franco's because it could not be Sanjurjo's, and it could not be Sanjurjo's because a Puss Moth could not lift two leather suitcases over a Portuguese cypress.** Historians have a professional resistance to explanations of this kind. They prefer structures, classes, ideologies, longue durée. But the Spanish state of 1939 to 1975 was, in its specific personal form, the product of an aviation accident on a summer morning in 1936, and there is no honest way to write its history without saying so.
