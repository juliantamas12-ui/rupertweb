The Bank That Killed the World Economy: Why Creditanstalt, Not Wall Street, Caused the Great Depression

The single most durable lie in twentieth-century economic history is that the Great Depression began with the Wall Street Crash of October 1929. It did not. The American stock market crash was a severe but recoverable financial event; by April 1930, the Dow had clawed back nearly half its losses and industrial production in the United States, while down, was still above the level of mid-1928. What turned a sharp American recession into a ten-year global depression that destroyed three empires and produced Hitler was not Black Tuesday. It was a run on a single Austrian bank in May 1931, and more specifically it was the French government's deliberate decision to let that bank fail in order to extract a political concession from Vienna. The Great Depression was not caused. It was chosen.

The Österreichische Creditanstalt für Handel und Gewerbe was, by 1931, the largest bank in Central Europe. Founded in 1855 by the Rothschilds, it had absorbed most of its serious competitors during the 1920s — the Bodencreditanstalt in 1929, the Unionbank in 1927 — at the explicit urging of the Austrian government, which preferred a single national champion to a fragile competitive landscape. By the spring of 1931 it held roughly half of all Austrian bank deposits and had a balance sheet larger than that of the Austrian state. It was, in the language of a later era, too big to fail. It was also, by then, insolvent.

The insolvency was not subtle. On 11 May 1931 the bank's directors informed Chancellor Otto Ender that an internal audit had revealed losses of 140 million schillings — roughly equal to the bank's entire paid-up capital. The losses were the product of bad industrial loans inherited from the absorbed Bodencreditanstalt, compounded by the collapse of Central European commodity prices through 1930. The Austrian government, after a 24-hour cabinet meeting, announced a rescue package: a tripartite injection of capital from the state, the Rothschilds, and the National Bank of Austria, totalling 160 million schillings. The announcement was meant to reassure depositors. It did the opposite. By revealing the scale of the hole, it triggered the run.

What happened next is the part that matters, and the part that is usually skipped. Within a week the Austrian National Bank's foreign-exchange reserves were being bled at a rate that would exhaust them by mid-June. Vienna applied to the Bank for International Settlements in Basel for an emergency credit. The BIS, then under the presidency of Gates McGarrah, organised a syndicated loan of 100 million schillings within five days — a remarkable feat for an institution barely a year old. The loan was insufficient, and Vienna applied for a second tranche of 150 million schillings on 5 June. It was at this point that the French government intervened.

The French Foreign Minister, Aristide Briand, and the Finance Minister, Pierre-Étienne Flandin, instructed the Banque de France to block its participation in the second BIS credit unless Austria first abandoned the proposed customs union with Germany announced two months earlier, on 21 March 1931. The Austro-German customs union — a modest agreement to harmonise tariffs between the two states — was, in Paris's view, the first step towards the Anschluss prohibited by the Treaty of Versailles. France had been waiting for leverage. Creditanstalt was the leverage.

The French position was not concealed. In a meeting at the Quai d'Orsay on 8 June, Flandin told the Austrian envoy Johannes Schober explicitly that the credit would be made available within 48 hours of a public Austrian renunciation of the customs union. Schober refused. The French press, briefed nightly by the Quai d'Orsay, ran headlines for the next ten days framing the rescue as a question of German revisionism. By the time the Hague Court ruled the customs union unlawful on 5 September — by which point Austria had already abandoned it under economic duress — the damage had metastasised.

Here is the chain, because the chain is the point. The run on Creditanstalt, prolonged by French obstruction, exhausted Austrian reserves by late June. On 13 July, the Darmstädter und Nationalbank — the second-largest commercial bank in Germany, heavily exposed to Austrian assets — closed its doors after a depositor run that the Reichsbank refused to halt. The Reichsbank refused because it had less than 40 per cent of the legally-required gold cover for the mark, the consequence of a parallel run on German reserves triggered by the Austrian crisis. The German government declared a bank holiday on 14 July and imposed exchange controls on 15 July, severing the Reichsmark from the gold standard in all but name. Britain, holding sterling balances in frozen German accounts, found its own reserves depleted to the point of crisis by mid-September. On 21 September 1931 the pound left gold. By the end of the year, twenty-three currencies had followed it. The international monetary system that had organised global trade since 1870 ceased to exist.

None of this was inevitable. The BIS credits would have stabilised Creditanstalt within a month. The French knew it. They chose, as a matter of conscious foreign-policy calculation, to use the threat of European-wide financial collapse to extract a concession from Vienna on a customs union that was already effectively dead. Briand later called it a "pressing of the lever". It was, in fact, a sawing of the branch on which everyone, including France, was sitting. By April 1932 French industrial production had fallen by a third and the franc itself was under pressure. By 1936 France too would leave gold. The lever had not pressed Vienna. It had broken the world.

The Wall Street Crash made the Great Depression possible. The Creditanstalt collapse made it global. And the French foreign ministry, in pursuit of a treaty point that the Hague was about to grant them anyway, made it inevitable. The deepest irony of the 1930s is that the men who caused the depression that produced Hitler did so in the name of preventing exactly the German revisionism Hitler would embody. The road to 1939 ran not through Versailles. It ran through a memo at the Quai d'Orsay in June 1931, and through the calculation, made by men who considered themselves prudent, that Austria's bank could be allowed to burn for one more week.

Sources

  • Aurel Schubert, The Credit-Anstalt Crisis of 1931 (Cambridge University Press, 1991) — the definitive monograph on the bank's collapse, with quantitative reconstruction of the run and the BIS rescue effort
  • Barry Eichengreen, Golden Fetters: The Gold Standard and the Great Depression, 1919–1939 (Oxford University Press, 1992) — for the transmission mechanism by which the Austrian crisis broke the international monetary system
  • Harold James, The End of Globalization: Lessons from the Great Depression (Harvard University Press, 2001) — on the political economy of the French intervention and the BIS's institutional limits
  • Edward W. Bennett, Germany and the Diplomacy of the Financial Crisis, 1931 (Harvard University Press, 1962) — the foundational diplomatic history, with extensive use of the Auswärtiges Amt archives on the Austro-German customs union
  • Patricia Clavin, Securing the World Economy: The Reinvention of the League of Nations, 1920–1946 (Oxford University Press, 2013) — for the multilateral context and the failure of the League's economic apparatus to act
  • Charles Kindleberger, The World in Depression, 1929–1939 (University of California Press, 1973; rev. 1986) — the classic argument that hegemonic absence, not policy error, produced the Depression; useful as the frame against which the French choice stands out
  • Bank for International Settlements, Second Annual Report (Basel, May 1932) — the BIS's own contemporary account of the rescue effort and its frustration
