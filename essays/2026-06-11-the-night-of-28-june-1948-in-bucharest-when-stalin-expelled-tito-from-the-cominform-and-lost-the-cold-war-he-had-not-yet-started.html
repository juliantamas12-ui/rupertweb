The Night of 28 June 1948 in Bucharest When Stalin Expelled Tito from the Cominform and Lost the Cold War He Had Not Yet Started

On the evening of Monday, 28 June 1948 — the Feast of St Vitus, the Serbian national holiday, the anniversary of Kosovo Polje in 1389 and of the Sarajevo assassination in 1914 — the Cominform meeting in Bucharest issued its resolution expelling the Communist Party of Yugoslavia from the international communist movement. The timing was Stalin's choice. The date was meant to humiliate Tito on the day Serbs reserved for their saints and their martyrs. The resolution accused the Yugoslav leadership of "Trotskyist" deviation, of nationalist arrogance, of a "shameful, purely Turkish, terrorist regime" inside its own party. It called on "healthy elements" within the Yugoslav party to overthrow Tito and his clique. It assumed that those healthy elements existed, that they would respond, and that Tito would be deposed within weeks by his own Central Committee. Stalin reportedly told Khrushchev that he had only to shake his little finger and Tito would fall. Within three months it was clear that Stalin had shaken his finger and nothing had fallen at all.

The conventional reading of the Tito–Stalin split treats it as a personal quarrel between two strong-willed dictators, or as an early symptom of the polycentrism that would later produce the Sino-Soviet rupture. Both readings are correct so far as they go and both miss the operational point. The expulsion of Yugoslavia from the Cominform was the single greatest strategic error of Stalin's career and the moment at which the Soviet bloc, before it had finished consolidating, acquired the structural weakness that would in the end destroy it. It did so for a specific reason: Stalin had constructed an empire whose legitimacy depended on the proposition that there was no alternative model of socialist development outside Moscow's direction, and on 28 June 1948 he produced an alternative model himself and certified it by his own decree.

To see this one has to understand what the Cominform was for. It was founded at Szklarska Poręba in September 1947 as a successor to the dissolved Comintern, but its real function was bureaucratic and disciplinary: to give Moscow a permanent mechanism for the coordination — meaning, the supervision — of the parties of Central and Eastern Europe at the moment when the Marshall Plan offer of June 1947 had to be refused by all of them in unison. Yugoslavia and Bulgaria, the most radical of the new people's democracies, were used by Zhdanov at Szklarska Poręba as the cudgel with which to discipline the more cautious parties — the French, the Italian, the Polish — into accepting the new line. Tito was, in September 1947, Stalin's favoured pupil. Belgrade, not Moscow, was chosen as the Cominform's headquarters. The Yugoslav party was held up as the model of revolutionary energy.

What broke this in nine months was not ideology but the Balkans. Tito and Dimitrov, beginning in the autumn of 1947, had been discussing a Balkan federation that would include Yugoslavia, Bulgaria, and possibly Albania, with the prospect of intervention in the Greek civil war on behalf of the communist insurgents. From Belgrade's perspective this was the natural completion of the South Slav project. From Moscow's perspective it was an unauthorised attempt by a satellite to construct a sub-empire of its own, in a region — Greece, the Aegean — where Stalin had explicitly conceded British and American primacy at the Percentages Agreement of 1944. In January 1948 Stalin summoned Kardelj and Djilas to Moscow and told them, in a meeting that Djilas recorded with extraordinary precision in Conversations with Stalin, that the Greek insurgency had to be wound up, that the federation with Bulgaria had to proceed on Moscow's timetable rather than Belgrade's, and that Yugoslav troops were to be withdrawn from Albania. The substance was strategic. The form was the assertion of imperial supremacy over a junior party that had begun to think for itself. The Yugoslavs went home and refused.

What followed between February and June 1948 is one of the better-documented escalations in the diplomatic record. The Soviet trade delegation was withdrawn on 18 March. Soviet military advisers were withdrawn on 19 March. A series of increasingly threatening letters were exchanged between the two Central Committees in March, April, and May, of which the most important is the Soviet letter of 27 March accusing the Yugoslav party of departing from "Marxism-Leninism" and listing specific Yugoslav functionaries as Western spies — Vladimir Velebit by name, by implication Djilas, by implication Tito himself. The Yugoslav reply, drafted by Tito personally on 13 April and approved unanimously by the Yugoslav Central Committee, did something no satellite party had ever done: it rejected the Soviet accusations in writing, by name, on the substance, and asserted the equality of communist parties in their internal affairs. Stalin had assumed, on the basis of his experience with Poland in 1944 and Romania in 1945, that a sufficiently sharp letter from Moscow would produce a coup inside the Yugoslav Central Committee. The letter of 27 March was the threat; the letter of 4 May, which followed when no coup materialised, was the ultimatum. The Cominform meeting of June was the public execution of a verdict that had failed to be administered privately.

This is the strategic core of the error. Stalin believed his own propaganda about the depth of Soviet penetration of the Yugoslav party. The historical record now shows that the Soviet intelligence presence inside the Yugoslav security services and the party apparatus was wide but shallow — wide enough to identify potential collaborators, too shallow to mobilise them when the moment came. Two Yugoslav Central Committee members, Sreten Žujović and Andrija Hebrang, did side with Moscow. Both were arrested in May 1948 before the Cominform resolution was issued. No third figure of any standing emerged. When the Bucharest resolution called on the Yugoslav party to overthrow its leadership, the Yugoslav party responded by convening its Fifth Congress in July 1948, which acclaimed Tito as general secretary with 2,318 votes in favour and none against. Stalin's instrument had been ideological discipline; Tito's instrument was the war of national liberation between 1941 and 1945, which had produced a communist party that owed its existence to itself and not to the Red Army. The 1944 Soviet liberation of Belgrade had been a footnote to a war the Partisans had largely already won. This distinction, invisible from the Kremlin, was decisive.

The downstream consequences ran in two directions and Stalin understood neither. The first was inside the bloc. The expulsion of Yugoslavia required Stalin to demonstrate, in the other satellites, that no second Tito would be tolerated. The purges of 1949–1953 — Rajk in Hungary, Kostov in Bulgaria, Slánský in Czechoslovakia, the arrest of Gomułka in Poland — were directly produced by the Yugoslav precedent. They consumed the founding cohort of indigenous Eastern European communism and replaced it with Moscow-trained apparatchiks whose authority within their own societies was thin and whose legitimacy was now openly tied to Soviet enforcement. The Hungarian rising of 1956 and the Prague Spring of 1968 are the long-run dividends of this hollowing-out. Stalin had wanted obedient satellites. He produced satellites whose obedience could no longer be assumed without armoured divisions, and the cost of those divisions, compounded over forty years, was one of the things that bankrupted the empire.

The second direction was outside the bloc. From August 1948, Tito was an isolated communist state on the front line of the Cold War with no Soviet protection and an open border with Italy and Austria. The American response, after some hesitation in Washington, was to extend wheat shipments in the autumn of 1949, an Export-Import Bank loan of $20 million in September 1949, IMF drawing rights, and from 1951 a flow of military equipment under the Mutual Defence Assistance Act that by 1955 had reached approximately $750 million. The Yugoslav army of 1956 was armed with American F-86 Sabres, M47 Pattons, and 105mm howitzers. A communist state, expelled from the communist movement, was being equipped by the United States to defend itself against the communist superpower. The model this offered to every subsequent dissident communist movement — that there was a way out from under Moscow that did not require ceasing to be communist — was the model that, twelve years later, Mao would partially adopt, that Ceaușescu would imitate in his eccentric fashion, that Hoxha would invert, and that, in its furthest development, Gorbachev would attempt to apply to the Soviet Union itself.

Stalin had wanted, on 28 June 1948, to demonstrate the indivisibility of the socialist camp. He had instead demonstrated its divisibility, named the conditions under which division was possible, and offered a working example. The Cominform was dissolved in 1956, eight years after Bucharest, with no resolution rescinding the expulsion of Yugoslavia and no admission of error. By then Khrushchev had been to Belgrade twice, apologised in writing, and conceded the principle of "different roads to socialism" that the Yugoslavs had asserted on 13 April 1948 and that Stalin had gone to war over. The concession, once made, could not be withdrawn. Forty-three years later, when the Soviet Union dissolved itself in December 1991, it dissolved on a principle — that there were multiple legitimate roads to socialism, including roads that led away from socialism altogether — which had been licensed at Bucharest, in the small hours of 29 June 1948, by the man who had assumed that he could shake his little finger and topple a partisan general.
