The Ambiguity That Made the Modern Middle East: McMahon, Hussein, and the Districts of Damascus

The grievance is canonical. In 1915 and 1916, the British High Commissioner in Cairo, Sir Henry McMahon, exchanged ten letters with the Sharif of Mecca, Hussein bin Ali, promising an independent Arab kingdom in exchange for a revolt against the Ottomans. The Arabs revolted. The British then carved up the promised territory with the French at Sykes-Picot, handed Palestine to the Zionists at Balfour, and installed Hussein's sons as figurehead monarchs in Iraq and Transjordan while keeping the strategic prizes for themselves. The Arabs were betrayed. The modern Middle East is the wreckage of that betrayal.

This story is true in outline and badly wrong in detail. The detail matters, because the detail is where the lie lives, and the lie has been recycled by everyone from Antonius in 1938 to contemporary Twitter for a century. The McMahon-Hussein correspondence was not a betrayed promise. It was a deliberately ambiguous document that both sides read against each other, and the ambiguity was Hussein's invention as much as McMahon's.

The hinge is one sentence in McMahon's letter of 24 October 1915. Hussein had demanded British recognition of Arab independence in a vast territory stretching from the Mediterranean to the Persian Gulf and from Mersin in southern Anatolia to the Indian Ocean. McMahon, instructed by London to commit to as little as possible while keeping Hussein in the war, replied with a series of exclusions. The critical one read: "The two districts of Mersina and Alexandretta and portions of Syria lying to the west of the districts of Damascus, Homs, Hama and Aleppo cannot be said to be purely Arab, and should be excluded from the limits demanded."

Notice what this sentence does not say. It does not say "west of a line running from Damascus to Aleppo". It says "west of the districts of" those four cities. The word translated as "districts" was wilayat in the Arabic version sent to Mecca — and wilayat in 1915 Ottoman usage meant the formal administrative province, the largest unit of imperial administration. The wilayat of Damascus extended south through the Hauran and into what is now Jordan, and Aleppo's wilayat reached east toward the Euphrates. If you exclude everything west of those provinces, you exclude only the coastal strip — Lebanon and the Alawite hinterland. Palestine, lying south and west of the Damascus wilayat, is not excluded by this reading. Hussein read it this way. He had to: anything else would have meant accepting a Jewish homeland in Palestine and a French Syria, which he could not sell to his fighters.

The British read it differently. In Cairo, McMahon's Arab Bureau understood wilayat in its older, looser sense — "district" as in vicinity, the surroundings of a named city. Under that reading the exclusion drew a vague line northward from the Damascus area through Aleppo, with everything to its west, including the bulk of what is now Lebanon and arguably southern Palestine, falling outside Arab control. The geography hinged on which dictionary you opened. McMahon's biographer Elie Kedourie established in 1976, with patient archival work in the India Office records, that McMahon himself was uncertain which meaning he had intended; his political secretary, Gilbert Clayton, drafted the sentence and could not, when pressed in 1922, reconstruct his own reasoning.

Hussein knew the language was unstable. His own foreign secretary, Fuad al-Khatib, raised the wilayat question internally in late 1915 and was told by Hussein himself to let it stand. The Sharif's calculation was rational: a clear promise from London naming Palestine would have given Britain pause and might have killed the deal; an ambiguous promise that he could later interpret maximally was worth more than a clear promise that committed to less. The British calculation was the mirror image. Sir Mark Sykes, briefing the War Cabinet in March 1916 — five months before he signed the agreement with Picot that contradicted everything — explicitly noted that "the McMahon letters are sufficiently elastic to permit the arrangement now contemplated with the French Government". Elastic. Not betrayed. Designed to be elastic.

This matters because the betrayal narrative does two things, both of them politically useful and historically misleading. It transfers full responsibility for the Middle Eastern settlement to perfidious Albion, absolving Hussein of his own miscalculation — which was that he had purchased clarity when in fact he had purchased ambiguity at a discount. And it treats the agreements of 1915-1920 as a single coherent betrayal, when in fact they were a sequence of contradictory commitments made by different parts of a wartime government that did not coordinate with each other and did not expect to win the war on the terms it eventually won it. The Foreign Office in London, the India Office in Delhi, the Arab Bureau in Cairo, and the War Office under Kitchener were running parallel and incompatible Middle Eastern policies in 1915. The contradictions only became visible in 1917, when the Bolsheviks published the secret Sykes-Picot text and forced the discrepancies into daylight.

The deepest casualty of the standard betrayal story is the Arab side's own agency. Hussein was not a credulous tribal sheikh who took the white man at his word. He was a sixty-year-old Ottoman politician who had spent fifteen years in Istanbul as Abdul Hamid's hostage and understood imperial diplomacy as well as anyone in Cairo. He gambled, calculatedly, that ambiguity could be cashed at the peace conference for more than clarity could buy in the field. He was wrong, because the peace conference was run by men — Lloyd George, Clemenceau, Wilson — who had no interest in the Arab Bureau's correspondence and who treated the Arab claim as a nuisance to be managed. The lesson of McMahon-Hussein is not that the British lied. It is that a wartime promise dictated by a colonial official under cable instruction from a Cabinet that did not control its own departments was always going to be worth less than a treaty signed by Prime Ministers in a hall at Versailles.

The Middle East was not made by betrayal. It was made by the routine collision of incompatible promises, none of which any of the issuing parties expected to be the binding one. That is a worse story than betrayal, because betrayal can be repaired by apology, and incoherence cannot.

  • George Antonius, The Arab Awakening: The Story of the Arab National Movement (Hamish Hamilton, 1938) — the foundational statement of the betrayal thesis; essential as the position Kedourie demolishes
