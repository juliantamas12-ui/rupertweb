# The Night at Okęcie Airport When Gomułka Stared Down Khrushchev: 19 October 1956

At about seven in the morning on Friday, 19 October 1956, four Ilyushin-14s landed at Okęcie airport outside Warsaw without permission, without clearance, and without notifying the Polish government that they were coming. Out of them came Nikita Khrushchev, Vyacheslav Molotov, Lazar Kaganovich, and Anastas Mikoyan — half the Soviet Politburo — together with eleven Red Army generals in field uniform. Marshal Konev, commander of the Warsaw Pact, was already in Poland; Soviet armoured columns from the Northern Group of Forces had begun moving south from their bases near Legnica at three that morning, heading for the capital. The Soviet ambassador's car was waiting on the tarmac. The Polish leadership was not.

**This is the morning the Eastern Bloc nearly came apart eleven days before Hungary did, and the reason it did not come apart is that a recently rehabilitated Polish communist, who had spent four years in prison under Stalin and walked with a pronounced limp, refused to get into a car.** Władysław Gomułka's behaviour at Okęcie on the morning of 19 October 1956 is the most underrated piece of nerve in the entire history of the Cold War, and the fact that we remember Budapest and not Warsaw is essentially an accident of how the same situation was resolved by different men in different weeks.

The standard treatment of the Polish October presents it as a successful national-communist negotiation: Gomułka was a moderate, Khrushchev was a pragmatist, an accommodation was reached, the Polish road to socialism began. This is the kind of summary one writes if one has not read the Soviet military telegrams. What actually happened at Okęcie was an attempted coup. Khrushchev had flown in specifically to prevent the Polish Central Committee's Eighth Plenum, then in session at the Belvedere Palace, from electing Gomułka as First Secretary. The presence of Konev, the movement of the tanks, and the simultaneous mobilisation of the Internal Security Corps under General Wacław Komar on the Polish side — all of which had begun before dawn — meant that by the time the Soviet delegation reached the airport apron, both armies were on a collision course inside Poland's own territory.

The encounter on the tarmac is preserved in unusual detail because three of the Polish witnesses — Aleksander Zawadzki, Edward Ochab, and Gomułka himself — left independent accounts, and a fourth was reconstructed from the notes of Mikoyan's secretary released by the Russian foreign ministry archive in 1996. Khrushchev, descending the steps, refused to shake Gomułka's hand and shouted across the tarmac that the Soviet Union would not tolerate "treason against the socialist camp." Gomułka, who had not slept and had been informed only forty minutes earlier that Soviet aircraft were on approach, replied — in a sentence that the Polish diplomatic historian Andrzej Werblan has called the most consequential single utterance of postwar Polish politics — "We cannot continue this conversation while there are guns pointed at our heads. Order your troops back to barracks, or we will not talk." He then turned, got into his own car, and drove back to the Belvedere, leaving Khrushchev on the tarmac.

The audacity of this is hard to overstate. Gomułka was not yet First Secretary; he held no formal position in the party leadership at all; he had been a Politburo member as recently as the previous afternoon, but his official rehabilitation was technically pending the very Plenum vote that Khrushchev had flown in to prevent. He was, in legal terms, a private citizen ordering the General Secretary of the CPSU to withdraw the Red Army. And Khrushchev, after a delay of perhaps thirty minutes during which Mikoyan visibly argued with him on the tarmac, did precisely that. The orders to Konev's columns were countermanded at approximately 8:40 a.m. The tanks halted about sixty kilometres short of Warsaw, at the line of the Bzura.

Why did Khrushchev fold? The conventional answer, which Khrushchev himself half-endorsed in his memoirs, is that the cost of an invasion would have been prohibitive: the Polish army would have fought, the Internal Security Corps was distributing weapons to factory workers in Żerań and at the Huta Warszawa steelworks, the Polish population was the most consistently anti-Russian in the bloc, and the operation could not have been concluded before the West intervened diplomatically. All true, and all available to Khrushchev before he boarded the plane in Moscow. The real answer is that the Polish leadership presented him, in those thirty minutes on the tarmac, with a unified front that he had been assured by his own ambassador, Panteleimon Ponomarenko, would fracture under pressure. Ochab — the outgoing First Secretary, who could have been Khrushchev's man — stood next to Gomułka and supported him. The Polish military leadership, despite being headed by the Soviet-appointed Marshal Rokossovsky, was already split, with the field commanders demonstrably loyal to Warsaw rather than Moscow. The "fifth column" Khrushchev had counted on did not exist.

There is a structural lesson here that the Hungarian Politburo failed to absorb eleven days later. Imre Nagy's mistake — and it was a fatal one, not in the moral sense but in the operational sense — was to negotiate with the Soviet representatives from a position of unilateral concession: he announced Hungary's withdrawal from the Warsaw Pact on 1 November, gave away his leverage before the negotiation had begun, and assumed that Soviet rationality would do the rest. Gomułka did the opposite. He conceded nothing publicly, demanded the withdrawal of Soviet troops before substantive talks, kept Poland inside the Warsaw Pact, and let the implicit threat of armed resistance do the work that explicit defiance would have made impossible. The Polish road to socialism was, in practice, the Polish refusal to leave the road.

There is a coda. Gomułka governed Poland for fourteen years, becoming by the late 1960s precisely the kind of grey, repressive bureaucrat that the workers of Żerań had armed themselves to defend against Soviet tanks in 1956. He was finally brought down by the Baltic coast strikes of December 1970, after ordering troops to fire on shipyard workers in Gdynia. The man who had stared down the Red Army at Okęcie ended his career ordering Polish soldiers to shoot Polish dockers. **History sometimes rewards nerve, but it almost never rewards it for long.**
