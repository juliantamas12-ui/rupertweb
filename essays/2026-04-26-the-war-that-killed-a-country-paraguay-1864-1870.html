The War That Killed a Country: Paraguay, 1864-1870

Between 1864 and 1870, the Republic of Paraguay fought a war against Brazil, Argentina, and Uruguay simultaneously, and lost. The losing was not the unusual part. The unusual part is what the loss looked like. By the time the Paraguayan dictator Francisco Solano López was speared to death on a riverbank at Cerro Corá in March 1870, somewhere between half and two-thirds of the country's pre-war population was dead. Most credible modern estimates put the male population loss at around ninety percent. Not ninety percent of soldiers. Ninety percent of men. The country went into the war with roughly 450,000 people and emerged with around 220,000, of whom perhaps 28,000 were adult males. This is, by a considerable margin, the most demographically destructive war fought by any state in the modern era. It is also a war almost no one outside South America has heard of.

The standard textbook account, when it bothers, treats this as a tragic accident — an unhinged tyrant fighting on past all reason against overwhelming odds. This is wrong, or at least incomplete. The War of the Triple Alliance was not a freak event. It was the predictable outcome of a particular kind of state colliding with a particular kind of world economy at a particular moment, and the reason it killed so many people is that all three of those things were structurally committed to its continuation long after any rational war aim had been achieved.

Start with Paraguay itself. By the 1860s, Paraguay was something genuinely strange in Latin America: a closed, autarkic, state-directed economy run since independence by two successive dictators — first the bizarre, brilliant José Gaspar Rodríguez de Francia, then the López family — on the principle that foreign capital was a trap and free trade a slow form of conquest. They were not entirely wrong. Argentina and Brazil in the same period were being economically colonised by British finance, and their sovereignty was visibly thinning. Paraguay, by contrast, had no foreign debt, a state-owned merchant fleet, the only telegraph line and the only railway in the southern interior of South America, a small but functional armaments industry, and a literacy rate that compared favourably with most of Europe. It was, on paper, the most successful experiment in nineteenth-century economic nationalism anywhere on the continent. It was also a hereditary military despotism with a standing army of around 60,000 in a country of less than half a million, which is to say a society organised around the assumption of imminent war.

That assumption was correct. The war began in late 1864 over a Uruguayan civil war that López, with reasonable strategic logic, did not want Brazil to win. He intervened. The intervention required crossing Argentine territory, which Argentina refused. López, having miscalculated catastrophically, declared war on Argentina too. By May 1865 he was at war with all three of his neighbours, who signed the Treaty of the Triple Alliance binding them to fight until his regime was destroyed. The treaty's secret clauses, leaked the following year by the British, provided for the partition of nearly half of Paraguay's territory between Brazil and Argentina. From that moment, the war could not end through negotiation, because the allied governments had legally committed themselves not to negotiate.

This is the first structural fact. Coalition wars with maximalist treaty terms cannot be stopped by their authors once they begin to bleed. The same logic that bound the Allies in 1918 to demand unconditional German surrender, and the Allies in 1945 to demand it from Japan, bound Brazil and Argentina in 1866. Even after López had been driven out of Argentine territory, even after Asunción had fallen, even after every conceivable strategic threat from Paraguay had ceased to exist, the war kept going. It kept going because the treaty said it had to.

The second structural fact is López himself. He was not insane, despite the standard accounts. He was something more interesting and more dangerous: a ruler whose entire political legitimacy rested on the fiction that the war was winnable. The Paraguayan state had built itself around military glory and racial-national exceptionalism — the Guaraní-speaking Catholic republic standing alone against Anglo-Brazilian-Argentine encirclement. To admit defeat was to dissolve the state's reason for existing. So López retreated into the interior with whatever army he had left, and when the army died he conscripted teenagers, and when the teenagers died he conscripted boys of ten and twelve, and when the boys died he conscripted women to carry supplies. The famous battle of Acosta Ñu in August 1869 was fought between the Brazilian army and a Paraguayan force consisting almost entirely of children. About 3,500 of the children were killed. The Brazilians lost 46 men.

The third structural fact is the global one. The 1860s were the decade in which the great powers became willing to underwrite extermination wars in the periphery as long as the periphery paid for it. Brazil financed its share of the war on London bond markets. Argentina did the same. The conflict was, from the City's point of view, a perfectly ordinary credit operation. No European government intervened to stop it, despite having intervened the previous decade in Crimea and the following decade in the Balkans. Paraguay's destruction generated debt service for British bondholders and opened a previously closed market to British exports. There was no reason to stop it.

What does this episode tell us? Three things, at least. First, that the most catastrophic wars are not the ones fought between great powers — those tend to be paused by exhaustion or stalemate — but the ones in which a small state with a totalising ideology meets a coalition contractually obliged to destroy it. Second, that the nineteenth-century liberal world order, the one we are sometimes told to mourn, was perfectly capable of presiding over the demographic annihilation of an entire country and recording it as a credit event. Third, that the historical memory of an atrocity is not a function of its scale. It is a function of who survives to tell it, and what languages they tell it in, and whether the metropolitan press is paying attention. Paraguay lost the war, lost half its territory, lost its men, and then lost the right to its own story. That last loss is the one that has lasted longest.
