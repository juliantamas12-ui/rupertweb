# The Last Man Who Could Have Saved the Tsar: Why Stolypin Was Killed by the Okhrana

On the evening of 1 September 1911, in the Kiev Opera House during a gala performance of Rimsky-Korsakov's *The Tale of Tsar Saltan* attended by Nicholas II, his daughters, and most of the senior officials of the Russian Empire, a 24-year-old anarchist named Dmitry Bogrov walked up to the orchestra-pit barrier during the second interval and shot Pyotr Arkadyevich Stolypin twice at point-blank range. One bullet lodged in his liver. Stolypin, the Prime Minister of the Russian Empire, the man who had crushed the 1905 revolution and was in the middle of remaking Russian agriculture from the ground up, turned to the imperial box, made the sign of the cross in the direction of the Tsar, and said, audibly, *"Happy to die for the Tsar."* He died four days later. The Tsar attended his funeral briefly and left for a hunting trip in Spała two weeks afterwards.

The standard line, repeated in textbooks and in the memoirs of every White émigré who survived the civil war to write them, is that Stolypin was the last great hope of Imperial Russia, that his murder removed the only statesman capable of carrying through the reforms that might have prevented 1917, and that Bogrov was a lone fanatic from the revolutionary fringe who slipped through inadequate security. Two of these three claims are roughly true. **The third is a lie that the Russian state, both before and after the revolution, has had institutional reasons to maintain. Bogrov was not a lone fanatic. He was a paid informant of the Kiev Okhrana — the Tsarist political police — and he was inside the Opera House that night, in a tailcoat, holding ticket number 406 in the parterre, because the Okhrana had given him the ticket.**

The mechanics of the assassination are the part of the story that gets quietly buried in most accounts. The chief of the Kiev Okhrana, Lieutenant-Colonel Nikolai Kulyabko, was the brother-in-law of Alexander Spiridovich, the head of the imperial palace guard. Kulyabko had been running Bogrov as an informant since 1907, when Bogrov, a law student from a wealthy Jewish family in Kiev, had walked into the Okhrana office and offered to denounce his anarchist comrades in exchange for protection from his own circle's growing suspicion that he was, in fact, a police informant. He was. Over the next four years he gave the Okhrana enough material to roll up cells in Kiev, St. Petersburg, and Paris.

In late August 1911, with the imperial visit to Kiev imminent, Bogrov contacted Kulyabko and reported a plot. There were, he said, two terrorists in the city — a man called "Nikolai Yakovlevich" and a woman called "Nina Aleksandrovna" — planning to assassinate either the Tsar or Stolypin during the festivities. Bogrov, he claimed, had been ordered by them to identify the targets in advance. To do this credibly, he needed access. Specifically, he needed a ticket to the gala at the Opera House.

Kulyabko gave him one. He did not search him. He did not assign a watcher. He did not inform either of the two ministers whose own security details might have noticed an unvetted civilian roaming the parterre during a state event. The director of the Department of Police, Pavel Kurlov — the man with overall responsibility for the Tsar's security on the trip — later claimed in his memoirs that he had assumed Bogrov was being kept under continuous surveillance by Kulyabko's men. He was not. The "two terrorists" did not exist. There was no plot to expose. Bogrov walked into the Opera House with a Browning pistol in his pocket and the active complicity, or at minimum the studied negligence, of the entire senior staff of the Kiev Okhrana.

The question of whether this was complicity or negligence is the question that mattered, and it was the question the regime refused to answer. Senator Maximilian Trusevich was appointed to investigate. His report, completed in 1912, recommended the criminal prosecution of Kurlov, Kulyabko, Spiridovich, and Mitrofan Verigin, the deputy director of the Department of Police, for dereliction of duty amounting to complicity in the murder of a Prime Minister. Nicholas II read the report and ordered the prosecution suspended. Kurlov was quietly retired on full pension. Kulyabko was tried, convicted of a minor administrative offence, and released after sixteen months. Spiridovich was promoted. The investigation was sealed.

Why? Because the people who had incentives to want Stolypin dead were not Bogrov's anarchist comrades, who barely knew him and considered him a traitor, but the men around the Tsar himself. **Stolypin's land reforms — the famous "wager on the strong", which encouraged peasants to leave the village commune and consolidate their holdings into private farms — were dissolving the social base of the conservative landed nobility on which the regime depended.** His proposed reform of local government would have replaced the noble-dominated zemstvo system with elected councils based on property rather than estate, breaking the political monopoly of the gentry in the provinces. He had humiliated the State Council in March 1911 by forcing through the western zemstvo law over its veto, using emergency powers under Article 87 in a way that infuriated both the right and the Tsar himself. He had publicly clashed with Rasputin, demanding his removal from the capital, and Alexandra had never forgiven him. By August 1911, Nicholas had decided to dismiss him. The dismissal was scheduled for the autumn. Stolypin knew it. He had told his deputy, Vladimir Kokovtsov, three weeks before Kiev: *"They no longer need me. They will kick me out."*

What the Okhrana did, on this reading, was solve a political problem the Tsar was about to face — how to dispose of a Prime Minister too popular and too effective to dismiss without scandal — by allowing it to be solved on his behalf. There is no smoking-gun document. There never is, in cases like this; that is the point of running an informant. What there is, instead, is a pattern: the unsearched assassin, the suspended investigation, the promoted officials, the sealed file, the imperial indifference at the funeral. The Okhrana had spent thirty years infiltrating revolutionary movements to the point where, by 1911, it was sometimes unclear whether a given terrorist cell was running its police informants or being run by them. Yevno Azef, the head of the Socialist Revolutionary Combat Organization, had been an Okhrana agent throughout the years he was personally organising the assassinations of Interior Minister Plehve and Grand Duke Sergei. The line between political police and political terrorism, in late Imperial Russia, was not a line. It was a fog.

Stolypin's death is usually read as a tragedy — the moment when the last serious reformer fell and the autocracy began the slow drift towards 1917. It is more useful read as a diagnosis. *A regime whose secret police can murder its own Prime Minister, and whose Tsar can decline to investigate the murder, is not a regime that can be reformed. It is a regime that has lost the capacity to distinguish between the agents of its survival and the agents of its destruction.* The six years between Stolypin's funeral and the February Revolution were not a missed opportunity. They were the working out, in slow motion, of a problem that had already been answered in a Kiev opera house on a September evening in 1911.
