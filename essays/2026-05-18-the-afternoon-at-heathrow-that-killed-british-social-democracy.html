The Afternoon at Heathrow That Killed British Social Democracy: 28 September 1976

On the afternoon of Tuesday, 28 September 1976, Denis Healey, Chancellor of the Exchequer in James Callaghan's Labour government, was driven from the Treasury to Heathrow airport in the back of a government Rover, intending to board a flight to Hong Kong for the annual meetings of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. He never boarded. By the time he reached the airport, sterling was in free fall on the foreign exchanges — it had broken through $1.65, down from $2.02 at the beginning of the year and from $2.40 two years before — and the duty clerk at the Treasury had Healey's private secretary on the line begging him to come back. Healey turned around in the VIP lounge, returned to London, and three days later, on 30 September, on the rostrum of the Labour Party Conference at Blackpool, told a furious hall of trade unionists and constituency activists that he was applying to the International Monetary Fund for the largest loan in its history, that the conditions attached would require cuts to public expenditure of two billion pounds, and that there was no alternative. The hall booed him. Tony Benn, watching from the platform, recorded in his diary that night that he had just witnessed the death of the Labour Party as a socialist project. He was three years out and entirely correct. The 1976 IMF crisis, and not Margaret Thatcher's victory in May 1979, was the moment that British social democracy as it had existed since 1945 came to an end. Everything that followed — the monetarism, the privatisations, the assault on the unions, the financialisation of the economy — was made possible because a Labour government had already conceded, under duress, the central premise on which the postwar settlement rested.

The premise was that the British state had both the right and the duty to manage demand in the domestic economy in pursuit of full employment, and that the constraints imposed by the external balance of payments could be negotiated, evaded, or borrowed around. This had been the working creed of British economic policy under every government, Labour and Conservative, since Hugh Dalton's first budget in 1945. It had survived the devaluation of 1949, the Suez sterling crisis of 1956, the devaluation of 1967, the floating of the pound in 1972, the oil shock of 1973-74, and the inflation of 1974-75 which had reached an annualised rate of twenty-seven per cent in the summer of 1975. What it could not survive was the simultaneous collapse of the trade unions' willingness to accept wage restraint, the markets' willingness to finance the British deficit, and the Cabinet's willingness to defend the existing strategy against the new orthodoxy emanating from Chicago and Frankfurt. By September 1976 all three had given way at once.

The immediate cause of the sterling crisis was not, as the Treasury subsequently claimed and as a generation of British political memoir has repeated, the irresponsibility of the markets. It was a calibrated decision by the Bank of England, taken in March 1976, to nudge the pound downward to make British exports more competitive. The nudge, badly handled, became a rout. By June, Healey had been forced to negotiate a $5.3 billion standby credit from the central banks of the G10 — money that was explicitly conditional on it being repaid in full by December, and which Britain had no realistic prospect of repaying without further action. The trap closed in late August when the figures for the Public Sector Borrowing Requirement, briefed selectively to the financial press by Treasury officials who wanted to force the Cabinet's hand, came in at twelve billion pounds — roughly eleven per cent of GDP, a figure that would not be exceeded by any British government for nearly half a century. The markets concluded that the British state was effectively insolvent. Sterling resumed its fall. By the third week of September the Bank of England's reserves were being burned through at a rate that, on internal Treasury projections, would have exhausted them within six weeks.

What followed was a four-month Cabinet battle that is, even now, the most extraordinary documented argument over economic strategy in modern British government. Tony Benn, Secretary of State for Energy, drafted with the help of his special advisers — Francis Cripps and Frances Morrell — a complete alternative economic strategy: import controls, mandatory planning agreements with the largest corporations, an emergency budget aimed at protecting employment, and, if necessary, the resignation of Britain's IMF application. Peter Shore, at Environment, supported him. Stan Orme and Albert Booth joined them at the critical Cabinet meetings of 23 November and 1-2 December. Against them stood Healey, Callaghan, and a Treasury machine that had already, in private bilateral conversations with the IMF mission in London, agreed in principle to the cuts the IMF would require. The decisive figure was not Callaghan, who oscillated, but the Foreign Secretary Anthony Crosland — author of The Future of Socialism, the intellectual founder of postwar Labour revisionism, the man whose entire political identity had been built on the proposition that growth and equality were compatible. Crosland, on 1 December, told the Cabinet that the IMF terms were "intolerable" and that he opposed them. Eighteen hours later, after a meeting with Callaghan at which the Prime Minister told him that the alternative was the collapse of the government, he reversed himself and voted to accept. He died of a stroke six weeks later. His widow told friends that what had killed him was the knowledge that he had ended his own life's work.

The terms were imposed on 15 December 1976. They were not, in fact, particularly harsh by the standards of subsequent IMF interventions: cuts to public spending of two and a half billion pounds over two years, ceilings on Domestic Credit Expansion, and the sale of the government's residual holding in BP. The damage was not in the numbers. The damage was that a Labour government had, for the first time, formally accepted that the management of the British economy was subject to external monetary discipline; that the priority of the British state, in extremis, had to be the defence of the currency rather than the maintenance of employment; and that the trade-off between inflation and unemployment, which Keynesian theory had treated as a continuous variable that policy could optimise, was now to be understood as a hard constraint that policy had to accept. Callaghan said it himself at the Labour Conference in Blackpool, in the speech immediately preceding Healey's: "We used to think that you could spend your way out of a recession and increase employment by cutting taxes and boosting government spending. I tell you in all candour that that option no longer exists." The line, ghosted by his son-in-law Peter Jay, was a deliberate and conscious repudiation of every assumption on which Labour economic policy had been built since the war. It was delivered ten weeks before Margaret Thatcher had even consolidated her position as Conservative leader against the Heathite remnant of her own front bench.

The deeper question, and the one that British political historiography has spent fifty years trying not to ask honestly, is whether the alternative existed. Benn's strategy would have required exchange controls of a kind that, after 1979, would have been incompatible with the Single European Act and, after 1985, with the global liberalisation of capital flows. The political coalition required to enforce it — a permanent alliance between the parliamentary left and the militant industrial unions — was already breaking down by 1977, and would shatter completely in the Winter of Discontent. The honest answer is that the alternative was not available to a Labour government working within the institutions of the British state as those institutions had developed since the end of capital controls and the floating of sterling. It might have been available to a different kind of state. The Labour Party had not built one and was not going to.

What was lost in the autumn of 1976 was not a particular policy or even a particular generation's political project. It was the conviction that democratic politics, in a country with an open economy and a reserve currency under pressure, could set the terms on which capital was deployed. Once that conviction was abandoned — and it was abandoned by Labour, not by the Conservatives — the entire subsequent history of British politics became a series of arguments about how to manage decline rather than how to direct development. Blair and Brown built their entire project on the premise that Callaghan had accepted: that the constraints were given, that the markets had to be conciliated, that the role of social democracy was to redistribute at the margins what a financialised economy produced. The implosion of that settlement after 2008, and the strange politics of the years since — the Scottish referendum, Brexit, Corbyn, the Truss disaster, the slow disintegration of Labour's hold on its industrial heartlands — are all reverberations of a decision taken in a VIP lounge at Heathrow on a Tuesday afternoon in September 1976, by a man who had been told that if he got on the plane the pound would not be there when he landed.

Healey turned the car around. Nothing in British politics has been the same since.

  • Kathleen Burk and Alec Cairncross, "Goodbye, Great Britain": The 1976 IMF Crisis (Yale University Press, 1992) — the standard archival account, based on full access to Treasury and Cabinet Office papers
  • Denis Healey, The Time of My Life (Michael Joseph, 1989) — Healey's own account of the Heathrow turnaround and the Cabinet battle, candid and self-justifying in roughly equal measure

  • Steve Ludlam, "The Gnomes of Washington: Four Myths of the 1976 IMF Crisis," Political Studies 40, no. 4 (1992) — the definitive corrective to the Treasury's official narrative
  • Edmund Dell, A Hard Pounding: Politics and Economic Crisis, 1974-1976 (Oxford, 1991) — by Healey's Paymaster General; particularly strong on the Treasury machine's role in pre-cooking the IMF terms
  • Susan Crosland, Tony Crosland (Jonathan Cape, 1982) — the source for Crosland's reversal and the personal cost it carried
  • James Callaghan, Time and Chance (Collins, 1987) — including the full text of the 28 September Blackpool speech and Callaghan's account of his decisive meeting with Crosland
