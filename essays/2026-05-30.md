# The Three Weeks in May 1958 When de Gaulle Took France Without Firing a Shot

On 13 May 1958, a crowd of European settlers stormed the headquarters of the Government-General in Algiers, ransacked the offices, and set up a Committee of Public Safety chaired by General Jacques Massu, the paratroop commander who had run the torture campaign in the Battle of Algiers fifteen months earlier. The committee's first cable to Paris demanded a government of national unity under Charles de Gaulle. De Gaulle had been in political retirement at his house in Colombey-les-Deux-Églises for almost twelve years. He had no party, no parliamentary seat, and no formal connection with the army officers who had just seized the largest French city in Africa in his name. Twenty-two days later, on 1 June, the National Assembly in Paris voted him full powers as the last Prime Minister of the Fourth Republic by 329 votes to 224. On 3 June it voted to dissolve itself and grant him six months to draft a new constitution. On 28 September the French electorate approved that constitution by 82.6 per cent. On 21 December the same electorate elected him President of the Fifth Republic.

**De Gaulle took power in 1958 without leading a single soldier, without issuing a single threat, and without breaking, on paper, a single article of the existing constitution. He took power because, for three weeks in May, he allowed the army in Algiers to believe he was about to lead a coup, allowed the politicians in Paris to believe he was the only man who could stop one, and never confirmed either reading to either audience.** It is one of the cleanest examples in twentieth-century European history of a politician winning by saying as little as possible to as many people as possible. It is also the moment the French Republic, for the second time in eighty years, was rescued from the army by a general who, technically, did not have an army of his own.

The mechanics begin with what the Algiers committee actually wanted. The settlers and the paratroopers who joined them did not want de Gaulle as such. They wanted *Algérie française* — Algeria as an integral part of France — and they wanted the Fourth Republic, which they believed was about to negotiate independence with the FLN, replaced by a government that would prosecute the war to a finish. De Gaulle had never publicly committed himself to either position. His last political act before retiring in 1946 had been to resign in protest at the constitution the Fourth Republic was then drafting; his most recent significant utterance, a press conference in 1955, had been deliberately ambiguous on Algeria. The Algiers conspirators chose him because they believed his prestige would be enough to bring the politicians into line, and because his silence on the substantive question allowed each faction within the committee to project its own preferred policy onto him. He did not correct them.

On 15 May, two days after the Algiers committee was established, de Gaulle issued a one-paragraph statement from Colombey. It said that the degradation of the state inevitably led to alienation from associated peoples, troubles in the army at war, national dislocation, and the loss of independence; that twelve years earlier, faced with the necessity of reconstructing the country, he had withdrawn from public affairs; and that today, faced with the trials again rising before the country, he was ready to assume the powers of the Republic. He used the phrase *les pouvoirs de la République* — the powers of the Republic. He did not say which powers, or from whom he expected to receive them, or what he would do with them. The Algiers committee read it as endorsement. The Paris political class read it as a warning.

This was the operative ambiguity. For twelve days the question of whether de Gaulle was the leader of the Algiers coup or the alternative to it remained unanswered. On 19 May he gave a press conference at the Hôtel d'Orsay in which he refused, with the patience of a man explaining a simple matter to a child, to disavow Massu, to disavow the Committee of Public Safety, or to commit himself to any constitutional procedure for taking office. When asked whether he intended to respect public liberties, he replied that he had restored them in 1944 and had no need to prove anything on the point. When asked whether he was a danger to the Republic, he replied that at sixty-seven he was unlikely to begin a career as a dictator. He did not say he would not take power outside the constitution. He said he would not need to.

In the meantime the operational pressure built. On 24 May the paratroop garrison of Corsica, acting on orders from Algiers, seized the island in a bloodless operation called *Opération Résurrection*. Plans existed for a parallel operation against metropolitan France, with paratroop drops on Paris scheduled for the night of 27–28 May if the Pflimlin government had not resigned by then. The Paris police prefecture, the gendarmerie command, and elements of the air force had been quietly contacted by intermediaries connected to the Algiers committee and had returned ambiguous answers. The Fourth Republic, by the fourth week of May, possessed no security force on which the government could be certain to rely against a determined airborne assault by its own army.

It was at exactly this moment, on the evening of 27 May, that de Gaulle issued a second short statement, this one of two sentences. The first sentence said he had begun the regular process necessary for the establishment of a republican government capable of assuring the unity and independence of the country. The second sentence asked the armed forces in Algeria to maintain exemplary discipline under the orders of their commanders. **The statement was a lie. He had begun no such process. He had no mandate to give orders to the army. But because nobody in Paris or Algiers could afford to call him on it — the politicians because they were terrified of the paratroopers, the paratroopers because they needed him to be a politician — the lie became true by being believed.** *Opération Résurrection* was postponed. The Pflimlin government, faced with the impossibility of governing against an army that now believed it was acting on de Gaulle's authority, resigned on 28 May. President Coty asked de Gaulle to form a government on 29 May. The National Assembly invested him on 1 June.

The Fifth Republic was therefore born from a manoeuvre in which a private citizen, by issuing two ambiguous press statements over a period of twelve days, induced an army to suspend a coup it had already begun and induced a parliament to vote itself out of existence to avoid that coup. The constitution he drafted afterwards gave the President powers no French head of state had held since the constitutional crisis of 1877; the referendum that ratified it consolidated those powers with the moral authority of an 82 per cent majority. Within four years he would use those powers to negotiate Algerian independence — the precise outcome the Algiers committee had risen on 13 May to prevent. The army, when it understood it had been used, attempted a second coup in April 1961. De Gaulle defeated that one in three days by speaking directly to the conscripts over the radio. By then he held the powers he had refused to specify in May 1958, and he no longer needed to be ambiguous about them.

The interesting question is not how de Gaulle did it, which is reasonably clear from the documents. The interesting question is why nobody since has been able to repeat the trick. The answer is probably that de Gaulle's ambiguity worked because both audiences — the paratroopers and the parliament — believed, correctly, that he was the only available alternative to a civil war neither of them wanted. He did not have to threaten. He had only to exist, and to refuse to clarify what his existence meant. **Power, in the most refined form Europe has produced in the past century, was taken by a man who never raised his voice, never deployed a soldier, and never explicitly said what he wanted.** He simply waited, in a village two hundred miles from Paris, until both sides agreed that whatever he wanted was preferable to whatever they were about to do to each other. The Fifth Republic, now sixty-eight years old and still the longest-lived French constitutional order since 1789, rests on three weeks of strategic silence and one carefully constructed press release that was not true at the moment it was issued.

It became true because it had to.
