History 27 April 2026

The Two Men Thrown Out a Window Who Killed a Third of Germany

On 23 May 1618, a group of Bohemian Protestant nobles entered Prague Castle, seized two Catholic regents named Slavata and Martinitz along with their secretary Fabricius, dragged them across a council chamber, and threw all three out of a third-storey window. They survived. The Catholics later said angels caught them. The Protestants said they landed in a dung heap. The historical consensus is that the dung heap is more probable. What is not in dispute is that this minor act of political theatre — the Second Defenestration of Prague — set off a war that lasted thirty years, killed somewhere between a quarter and a third of the population of the German lands, reduced cities like Magdeburg from twenty-five thousand inhabitants to under five thousand, and produced a peace treaty that is still routinely cited as the founding document of the modern international system.

The interesting question is not why the Bohemians threw the regents out a window. Bohemia had a tradition of defenestrating people it disliked; this was the second time, hence the number. The interesting question is why a local quarrel between a Protestant Estates and their Catholic Habsburg overlord was capable of metastasising into a continental catastrophe involving Sweden, France, Spain, the Dutch Republic, Denmark, the Papacy, the Ottomans at the margins, and roughly three hundred German principalities most of whose names no one alive can now reliably pronounce.

The standard answer is religion. This is not wrong but it is shallow. Europe had been religiously divided since 1517 and had managed not to destroy itself. The Peace of Augsburg in 1555 had produced a workable, if cynical, settlement: cuius regio, eius religio — whose realm, his religion. Each prince in the Holy Roman Empire chose Catholic or Lutheran for his own territory, and dissenters could emigrate. It was not tolerance. It was a containment strategy. And for sixty-three years, it worked.

What broke it was not religious passion. It was a structural feature of the Holy Roman Empire that had previously been a strength: deliberate constitutional ambiguity.

The Empire was not a state. It was a frozen lawsuit. It had an emperor, but the emperor was elected by seven prince-electors and could not tax the constituent territories. It had an Imperial Diet, but the Diet operated by consensus across three colleges with conflicting interests. It had imperial courts, but their rulings were enforceable only when the major princes agreed they should be. The system functioned because everyone — Catholics, Lutherans, the Habsburgs, the French — preferred a weak Empire to any clear alternative. A strong Empire would have been a Habsburg superpower. A dissolved Empire would have been chaos. So everyone tolerated a structure that no one would have designed from scratch.

The Augsburg settlement papered over three specific time bombs. First, it had not recognised Calvinism, which by 1618 was the dominant Protestant confession in the Rhineland and Bohemia. Second, it had said nothing clear about what happened when a Catholic bishop converted to Protestantism and tried to take his bishopric's lands with him. Third, it had assumed that the religion of a territory could be changed only by its prince, ignoring the question of what to do when the prince was elected by his subjects — which was exactly the constitutional position of the King of Bohemia.

In 1617 the Bohemian Estates were forced to accept the fanatically Catholic Ferdinand of Styria as their next king. Ferdinand had spent his youth in Jesuit Bavaria and had reconverted his own duchy by force. The Bohemian Protestants, reading the future correctly, threw his regents out of a window and offered the crown to Frederick V of the Palatinate, a Calvinist. This was the moment the war became unstoppable. Frederick was the leading Calvinist prince in the Empire and the son-in-law of James I of England. His acceptance turned a Bohemian succession dispute into a confessional war, an imperial constitutional crisis, and a European diplomatic emergency simultaneously, because every interested power now had to decide whether the Empire was about to become a Habsburg dictatorship or a Calvinist federation.

It became neither, because once the war started it acquired its own logic. Spain intervened to protect the overland route from Italy to the Netherlands. Denmark intervened to grab North German bishoprics. Sweden intervened, under Gustavus Adolphus, partly for Lutheranism and largely for Baltic trade revenues. France, Catholic France, intervened on the Protestant side because Cardinal Richelieu had correctly diagnosed that a Habsburg-dominated Empire would surround France on three sides. By the 1630s the original Bohemian quarrel was forgotten. The armies were mostly mercenary, paid by plunder, and they moved through Germany burning what they could not eat. This is how Magdeburg lost eighty percent of its population in three days in 1631.

The lesson is not that ambiguity in constitutions is bad. Ambiguity is often what allows incompatible factions to coexist. The lesson is that ambiguity has a half-life. The compromises that hold a system together in one generation are precisely the unresolved questions that detonate it in the next, once the original negotiators are dead and the conditions that made the fudge tolerable have changed. The Peace of Augsburg worked until it didn't, and when it stopped working the cost of writing a new settlement was a third of the German population.

The Peace of Westphalia in 1648 finally wrote that settlement. It recognised Calvinism, fixed the bishopric question, and effectively ended the Holy Roman Empire as a political project while leaving its corpse formally upright for another century and a half. Historians like to call it the birth of the modern state system. It is more accurate to call it the bill for sixty-three years of unpaid constitutional debt.
