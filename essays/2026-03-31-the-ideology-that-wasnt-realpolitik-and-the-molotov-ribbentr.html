The Ideology That Wasn't: Realpolitik and the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact

In August 1939, the two most ideologically opposed regimes in modern history signed a non-aggression pact. Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union — one built on racial supremacism and violent anti-communism, the other on proletarian internationalism and violent anti-fascism — shook hands, divided Eastern Europe between them in secret protocols, and cooperated in the destruction of Poland. Within two years, one had invaded the other. Both sides claimed betrayal. Neither had any right to be surprised.

The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact is usually taught as a curiosity, a cynical maneuver before the real war started. That framing is wrong. It is one of the clearest demonstrations in twentieth-century history of what political scientists call the security dilemma operating at full force — and of what happens when you take regime ideology seriously as a predictor of state behavior. The pact doesn't just tell us about 1939. It tells us something fundamental about power, survival, and the difference between a state's stated ideology and its operational logic.

The Ideological Gap

Hitler had been explicit. Mein Kampf — published in 1925, fourteen years before the pact — described Bolshevism as the ultimate expression of Jewish domination of a racially inferior Slavic people. The destruction of the Soviet Union was not a contingency plan but a stated civilizational mission. Lebensraum, the idea of German expansion into the East, required the subjugation or elimination of the Soviet state. There was no ideological wiggle room. The Nazis did not secretly admire communism, nor did they misunderstand it. They hated it on principle, systematically, loudly.

Stalin's position was symmetrical. Soviet ideology cast fascism as the terminal crisis of capitalism — monopoly capital's last resort when threatened by the revolutionary proletariat. The Comintern had classified fascism as the primary enemy for most of the 1930s. The Popular Front strategy, adopted after 1935, was specifically designed to build a broad anti-fascist coalition. Communist parties across Europe were instructed to align with liberal democracies against Hitler. Anti-fascism was the official line, enforced through purges and executions for anyone who suggested otherwise.

And yet, in August 1939, both regimes discarded this ideology as completely as if it had never existed.

What Actually Drove the Decision

The standard explanation — "both sides bought time" — is accurate but incomplete. Stalin genuinely feared that Britain and France were maneuvering to direct German aggression eastward. The Munich Agreement of 1938, which handed Czechoslovakia's Sudetenland to Hitler without consulting the Soviets, was not lost on him. The Western democracies had shown they would rather appease Hitler at others' expense than risk war themselves. From Moscow's perspective, a pact with Germany was insurance against being handed over.

For Hitler, the calculus was simpler: he wanted to avoid a two-front war. Invading Poland while the Soviet Union remained neutral was manageable. Invading Poland with Soviet hostility in the East was a repeat of the First World War's strategic nightmare. The pact bought him the eastern flank.

But here is what gets overlooked: neither side believed the other. German military planners never stopped preparing for eventual war with the USSR. Soviet intelligence repeatedly warned Stalin of German invasion preparations in 1941, and he ignored the warnings not because he trusted Hitler but because he calculated he needed more time. The pact was not a relationship. It was two parties using each other instrumentally, both knowing the other was doing exactly the same thing.

This is what Realpolitik actually looks like in practice. Not cynicism as a personality trait but as a structural feature of how states behave when survival is the primary variable. Ideology is expensive. It constrains choices. When survival is directly threatened, states routinely discard it — and then reconstruct it afterward to justify what they did.

The Secret Protocols

The non-aggression pact's public text was already jarring enough. The secret protocols — which the Soviet Union denied until 1989 — were nakedly imperial. Europe east of Germany was divided into German and Soviet "spheres of influence." Finland, Estonia, Latvia, and eastern Poland went to the Soviet sphere. Lithuania and western Poland went to Germany. This was not socialist internationalism. It was spheres-of-influence imperialism of the type that socialists had spent decades condemning as the defining crime of capitalist states.

The Soviet occupation of eastern Poland in September 1939, coordinated with the German invasion from the west, resulted in mass deportations, the Katyn massacre of approximately 22,000 Polish officers and intellectuals by the NKVD, and the incorporation of the Baltic states into the USSR by 1940. None of this is consistent with any reading of Marxist-Leninist ideology. It is, however, entirely consistent with the logic of a great power expanding its buffer zone while it had the chance.

What This Should Change About How You Think

The temptation, especially for young people learning political theory, is to take ideological pronouncements seriously as predictors. States that call themselves democratic act democratically; states that call themselves socialist act according to socialist principles; fascist states act according to fascist principles. This is naive — not in a cynical "everything is equally corrupt" way, but in a precise analytical sense.

The better framework is to distinguish between a regime's legitimating ideology — what it tells its population to justify its rule — and its operational logic, which is determined primarily by the structural pressures of the international system: survival, security, resource competition, and the behavior of rivals. Ideology shapes legitimating narratives and often constrains certain choices at the margins. It does not override survival imperatives.

This doesn't mean ideology is irrelevant. Hitler's ideological commitment to the racial destruction of Soviet peoples made the 1941 invasion more brutal and ultimately more self-defeating than pure strategic logic would have required. If Germany had treated the Soviet population as a liberated people rather than subhumans to be exploited, the Wehrmacht's advance might have looked very different. Ideology made Operation Barbarossa worse as a military campaign precisely because it overrode rational operational decisions.

But in August 1939, ideology was set aside entirely. What remained was power, fear, and time.

The Lesson

The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact should be a permanent corrective against the assumption that political actors mean what they say. States cooperate with enemies when expedient. They abandon stated principles when survival demands it. They reconstruct ideological justifications after the fact. This is not a special feature of totalitarian regimes — liberal democracies do it too. Britain's alliance with Stalin against Hitler, America's support for authoritarian regimes throughout the Cold War, the endless flexibility of "democratic values" when oil or strategic interests are at stake — the pattern is universal.

The question worth asking about any geopolitical situation is not "what does this country say it believes?" but "what structural pressures is it operating under, and what does that predict about its behavior?" The answer is usually less inspiring and more accurate.
