# The Night Two Junior Officers Conquered Manchuria and Killed Japanese Democracy

On the evening of 18 September 1931, a small charge of dynamite exploded on the South Manchuria Railway line just outside Mukden, in what is now Shenyang. The damage was negligible. A train passed over the spot a few minutes later without derailing. By morning the Kwantung Army — the Japanese garrison force in the leased Liaodong territory — had occupied Mukden, killed several hundred Chinese soldiers in their barracks, and begun a campaign that within five months had seized a territory larger than France, Germany, and Italy combined. The Japanese cabinet in Tokyo learned about the operation from the newspapers. The Foreign Minister, Shidehara Kijūrō, who had spent a decade building a cooperative Asia policy on the assumption that civilian government could control its own army, resigned within three months. He never held office again.

The conventional reading is that the Mukden Incident — *Manshū Jihen* — was the opening shot of Japanese militarism, the moment a militarist state began the march that ended at Pearl Harbor. **This reading mistakes the consequence for the cause.** Japan was not a militarist state in September 1931. It was a parliamentary democracy with a mass-circulation liberal press, two major political parties alternating in office, a labour movement large enough to elect socialist deputies, and a foreign policy built around the League of Nations and naval arms limitation. What Mukden did was not to confirm an existing militarism. It established a constitutional principle that destroyed the civilian state from within: that field officers, acting without orders, could initiate foreign wars, and that Tokyo would ratify their decisions retroactively rather than punish them. Once that principle was accepted, parliamentary government in Japan had perhaps eighteen months to live.

The operation was the work of two men. Colonel Ishiwara Kanji, the Kwantung Army's operations officer, was a Nichiren Buddhist mystic who believed that history was moving toward a final apocalyptic war between Japan and the United States, and that Japan could only win this war if it controlled the resources of Manchuria. Colonel Itagaki Seishirō, the senior staff officer, was a less interesting figure but a more competent organiser. The two had spent eighteen months planning the incident in detail. They had mapped Chinese garrisons, pre-positioned Japanese forces, drafted the proclamations to be issued in the names of local Chinese collaborators, and selected the specific point on the railway where the explosion would occur — close enough to a Chinese barracks to make the frame-up plausible, far enough from a Japanese garrison to make the response look defensive. They had not informed their superiors. They had not informed the General Staff in Tokyo. They had not informed the Emperor. The Kwantung Army's commanding general, Honjō Shigeru, learned of the operation roughly an hour before it began, accepted it as a fait accompli, and signed the orders he was handed.

This was, by any reading of the Meiji Constitution, treason. The constitution placed supreme command in the Emperor. It placed the conduct of foreign relations in the cabinet. Two colonels had assumed both prerogatives. The Prime Minister, Wakatsuki Reijirō, understood this perfectly. He convened the cabinet within hours of receiving news of the operation and demanded that the General Staff order the Kwantung Army to halt its advance. The Army Minister, General Minami Jirō, agreed, issued the order, and then quietly informed the Kwantung Army that the order was for political purposes and need not be obeyed. The Kwantung Army did not obey it. Reinforcements crossed the Korean border without imperial sanction — a separate and graver constitutional violation, since the Korea Army was a different command — and the General Staff, when asked, told the cabinet that the movement could not now be reversed. **The civilian government discovered, in the space of about ten days, that it did not control the army that nominally served it.**

It could have asserted control. The Emperor could have ordered Honjō and his subordinates court-martialled. The cabinet could have refused supplementary military appropriations. The Diet, which the parties controlled, could have impeached the Army Minister. None of this happened. The reason is the second, and more important, half of the story: the Japanese public loved what the Kwantung Army had done. Mukden was reported in the press as a defensive operation against Chinese provocation, then as a glorious recovery of Japan's rightful position in Asia, then as a vindication of national honour against the cowardice and treachery of the political parties. The newspapers — including the great liberal papers, the *Asahi* and the *Mainichi* — competed to outdo each other in jingoism. Circulation surged. The opposition Seiyūkai party, smelling political advantage, denounced the Wakatsuki cabinet for its weakness in restraining the army. By December, Wakatsuki had resigned. His successor, Inukai Tsuyoshi of the Seiyūkai, took office on a platform of supporting the Manchurian operation while attempting, quietly, to limit its further expansion. Six months later a group of young naval officers shot him dead in his official residence. They were tried, convicted, and given derisory sentences after a public campaign — including a petition signed in blood by eleven young men in Niigata who offered to take their place — convinced the court that their patriotism was its own mitigation. No civilian politician ever held the premiership again until 1945.

The point is not that the army seized power. The army did not need to seize power. It had discovered something more useful: that it could act, and the political system would adjust itself around its actions. **Once the cost of insubordination was zero and the reward was promotion, the question of who governed Japan was settled in favour of whoever was willing to act first.** Ishiwara himself, who wanted to consolidate Manchuria and avoid war with China proper, was sidelined within six years by officers who had learned his lesson and applied it more ambitiously. The Marco Polo Bridge Incident in 1937, which began the full Sino-Japanese War, was another local provocation by junior officers, ratified retroactively by Tokyo because the alternative — disciplining the insubordinates — would have been politically impossible. Pearl Harbor, in 1941, was the same principle applied at the level of the Naval General Staff itself: a decision made by the men with the operational capability to make it, presented to the cabinet as the only option left.

Ishiwara Kanji died in 1949, in his bed, unindicted at Tokyo. The prosecutors had concluded that since the Mukden Incident had not been an official act of the Japanese state, it could not be a war crime. This was, in its way, an accurate verdict. It was not an act of the Japanese state. It was the act that ended the Japanese state, replacing it with something that wore its uniform but obeyed its own logic. The two colonels who planted that small charge on the South Manchuria Railway did not know they were inaugurating fourteen years of war that would kill thirty million people, most of them Chinese. They knew only that they had decided to act, that no one had stopped them, and that no one would. That, in the end, is how parliamentary systems die: not in a single coup, but in a series of small precedents in which the people charged with enforcing the rules discover, one by one, that they would rather not.
