The Silver That Killed an Empire: Potosí, the Ming, and the First Global Crisis

In 1545, an indigenous llama herder named Diego Huallpa slipped on a mountainside in what is now Bolivia, grabbed a shrub to steady himself, and pulled up roots coated in raw silver. The mountain was Cerro Rico — the Rich Hill — and within a decade it had become the most valuable piece of real estate on Earth. Over the next two centuries, Potosí would produce roughly 60 percent of all the silver mined in the world. What happened next is usually told as a story about Spain: galleons, conquistadors, the Habsburg empire gorging itself on American bullion until inflation destroyed it. That story is not wrong. It is just provincial. The real drama of Potosí silver was not what it did to Madrid. It was what it did to Beijing.

By the mid-sixteenth century, Ming China had quietly made a decision that would tether its fate to a mountain on the other side of the planet. For over a century the Ming had tried to run the world's largest economy on paper money and copper coinage, and both had failed spectacularly — the paper through hyperinflation, the copper through sheer inadequacy for large transactions. In a series of reforms culminating in the Single Whip Law of 1581, the Ming state demanded that taxes be paid in silver. Not in grain. Not in labor. Not in cloth. Silver. An empire of 150 million people suddenly needed enormous and continuous quantities of a metal it barely produced.

The solution arrived by accident. Spanish silver, carried across the Pacific on the Manila galleons that sailed from Acapulco to the Philippines from 1565 onward, poured into Chinese ports because Chinese demand made silver worth roughly twice as much in China as in Europe. Arbitrage on this scale had never existed before. A Spanish merchant could ship silver across two oceans and still double his money in Canton. The result was the first genuinely global economy — a circuit that ran from Andean mines worked by conscripted indigenous labor, through Seville, Antwerp, and Manila, into the tax collectors of a Confucian bureaucracy trying to pay its soldiers. Historians call this the world's first silver standard. It was also, arguably, the first time a monetary policy error in one civilization could be triggered by geology in another.

For a while the system was miraculous. Ming tax receipts stabilized. Long-distance trade exploded. Ceramics, silk, and tea moved west; silver moved east; and a small Portuguese outpost at Macao became the hinge of the world. Then, starting in the 1630s, something went wrong simultaneously in three places that had no business being connected.

In Potosí, the richest veins of the Cerro Rico were exhausted. Extraction costs rose, output fell. In Japan — which had become the world's second-largest silver producer — the Tokugawa shogunate, alarmed by the outflow of bullion, began severely restricting silver exports and eventually closed the country. And in Spain, a combination of Philip IV's ruinous European wars, plague, and fiscal mismanagement reduced the quantity of American silver actually reaching Asian markets by a third. The global silver supply contracted at exactly the moment the Ming had made itself structurally dependent on its expansion.

The effects inside China were savage. Silver-denominated taxes had been calibrated to one exchange rate; when silver grew scarce, its value against copper doubled in two decades. A peasant who paid his tax in copper coins now owed twice as much in real terms. Rural debt exploded. Bandit armies formed. The imperial treasury, paid in appreciating silver but unable to squeeze more of it from an already crushed peasantry, could not pay its own soldiers on the northern frontier. Manchu raids turned into Manchu armies. In 1644, a peasant rebel named Li Zicheng took Beijing. The last Ming emperor hanged himself from a tree behind the Forbidden City. Within a year the Manchus had marched in, declared the Qing dynasty, and begun a conquest that would take decades.

Every dynasty that collapses collapses for a dozen reasons, and the Ming had no shortage: court factionalism, eunuch corruption, a disastrous little ice age that wrecked harvests, Jurchen cavalry, administrative rot. But the monetary dimension is the one that gets under-told because it demands you look at a mountain in Bolivia to explain a suicide in Beijing. Once you see the circuit, it is impossible to unsee. A state had made its entire fiscal architecture depend on a commodity produced on another continent, controlled by a foreign empire, transported across oceans it could not patrol, extracted by labor systems it did not oversee. When the supply chain broke — for reasons that had nothing to do with Chinese policy — the Ming state lost the ability to pay its army at precisely the moment it needed to fight two wars.

This is the first historical example of what we would now call a global supply shock bringing down a great power. It is also a useful corrective to a certain comfortable Western history that treats early modern globalization as something Europeans did to everyone else. The Ming were not passive victims. They made a deliberate monetary choice — a sophisticated one — and integrated themselves into a world system on their own terms. What they could not anticipate was that a world system has its own weather, and that the weather gets made in places you cannot see.

The lesson is not that interdependence is bad. The lesson is that interdependence is load-bearing, and states that treat foreign-supplied commodities as permanent features of their tax base are gambling on geopolitics they do not control. Potosí's silver was the oil of its century. And like oil, it eventually taught its consumers that dependence and sovereignty are not the same word.

The Qing learned the lesson. They rebuilt the Chinese state on a more diversified fiscal base, diffused silver dependence across multiple commodities, and lasted 267 years. Then, in the nineteenth century, another foreign substance arrived by ship at Canton and did to them what silver had done to the Ming. Its name was opium. But that is a different essay.
