The Man Who Made Authoritarianism Respectable: Carl Schmitt and the Permanent Emergency

The Man Who Made Authoritarianism Respectable: Carl Schmitt and the Permanent Emergency

In 1922, a German legal theorist named Carl Schmitt wrote a single sentence that has haunted democratic theory ever since: "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception." It is the most consequential sentence in twentieth-century political philosophy, and it came from a man who would go on to join the Nazi Party in 1933 and spend the rest of his career trying to live down the association. The tension between the power of the idea and the wretchedness of the man is exactly the problem Schmitt forces you to confront.

Schmitt's central argument is this: every political order ultimately rests not on law, but on a decision. Legal systems cannot govern themselves. When a genuine crisis arrives — war, revolution, catastrophe — someone has to act outside the normal rules. The law itself cannot tell you when to suspend the law. That gap, that necessary exception, reveals where real power actually lives. The sovereign is whoever fills it.

This is not a celebration of dictatorship. It is a description of something unavoidably true. Every functioning state maintains some version of emergency powers. The United States has had formal states of emergency continuously since 1979. France invoked emergency rule after the 2015 Paris attacks and eventually absorbed those powers into ordinary law. Britain's wartime Emergency Powers Act gave the executive near-dictatorial authority between 1939 and 1945. Schmitt did not invent the state of exception; he named it and showed why it could never be abolished.

The second concept that makes Schmitt indispensable is his definition of "the political" itself. Most liberal political theory assumes politics is ultimately about economics, rights, preferences, deliberation — the management of competing interests toward some shared benefit. Schmitt thought this was naive to the point of delusion. His definition is stark: the political is defined by the distinction between friend and enemy. Not moral enemies. Not personal enemies. But a collective "they" whose existence and difference constitutes a genuine existential threat to the "we." Politics, in this reading, is not disagreement that gets resolved. It is antagonism that structures everything else.

Why does this matter? Because liberal democracy has an enormous blind spot on exactly this point. It wants to believe that all conflicts are ultimately negotiable — that the right institutions, the right deliberative procedures, the right constitutional design, can dissolve the friend/enemy distinction into mere policy debate. Schmitt's argument is that this belief is not just wrong but actively dangerous, because it leaves liberal states without a conceptual vocabulary for genuine political crises. When the enemy arrives, the liberal constitutionalist finds himself paralyzed: the rules he trusts are precisely what cannot save him.

You can see this in recent history with uncomfortable clarity. The European Union was built on the premise that economic interdependence would gradually dissolve the friend/enemy logic that produced two world wars. For decades, this worked well enough that serious people started believing it was true. Then came migration crises, economic divergence, and the return of great-power competition. The friend/enemy distinction did not vanish. It came back in the form of populist parties across the continent that openly named their enemies and were rewarded with electoral success by electorates that liberal elites had decided no longer harbored such primitive instincts.

Schmitt's final major provocation concerns legitimacy versus legality. Weimar Germany, he argued in 1932, was legal but not legitimate. It had functioning courts, elections, procedures. What it lacked was a population that believed in the system enough to defend it against those who wanted to destroy it from within. He was correct in his diagnosis, catastrophically wrong in his prescription. Schmitt thought the answer was stronger executive authority, a decisionist sovereignty that could act against constitutional enemies without being hamstrung by constitutional niceties. He underestimated — or deliberately ignored — the question of who decides when the constitution's enemies have arrived. He gave that power to Hitler and called it theory.

This is the Schmitt problem in miniature. His tools are sharp and they cut in every direction, including the hand that wields them. Left-wing theorists like Walter Benjamin, Chantal Mouffe, and Giorgio Agamben have all drawn heavily on Schmitt precisely because his framework exposes the pretensions of liberal neutrality. If all politics is ultimately the friend/enemy distinction, then the liberal claim to represent universal values rather than a particular interest is just another partisan move dressed up in cosmopolitan clothing. That critique lands. It stings. It is useful.

But Schmitt himself never resolved the question his framework raises most urgently: if every political order is grounded in a decision rather than a norm, what constrains the decider? His answer was essentially nothing — the sovereign stands outside the law by definition. Which is another way of saying that Schmitt's political theory has no internal resources for limiting power. It is a brilliant description of how authority actually works, and a complete silence on whether authority should have limits.

The honest reader takes both things from Schmitt: the diagnostic power and the warning embedded in his biography. He was not wrong that emergencies reveal the true architecture of power. He was catastrophically wrong that this revelation should make us comfortable with unconstrained sovereignty. The state of exception is real. The question is whether you build institutions that return to normalcy after the exception, or institutions that make the exception permanent.

Most governments since 1945 have quietly chosen the latter. Schmitt would not have been surprised.
