# The Stranded Army That Decided the Russian Civil War: The Czechoslovak Legion and the Trans-Siberian Railway, 1918

On the afternoon of 14 May 1918, at the railway station in Chelyabinsk, in the south Urals, a Hungarian prisoner of war returning home under the terms of the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk threw a piece of cast iron out of his train carriage at a Czech soldier on the opposite platform. The Czech, named František Duchaček, fell to the ground with his skull fractured. His comrades, who belonged to the Czechoslovak Legion — a force of around forty thousand former Habsburg prisoners of war and deserters who had been raised to fight against Austria-Hungary on the side of Imperial Russia — dragged the Hungarian off the train and lynched him on the platform. The local Bolshevik soviet, alarmed, arrested ten of the Czechs. Within a fortnight, the rest of the Legion, strung out along four thousand miles of the Trans-Siberian Railway between Penza and Vladivostok, had risen against the Soviet government, seized the stations, and effectively detached Siberia from Moscow's control. The Russian Civil War, which until then had been a marginal and largely Cossack affair on the Don and the Kuban, suddenly acquired a continental front. **The Bolsheviks were almost destroyed in the summer of 1918, and the reason was not Denikin, not Kolchak, and not the British landing at Murmansk. The reason was forty thousand Czechs who had been trying to go home and could not.**

The story is so improbable that Western historiography has tended to treat it as a curiosity rather than a hinge. It deserves better. The Czechoslovak Legion in 1918 was the most coherent and best-equipped armed force on Russian soil. The Red Army did not yet exist as a functioning institution — Trotsky had been appointed Commissar for War only in March, and the first conscription decree was issued in June, in direct response to the Legion's revolt. The Volunteer Army of Alekseev and Kornilov in the south numbered perhaps four thousand men in February 1918 and was reduced to fewer than three thousand after the Ice March. The various Cossack hosts were locally formidable but politically incoherent. Into this vacuum, in late May, marched a fully-uniformed, fully-armed, fully-officered corps of trained infantry, commanded by Czech and Slovak nationalists whose only war aim was to get to the Western Front via Vladivostok so they could keep fighting Austria-Hungary and earn the diplomatic recognition that would make a postwar Czechoslovak state possible.

That war aim is the key to the whole episode. The Legion was not in Russia by accident, and it was not in Siberia by choice. Its members had been raised originally to fight on the Eastern Front under the Tsar; after the October Revolution and the Soviet decision to negotiate a separate peace at Brest-Litovsk, their position became impossible. Sending them home would mean sending them back to the Habsburg authorities, who would hang them as traitors. Sending them to the Western Front, where the French government, prompted by Tomáš Masaryk's ceaseless lobbying in Paris, had agreed to incorporate them as an autonomous Czechoslovak army, required moving them out of Russia. The shortest route — through the Baltic or the Black Sea — was closed by the Germans. The only route left was eastward: forty trains, four thousand miles, across the disintegrating Russian state, to the Pacific, around the world, and into northern France. In March 1918 Masaryk in Moscow personally negotiated with Stalin (then Commissar of Nationalities) a transit agreement: the Legion would surrender most of its heavy weapons and be permitted to travel east as a civilian formation. The agreement was signed and immediately began to break down, partly because the local soviets along the line did not trust it, partly because German diplomats in Moscow were pressuring Lenin to disarm the Legion entirely, and partly because the trains kept being held to give priority to the eastbound trains of the Austro-Hungarian and German prisoners of war who, under Brest-Litovsk, were being repatriated to fight against the French — which was, from the Czech point of view, intolerable.

What happened at Chelyabinsk was therefore not a misunderstanding. It was the eruption of a contradiction that had been building since February. The Hungarians on the eastbound trains were, in the Czechs' eyes, men they had been fighting six months before and would be fighting six months hence. The Bolshevik authorities were the people preventing them from getting to the war that mattered. When Trotsky, on 25 May, ordered the local soviets to disarm the Legion completely and shoot any Czech found bearing arms, the Legion's leadership — meeting in conference at Chelyabinsk on 20 May — decided to refuse, to seize the railway, and to fight their way through to Vladivostok. Within two weeks they had taken Penza, Samara, Ufa, Yekaterinburg, Omsk, and Vladivostok itself. By August they controlled the entire Trans-Siberian.

The political consequences cascaded with extraordinary speed. The Komuch government — the Committee of Members of the Constituent Assembly — was established in Samara on 8 June 1918, made up of the Socialist Revolutionaries who had won the November 1917 elections and been chased out of the assembly by Lenin in January 1918. It existed because the Czechs had cleared the Volga of Bolshevik forces and offered the SRs a base. The Provisional All-Russian Government was created at Ufa in September, again under Czech protection. Admiral Kolchak's coup in Omsk on 18 November 1918, which established the White dictatorship in Siberia, took place only because the Legion had created the Siberian space in which a Russian counter-revolutionary government could even imagine existing. The Romanov family was murdered at Yekaterinburg on the night of 16-17 July 1918 specifically because the Legion was a week away from taking the city and the local Bolsheviks had decided that the Tsar and his family could not be allowed to be liberated. The Allied intervention in Russia — the British at Archangel, the Americans and Japanese at Vladivostok, the French in Odessa — was justified at the time and afterwards by the need to "rescue" the Czechoslovak Legion, even though the Legion was by then in no need of rescue and was in fact engaged in conquering Siberia. The intervention's actual purpose, reopening an eastern front against Germany, was rendered moot by the Armistice in November; what remained was the policy of strangling the Bolshevik regime in its cradle, and the Legion was the pretext that made it intelligible to Western publics.

The Legion's own fate is the cruel coda. By the spring of 1919, Kolchak's dictatorship had alienated them politically — they were democratic nationalists, he was a reactionary admiral — and the war they had been raised to fight was over. They wanted, once again, only to go home. They negotiated with the Red Army for safe passage, and in February 1920, at Irkutsk, they handed Kolchak over to the Bolsheviks in exchange for the right to proceed unmolested to Vladivostok. Kolchak was shot the next morning and his body thrown through a hole in the ice of the Angara river. The Legion sailed from Vladivostok in batches between January and September 1920, having reached Russia in 1916 and 1917 to fight a war that had ended eighteen months before they reached home. The Czechoslovak state that Masaryk had built in their absence received them as heroes. The Russian Civil War, decided in their favour against the Bolsheviks in the summer of 1918 and decided against them by their own exhaustion eighteen months later, ended with the Reds in possession of everything from Petrograd to the Pacific.

The deepest lesson of the episode is not about Russia or Czechoslovakia. It is about how much of history is decided by armed bodies of men who are in the wrong place, want to be somewhere else, and find that the only way to get there is through. The Legion was not fighting for any of the political programmes its operations made possible. It was trying to catch a ship. The Komuch, the Provisional Government, Kolchak, the murder of the Romanovs, the Allied intervention, and arguably the entire shape of the Russian Civil War were the wake of a unit in transit. **Lenin understood this perfectly. In a remark in November 1918 he called the Czechoslovak revolt "the turning point of the entire situation."** He was right, and the Legion's commanders, who never wanted to determine the fate of Russia and never accepted that they had, would have been the last to disagree.

## Sources

- Joan McGuire Mohr, *The Czech and Slovak Legion in Siberia, 1917-1922* (McFarland, 2012) — the standard English-language operational and political history, with full archival coverage from Prague and Moscow
- Victor M. Fic, *The Bolsheviks and the Czechoslovak Legion: The Origin of Their Armed Conflict, March-May 1918* (Abhinav, 1978), and his subsequent volumes on Chelyabinsk and the Volga campaign — the most detailed reconstruction of the Brest-Litovsk transit negotiations and the breakdown at Chelyabinsk
- Jonathan D. Smele, *The "Russian" Civil Wars, 1916-1926: Ten Years That Shook the World* (Oxford, 2016) — places the Legion's revolt at the centre of the war's chronology rather than at its margin
- Evan Mawdsley, *The Russian Civil War* (Birlinn, 2008 edition) — clearest concise treatment of the Komuch and Ufa episodes
- Richard Pipes, *Russia Under the Bolshevik Regime* (Knopf, 1993) — for the Romanov murders and the Yekaterinburg context
- Tomáš G. Masaryk, *The Making of a State: Memories and Observations, 1914-1918* (Allen & Unwin, 1927) — Masaryk's own account of the Moscow negotiations with Stalin in spring 1918
- Bruce Lockhart, *Memoirs of a British Agent* (Putnam, 1932) — the contemporary British diplomatic view from Moscow during the Legion's revolt
