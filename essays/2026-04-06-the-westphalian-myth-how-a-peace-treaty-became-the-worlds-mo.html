The Westphalian Myth: How a Peace Treaty Became the World's Most Useful Fiction

The Westphalian Myth: How a Peace Treaty Became the World's Most Useful Fiction

In 1648, two treaties signed in the German cities of Osnabrück and Münster ended thirty years of catastrophic religious warfare in Europe. Hundreds of thousands were dead. The Holy Roman Empire — that peculiar, multi-layered constitutional structure that had dominated Central European politics for centuries — emerged from the conflict functionally hollow. What replaced it, according to a story now taught in every international relations course on earth, was the modern state system: sovereign, territorial, equal. The Peace of Westphalia, the story goes, invented the rules of the world we live in.

The problem is that this is almost entirely wrong. And the gap between the myth and the reality tells us something important about how international order actually works — and why it tends to collapse.

What Actually Happened in 1648

The treaties did several concrete things. They confirmed the Peace of Augsburg (1555), which had already established the principle of cuius regio, eius religio — whoever rules the territory determines its religion. They recognised Calvinism alongside Lutheranism and Catholicism as legitimate confessions. They redistributed territories, confirmed Swedish and French territorial gains, and established a complex system of guarantees among the signatory powers. The Pope, furious at Catholic concessions, issued a bull declaring the whole settlement null and void. It was ignored.

None of this, to be precise, established the principle of absolute sovereignty. The Emperor retained significant formal powers. The Pope retained moral authority. The great powers retained the explicit right to intervene in each other's religious affairs under the guarantee provisions. Small territories remained embedded in feudal hierarchies. The idea that Westphalia created a clean system of mutually non-interfering sovereign equals is a retrospective invention — largely constructed by legal scholars and IR theorists in the twentieth century who needed a founding myth for their discipline.

The historian Andreas Osiander demonstrated this comprehensively in a 2001 article in International Organization, showing that the "Westphalian system" as routinely described was essentially invented in the mid-twentieth century as a rhetorical and pedagogical device. The treaties themselves do not contain the language of sovereignty in any form recognisable to modern readers.

Why the Myth Became Indispensable

If the Westphalian system is a fiction, why has it been so durable? Because it is a useful fiction — perhaps the most useful one in the history of statecraft.

The post-1945 international order needed a principled foundation that could accommodate both the victorious powers and the wave of decolonising states entering the system. The UN Charter's Article 2(1), establishing the sovereign equality of member states, required a historical pedigree. Westphalia provided it — a clean origin story that made modern sovereignty seem like a natural, evolved solution rather than a Cold War political compromise.

For newly independent states in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, sovereignty was not an abstraction. It was a shield. The insistence on non-interference, however hypocritically applied by the great powers, gave smaller states a vocabulary with which to resist. The Westphalian myth, even if historically fabricated, did real political work.

This is how founding myths function. The American founding myth — rational men in Philadelphia calmly deliberating universal principles — obscures the slave economy, the land speculation, and the near-collapse of the Constitutional Convention. The myth is not historically accurate. It is politically productive. Societies and systems need origin stories that legitimise their present arrangements, and they tend to construct them retroactively.

The Cracks

The fiction is now visibly straining. From three directions simultaneously.

First, the great powers themselves have increasingly abandoned sovereign non-interference as a restraint. The 2003 invasion of Iraq, justified through the doctrine of preventive war and the spread of democracy, drove a truck through it. Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, justified through historical revisionism and claims of denazification, did so again — this time while explicitly invoking the Western precedent of Kosovo (1999) as counter-justification. When all sides abandon the norm while citing it against each other, the norm is dead in practice even if alive in rhetoric.

Second, transnational problems — climate change, pandemic disease, financial contagion — are structurally incompatible with Westphalian sovereignty. Carbon emissions do not respect borders. A pathogen moves through airports faster than any Security Council resolution. The international climate architecture that has slowly assembled since 1992 requires states to accept binding constraints on their domestic economic choices, which is precisely what absolute sovereignty forbids. The tension is not resolvable; it can only be managed.

Third, and most interestingly, the state itself is losing its monopoly on organised violence and significant political agency to non-state actors: transnational terrorist networks, private military companies, cryptocurrency-funded insurgencies, and technology platforms that control the information environment of entire populations. The Westphalian fiction assumed the state was the relevant unit. That assumption is increasingly difficult to maintain.

What Comes Next

This is where honest analysis becomes genuinely hard, because there is no obvious replacement. The alternatives to the sovereign state system on offer are not attractive. World government remains a fantasy. Regional governance (the EU being the most advanced experiment) has demonstrated both its potential and its brittleness — Brexit was a reminder that political loyalty cannot be simply transferred upward by technocratic design. A return to something resembling the pre-Westphalian world of overlapping jurisdictions and competing authorities is more likely than either cosmopolitan governance or pure great-power concert.

The more probable trajectory is fragmentation: a world where the formal language of sovereignty persists, but effective authority is exercised by a patchwork of states, corporations, international institutions, and non-state actors whose relative weight varies by domain and geography. In some areas — nuclear weapons, maritime shipping lanes, global finance — great-power coordination will remain indispensable. In others — digital regulation, public health, climate — looser coalitions of the willing will do most of the actual work, with the sovereign state providing legitimating cover.

The Westphalian myth will probably survive this transition, because it will remain useful. States will continue to invoke it selectively, when it serves their interests, and ignore it equally selectively when it does not. The fiction will persist precisely because no one has yet produced a better one.

That is, perhaps, the most important thing the story of Westphalia teaches: international order is not a discovered natural phenomenon. It is a political construction, built on shared fictions that work until they don't, and requiring constant maintenance, negotiation, and occasional creative dishonesty to keep functioning. The relevant question is never whether the founding myth is accurate. It is whether the myth is still doing enough useful work to justify maintaining it.

In 1648, exhausted Europeans decided that a treaty was worth calling a foundation. They were right to do so. Whether we are approaching a moment when a new fiction needs to be invented — or whether the old one can be patched again — is the defining question of the current disorder.
