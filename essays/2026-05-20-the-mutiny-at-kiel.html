The Mutiny at Kiel: How the German Navy Toppled the Kaiserreich in Seventy-Two Hours, October-November 1918

On the evening of 29 October 1918, in the roadstead of Schillig outside Wilhelmshaven, the stokers and seamen of the German High Seas Fleet refused to weigh anchor. The order had come from Admiral Reinhard Scheer's headquarters that morning: the fleet was to sortie into the North Sea, engage the Grand Fleet off the Flemish coast or the mouth of the Thames, and — in the words of Operation Order 19, drafted three days earlier by Admiral Adolf von Trotha — "die honourably." Germany was, at that moment, two weeks into an armistice negotiation. President Wilson's third note, delivered on 23 October, had effectively demanded the abdication of the Kaiser and democratic reform as the price of peace. The civilian government in Berlin under Prince Max of Baden was preparing to concede both. The Imperial Navy's senior officers, who had spent four years watching the army fight the war while their dreadnoughts swung at anchor, decided independently that they would not allow the war to end without a final action. They did not inform Prince Max. They did not inform the Kaiser. They issued the order, and the lower deck — eighty thousand men in the great ships at Wilhelmshaven and Kiel — refused.

This is the moment the Kaiserreich ended. Not the abdication on 9 November, not the armistice in the railway carriage at Compiègne on 11 November, not Scheidemann's improvised proclamation of the republic from a window of the Reichstag. The German Empire ended on the night of 29 October 1918, when twenty thousand sailors in the third squadron of the High Seas Fleet decided that they would rather face a firing squad than be sacrificed to redeem the honour of an officer corps they despised.

The standard account treats the November Revolution as the outcome of military defeat — Ludendorff's nervous collapse in September, the breakthrough at Amiens, the collapse of Bulgaria, the cascade of armistice notes. All of this is true and none of it is sufficient. Defeated states do not necessarily become republics; they become defeated monarchies. Austria-Hungary in October 1918 dissolved along its national fault lines but the Habsburgs did not fall to revolution. Bulgaria signed the armistice of Salonika on 29 September and Tsar Ferdinand abdicated five days later in favour of his son Boris III, who reigned for another twenty-five years. The Ottoman sultan signed the armistice of Mudros on 30 October and remained on his throne until 1922. The German case is the anomaly: a military defeat that produced not a managed dynastic transition but a republican rupture, complete with red flags over the naval bases and workers' and soldiers' councils running cities by mid-November. The reason is specifically what happened in the fleet between 29 October and 4 November, and what happened in the fleet was the consequence of a decision taken by perhaps fifteen senior naval officers who had read Trafalgar, Tsushima, and Jutland and concluded that they could not face the postwar world without one more battle.

The grievances of the lower deck were not new. Food rations on the capital ships had been catastrophically worse than those of submariners and small-craft crews since 1916; the famous "turnip winter" of 1916-17 had hit the great ships at anchor harder than it hit any other sector of the German armed forces, because they were not deployed and therefore not on combat priority. The officer corps lived in a parallel world of stewards, wine messes, and shore leave to Hamburg; the crews lived on coffee made from acorns and bread cut with sawdust. A first wave of unrest in August 1917 had culminated in the execution of stokers Albin Köbis and Max Reichpietsch at Wahn on 5 September 1917 — judicial murders that had been ordered explicitly to "make examples" and that were still vividly remembered fourteen months later. When the order came on 29 October 1918 to put to sea against the Grand Fleet — a fleet that outnumbered the High Seas Fleet roughly two to one and had radio intelligence that would have ensured interception — the lower deck reached an entirely rational conclusion. They were being asked to die to allow their officers to avoid the political consequences of an armistice their political masters had already conceded.

What followed was the most consequential refusal of orders in twentieth-century European history. On 30 October, fires were extinguished on the Thüringen and the Helgoland; the boilers went cold. The fleet command, terrified, cancelled the sortie but ordered the arrest of roughly a thousand sailors as ringleaders. The squadron was dispersed — the third squadron, including the Markgraf and the Kronprinz, was ordered to Kiel — in the belief that breaking up the units would break up the mutiny. It did the opposite. The arrested sailors arrived at Kiel under guard on 1 November and were marched through the streets to the military prison; the local sailors, soldiers, and dockworkers organised demonstrations to demand their release. On 3 November, a meeting of about three thousand sailors on the Großer Exerzierplatz was fired on by an army patrol under Lieutenant Steinhäuser; seven demonstrators were killed and twenty-nine wounded. By the morning of 4 November, the entire Kiel garrison — sailors, soldiers, and dockworkers — had elected councils, occupied the city, raised the red flag over the Imperial Yard, and forced the governor, Admiral Wilhelm Souchon, to negotiate. Souchon's superior, Prince Heinrich of Prussia, fled the city in a lorry with a red flag tied to it, having borrowed the flag from a sailor to get through the checkpoints.

The dynamics that followed were extraordinarily fast. Sailors from Kiel travelled — in many cases by train, with weapons, in organised parties — to every major German port and inland city. Hamburg fell to a workers' and sailors' council on 6 November; Bremen, Lübeck, and Wilhelmshaven the same day. Hanover and Cologne fell on the 7th. Munich fell on the night of 7-8 November, when Kurt Eisner proclaimed the Bavarian Republic; the Wittelsbach dynasty, which had ruled Bavaria for seven hundred and thirty-eight years, ended at about ten in the evening when King Ludwig III left the Residenz through a back door with his family in a motor-car. The Kaiser abdicated on 9 November under the pressure of the Berlin general strike, which had itself been triggered by news of the Kiel events. The republic was proclaimed twice in two hours by competing factions of the left from different windows of the Reichstag. Within seventy-two hours of the shots on the Großer Exerzierplatz, a four-hundred-year-old constitutional order had ceased to exist.

The deepest point about the Kiel mutiny is that it was not, in any meaningful sense, a revolution from the left. The sailors who refused to sortie on 29 October were not Spartacists; the great majority were not even Independent Social Democrats. They were trade unionists, conservative Catholic stokers from the Saar, Lutheran farm boys from Mecklenburg, and they were refusing a specific order on rational grounds. What turned their refusal into a revolution was the response of the naval command — the dispersal, the arrests, the march through Kiel — and then the shots fired on 3 November. The officers who had planned Operation Order 19 in the belief that a final battle would preserve the navy's honour ended by destroying both the navy and the state it served, and they did so by treating the refusal of an order to commit suicide as an act of treason. The Kaiserreich did not fall because the German army was beaten in the field. It fell because the German navy's senior officers tried to use it as a sacrificial instrument, and the lower deck, having been treated as expendable for four years, finally noticed.

There is a strange and instructive afterlife. Admiral Hipper, who succeeded Scheer in fleet command, scuttled what remained of the High Seas Fleet at Scapa Flow on 21 June 1919, finally achieving the "honourable end" his officers had wanted in October 1918 — but achieving it without the dignity of a battle, in a foreign harbour, with the crews already disarmed and dispersed. Trotha, the author of Operation Order 19, became chief of the Naval Command in the Weimar Republic and one of the principal organisers of the secret rearmament programmes that would lay the foundation for the Kriegsmarine. The officer corps that destroyed the empire by refusing to accept defeat blamed the destruction on the sailors who had refused to be destroyed with them, and propagated, for the next fifteen years, a version of events in which the mutineers — not the order to sortie, not the shots on the Großer Exerzierplatz, not the four years of acorn coffee — were the "stab in the back." The Dolchstoßlegende was, at its origin, a naval document, a self-exculpation by men who had ordered a battle they knew would lose and then blamed their crews for declining to fight it.

  • Daniel Horn, The German Naval Mutinies of World War I (Rutgers, 1969) — still the indispensable English-language operational and political account, drawing on the surviving records of the Reichsmarineamt and the Imperial Naval Office
  • Wilhelm Deist, "Die Politik der Seekriegsleitung und die Rebellion der Flotte Ende Oktober 1918," in Vierteljahrshefte für Zeitgeschichte 14 (1966) — the foundational article that exposed Operation Order 19 as an unauthorised initiative of the naval command
  • Holger H. Herwig, "Luxury" Fleet: The Imperial German Navy 1888-1918 (Allen & Unwin, 1980) — for the social composition of the lower deck and the officer corps, and the long-term grievances
  • Mark Jones, Founding Weimar: Violence and the German Revolution of 1918-1919 (Cambridge, 2016) — the best recent treatment of the chain of events from Kiel to Berlin
  • Robert Gerwarth, November 1918: The German Revolution (Oxford, 2020) — synoptic narrative placing Kiel at the centre of the revolutionary cascade
  • Richard Bessel, Germany after the First World War (Oxford, 1993) — for the demobilisation context and the postwar mythology
  • Ernst-Heinrich Schmidt, Heimatheer und Revolution 1918 (Deutsche Verlags-Anstalt, 1981) — German military historiography on the army's response to the naval events
