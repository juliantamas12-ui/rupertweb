# The Afternoon at Safwan on 3 March 1991 When Schwarzkopf Negotiated a Ceasefire on a Map He Had Not Read and Let the Republican Guard Walk Home

On the afternoon of Sunday, 3 March 1991, in a tent pitched on a captured Iraqi airstrip at Safwan, six miles inside Iraq from the Kuwaiti border, General Norman Schwarzkopf sat down opposite Lieutenant-General Sultan Hashim Ahmad of the Iraqi army to dictate the terms of a ceasefire that had, in operational reality, already been in force for forty-eight hours. The shooting had stopped at 0800 local on 28 February, by personal order of President George H. W. Bush, after exactly one hundred hours of ground combat. Bush had wanted a round number. He had got one. He had also got, as it would turn out, a ceasefire imposed before his theatre commander had finished the operation the ceasefire was supposed to conclude.

What happened in the tent at Safwan that afternoon was not a negotiation. It was a transcription. Schwarzkopf had been given, by the Joint Chiefs in Washington the previous evening, a one-page list of points to obtain from the Iraqi delegation: prisoner returns, mine maps, demarcation lines, the immediate release of allied POWs. He worked through them in order. The Iraqis agreed to all of them in the first ninety minutes. Then, near the end, Sultan Hashim asked a question that was not on Schwarzkopf's list. He asked whether Iraqi military helicopters could fly across the demarcation line, given that the road network in southern Iraq had been destroyed and the Iraqi government needed to move officials around. Schwarzkopf, who had been on his feet for one hundred and twenty hours straight, who had no specific instructions on the helicopter question, and who was not looking at a map of Iraq's internal political geography because no such map had been provided to him, said yes. He attached one condition: the helicopters were not to be armed.

**That single concession, granted in roughly ten seconds at the end of a four-hour meeting, kept Saddam Hussein in power for twelve more years.**

The standard account of why the 1991 Gulf War ended without regime change runs through Bush's strategic restraint, the Arab coalition's red lines, the UN mandate that did not authorise a march on Baghdad, the fear of Iraqi disintegration creating an Iranian sphere. All of these are real. None of them explains why the Republican Guard, which Schwarzkopf had spent six weeks planning to destroy, walked back into southern Iraq with most of its armour intact and proceeded, within seventy-two hours of the Safwan meeting, to massacre the Shia uprising that the Bush administration had publicly encouraged. The strategic decisions explain why Coalition tanks did not roll into Baghdad. They do not explain why Iraqi gunships strafed Shia civilians at Karbala and Najaf for three weeks while American Apaches sat on the ramp at airfields in Saudi Arabia and watched. The thing that explains that is the helicopter clause.

Consider the operational situation at the moment Bush ordered the ceasefire. Schwarzkopf's plan, codenamed Desert Sabre, had two phases. Phase One was the breach of the Iraqi front-line conscript divisions, completed within twelve hours. Phase Two was the destruction of the Republican Guard armoured corps — the Hammurabi, Medina, and Tawakalna divisions — by the VII Corps wheeling east through the desert and pinning them against the Euphrates. By the morning of 27 February, the Tawakalna had been broken at 73 Easting and the Medina mauled at Medina Ridge. The Hammurabi, however, was still intact, retreating northward across the Hawr al-Hammar causeway towards Basra. VII Corps under Frederick Franks needed perhaps another twenty-four to thirty-six hours to close the trap. **It was at this precise moment, with the most experienced and most loyal armoured formation of the Iraqi state still mobile and still escaping, that Bush imposed the cessation of hostilities.**

The decision was made in the Oval Office on the afternoon of 27 February, after Colin Powell, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, telephoned Schwarzkopf at Riyadh and asked him whether he could end the war that night. Schwarzkopf, exhausted and not in possession of the latest VII Corps position reports, said he could. Powell took that answer to Bush. Bush, who had been watching CNN footage of the so-called Highway of Death — the column of incinerated Iraqi vehicles north of Kuwait City — and was being warned by Brent Scowcroft and Richard Haass that the political optics of continuing to bomb a retreating army would shred the coalition's moral standing, decided to stop the war at the next round-number hour. That was 0800 local on 28 February, the hundredth hour. Schwarzkopf was informed. He passed the order to his corps commanders. Franks, who had not been consulted, was instructed to halt VII Corps in place and to release no more rounds at retreating Iraqi forces.

The Hammurabi crossed the Hawr al-Hammar causeway on the morning of 28 February, in some haste but in good order, and dispersed into the Basra region. With it crossed roughly seven hundred T-72 tanks, two thousand armoured personnel carriers, and most of the surviving leadership cadre of the Iraqi Republican Guard. **It was these forces, and not the conscript army that had been destroyed in Kuwait, that constituted the actual coercive apparatus of the Ba'athist state.** Saddam still had them. The question over the next seventy-two hours was whether he could use them.

The Shia uprising had begun in Basra on the afternoon of 1 March, two days into the ceasefire. By the morning of 2 March it had spread to Najaf, Karbala, Nasiriyah, Amara, and most of the southern provincial capitals. Within seventy-two hours the entire south was in revolt, and by the second week of March the Kurdish north had risen as well. Saddam's territorial authority, at the moment of the Safwan meeting, extended over Baghdad and the Sunni triangle. Nowhere else. The Republican Guard armour was sitting in Basra, intact but immobile. Its tanks could move. Its troops could fight. What it could not do, at the operational level required to suppress a province-wide insurrection across roads still cratered by Coalition air strikes, was concentrate force quickly across the south. For that it needed helicopters.

This was the question that Sultan Hashim asked at Safwan on the afternoon of 3 March. He framed it in the language of civilian administration. He did not mention the uprising, although the uprising had been front-page news in Riyadh for forty-eight hours and Schwarzkopf had been briefed on it. He asked for permission to fly Iraqi army helicopters across the Coalition-imposed no-fly zone south of the 32nd parallel, on the grounds that ground transport was impossible. Schwarzkopf, in his memoirs (*It Doesn't Take a Hero*, 1992, p. 488), records the exchange in these words: "I asked the Iraqi general if he had any final concerns. He requested permission to fly helicopters in Iraq to move officials around in the absence of paved roads. I said: that is acceptable to me. There will be no fixed-wing flights, only helicopters, and they must not be armed."

The phrase "armed" carried a precision that turned out to be operationally meaningless. The Mi-17s and Mi-24s that the Iraqi Republican Guard flew out of bases at Tallil and Jalibah over the next seventy-two hours were, in the technical sense, not armed: their door guns had been dismounted and their pylon rockets unloaded. They carried, instead, troops, fuel, ammunition, and forward observers. They redeployed Republican Guard infantry from Basra to Najaf in two hours rather than two days. They concentrated armour against Karbala by spotting for ground columns. They did, in other words, exactly what helicopters are for: they multiplied the operational mobility of a ground force whose ground mobility had been broken. **By 7 March, the Republican Guard had retaken Basra. By 16 March, Karbala. By 19 March, Najaf, where the Imam Ali shrine was shelled and where, according to the most conservative estimates, perhaps thirty thousand Iraqis were killed in five days. By the end of March the south was pacified. The uprising was over. Saddam was secure.**

The Bush administration's public position throughout this period was that it would not intervene in Iraqi internal affairs. The private position, which has been thoroughly documented by Kenneth Pollack and others, was that the administration was watching the helicopter flights with horror and could not work out how to stop them without appearing to repudiate the Safwan terms. On 13 March, Bush gave a press conference at which he was asked specifically about the gunships. He said the United States had warned Iraq not to use military aircraft. He did not say that the Coalition would shoot them down. The omission was deliberate. Brent Scowcroft, in his memoir with Bush (*A World Transformed*, 1998), is candid: "We had no authority under the Safwan terms to engage Iraqi helicopters not in direct contact with Coalition forces. That had been Norm's concession at the airstrip. We could not retract it without confessing that the war had ended badly." The Coalition watched the gunships fly and did not engage them.

The deeper question about Safwan is what it tells us about the architecture of war termination in the modern American military. Schwarzkopf was the theatre commander, in the sense that he commanded the theatre. He was not, however, a politically literate negotiator. He had been given a list of military demands and instructed to obtain them. He had not been given a map of the political topography of post-war Iraq. He had not been told that an uprising was likely. He had not been briefed on which Iraqi military capabilities mattered to the survival of the Ba'athist regime and which did not. He did not know that gunships were the single most strategically significant asset Saddam possessed against an internal rebellion. He was a professional soldier conducting what he believed to be a professional military negotiation, and he made a professional military concession because the alternative — saying no to a procedural request near the end of an otherwise satisfactory meeting — would have felt, to a tired man working through a checklist, like obstructionism.

The American national security apparatus had not failed to plan for this. It had failed even to imagine that it needed to plan for it. **The interagency process that should have produced a Safwan brief for Schwarzkopf — covering not just military terms but the post-war political configuration the Coalition wanted to encourage or prevent — had not run, because nobody at the Deputies Committee level had identified war termination as a discrete planning problem requiring its own document.** The State Department had not been involved in the drafting of Schwarzkopf's negotiating instructions. The CIA's Iraq analysts, who had been warning since 1 March that Saddam was about to use his remaining helicopters to crush the uprising, had not been asked. Powell had drafted the list himself, with input from Schwarzkopf's J-3 at CENTCOM, in a few hours on 2 March. It was a military list. It assumed that war termination was the inverse of war initiation: stop shooting, return prisoners, exchange maps, go home.

The Iraqi delegation knew better. Sultan Hashim was not freelancing when he asked the helicopter question. He had been instructed by Saddam personally to obtain that concession and no other. The Iraqi state, on the morning of 3 March 1991, was a regime that had lost a war abroad and was about to lose a country at home, and its negotiating posture was calibrated to the latter. It needed two things from Safwan: it needed the war to be definitively over, so that Coalition forces would not re-engage when the Republican Guard moved south, and it needed permission to move the Republican Guard south. It got both. The first was implicit in the ceasefire itself. The second was the helicopter clause.

The lesson Safwan taught the American military planning apparatus was learned, with some delay and incompletely. The post-2003 Iraq war was conducted with a Future of Iraq Project at the State Department that produced thirteen volumes of post-war planning, all of which were discarded by Donald Rumsfeld's Office of the Secretary of Defense, with consequences not unfamiliar. The pattern repeats. The American government is exceptionally good at the kinetic phase of war and exceptionally bad at war's conclusion, because it conceives of war termination as a return to a status quo ante rather than as an act of political construction. Safwan was the moment when this institutional weakness produced a concrete, dateable, attributable disaster: thirty thousand dead at Karbala, twelve more years of Saddam, the Kurdish refugee crisis in the mountains of Turkey, the no-fly zones, the sanctions regime, the inspection crisis, the 2003 invasion, and everything downstream of that.

It is sometimes said that Bush should not have stopped the war at one hundred hours. The harder, more accurate criticism is that he should not have stopped the war without a Safwan brief. He stopped the shooting before his apparatus had decided what the silence was supposed to accomplish. Schwarzkopf walked into the tent with a checklist and walked out having given Saddam an air force. The Republican Guard flew home. The Shia died. The peace, such as it was, was made.
