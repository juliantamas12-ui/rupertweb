# The Bond Vigilantes of 1994: How a Bunch of Traders Mugged Bill Clinton

In February 1994, the United States economy was, by every conventional measure, in good shape. Unemployment was falling. Inflation was running at 2.7 percent — well within any reasonable target. GDP growth in the previous quarter had come in at 7.5 percent annualised, the strongest print in nearly a decade. The federal budget deficit, while still large, was on a clearly downward trajectory thanks to the tax increases Clinton had pushed through Congress the previous summer. A finance minister in any other country looking at this dashboard would have been pouring a celebratory drink.

Then, on 4 February 1994, Alan Greenspan raised the federal funds rate by twenty-five basis points — from 3.0 to 3.25 percent — and the bond market lost its mind. Over the next nine months, the yield on the ten-year Treasury rose from 5.6 percent to 8.0 percent. The Lehman Aggregate Bond Index, which had never had a calendar-year loss in its existence, finished 1994 down 2.9 percent. Roughly $1.5 trillion in global bond market value evaporated. Orange County, California, went bankrupt in December after its treasurer Robert Citron lost $1.7 billion betting that rates would stay low. Kidder Peabody collapsed. Mexico, whose dollar-denominated tesobonos had been priced off the assumption of low US rates, blew up by Christmas. The Great Bond Massacre of 1994 was the largest fixed-income loss in history at the time, and it had been triggered by a quarter-point hike in a country where inflation was, by any honest reading, not a problem.

**What happened in 1994 was not a story about inflation. It was a story about who actually runs economic policy in a country that finances itself through capital markets — and the answer, it turns out, is not the elected government.** This is the episode that gave us the phrase "bond vigilantes," coined by the economist Ed Yardeni a decade earlier but only really earning its keep in 1994. James Carville, Clinton's strategist, said the line that everyone now remembers: *"I used to think that if there was reincarnation, I wanted to come back as the president or the pope. But now I want to come back as the bond market. You can intimidate everybody."* Carville said this in February 1993, before the Clinton administration had even passed its first budget. By February 1994 he had been proven right in a way nobody, including him, had quite expected.

The mechanics deserve attention because they are still operative. Clinton had come into office in January 1993 promising a public investment programme — infrastructure, training, technology — funded partly by deficit spending. Robert Rubin, then chairing the National Economic Council, and Lloyd Bentsen at Treasury talked him out of it within weeks. Their argument was not that the investment was unwise on the merits. It was that the bond market would never let him do it. If long rates rose by even a hundred basis points in response to perceived fiscal indiscipline, the resulting drag on mortgages, business investment, and consumer credit would dwarf any direct economic benefit from the spending. The recovery — which was real, if slow — would die. Clinton, by his own later account, sat in the Roosevelt Room and asked: *"You mean to tell me that the success of my programme and my re-election hinges on the Federal Reserve and a bunch of fucking bond traders?"* Bentsen confirmed that this was exactly what he meant.

The 1994 sell-off proved this was not paranoia. Greenspan had signalled in late 1993 that the Fed would begin to normalise rates — they were still at recession-emergency levels three years into a recovery — and the bond market, having gorged itself on long-duration paper at low yields, panicked at the prospect of being caught holding the bag. The selling fed on itself. Mortgage hedgers, who had bought ten-year Treasuries to offset prepayment risk on their MBS books, were suddenly facing the opposite problem: rising rates meant fewer prepayments, longer duration, and the need to sell more Treasuries to rebalance. This is what fixed-income desks call a convexity event, and in 1994 it was the largest one anybody had seen. Treasuries were being sold not because anyone had a view on inflation but because traders' models told them they had to be sold, which made other traders' models tell them the same thing.

The political consequence was that the Clinton administration spent its remaining seven years governing as the most fiscally orthodox Democratic White House since Grover Cleveland. The 1993 budget had raised the top marginal rate; every subsequent fiscal move was about deficit reduction. By 1998 the federal budget was in surplus for the first time in thirty years. Rubin, who became Treasury Secretary in 1995, made a doctrine of it: the *"strong dollar policy,"* which was less a policy about dollar strength than a discipline that the United States would not — could not — finance itself through inflation, currency depreciation, or fiscal surprises. The bond market had won, and the prize was a permanent veto over anything that looked like fiscal expansion not pre-cleared with Wall Street.

**This is the part of the 1994 story that economists prefer not to dwell on.** The conventional account treats the bond market's discipline as benign — a check on government overreach, a signal that helped force responsible policy. There is some truth to this. The 1990s boom would not have happened without the long-rate decline that followed Clinton's deficit reduction. But the same logic also explains why every elected government in the developed world spent the next thirty years unable to fund significant public investment without first making peace with markets that had no electoral mandate and no theory of the public good beyond the present value of their book. The bond market did not care about American infrastructure, or training, or whether the median household was actually getting richer. It cared about duration risk and inflation expectations. These are not the same thing as a national interest, but in 1994 they became, effectively, a constitutional constraint on one.

The vigilantes have had a strange afterlife. They were declared dead at least three times — after the dot-com bust, after 2008, after Covid — and each time they came back, most recently in October 2022 when they ate Liz Truss for breakfast in the gilt market over a single mini-budget. The Truss episode was 1994 compressed into seventy-two hours: a government that had not consulted the bond market about its fiscal plans, a market that decided it had not been asked, and a prime minister who lasted forty-nine days. The mechanism was identical. The lesson was identical. The only thing that had changed was the speed at which the discipline could now be administered.

What 1994 settled, and what Truss confirmed, is that in a country financed by sovereign debt held mostly by private institutions, the median voter does not set the upper bound on fiscal policy. The marginal bond holder does. *Carville was right. He just underestimated how long the reincarnation would last.*
