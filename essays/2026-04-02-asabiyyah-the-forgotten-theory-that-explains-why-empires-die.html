Asabiyyah: The Forgotten Theory That Explains Why Empires Die

Asabiyyah: The Forgotten Theory That Explains Why Empires Die

Ibn Khaldun (1332-1406) was a North African jurist, historian, and diplomat who wrote the Muqaddimah — an introduction to his universal history that turned out to be one of the most original works of social philosophy ever produced. He was writing in Arabic, in Tunis and Cairo, during the twilight of Islamic civilization's golden age, watching dynasty after dynasty collapse around him. Out of that observation came a theory.

The core concept is asabiyyah — usually translated as "group feeling," "social cohesion," or "tribal solidarity." It is the glue that holds a people together: the shared purpose, shared identity, and willingness to fight and sacrifice for the collective. Ibn Khaldun argued that it was the primary engine of political power. Armies did not conquer because they had better weapons. Empires did not hold together because their constitutions were well-designed. They rose and fell on the strength or weakness of asabiyyah.

Here is the cycle he described, and it is almost mechanical:

A rough, hungry people on the periphery — desert nomads, mountain clans, border communities — possess fierce asabiyyah. They have nothing, so they hold tight to each other. They conquer an established, weakened civilization. Victory brings wealth. The conquerors settle in cities, adopt luxury, specialize in trade. They start hiring mercenaries instead of fighting themselves. The rulers distance themselves from their people, surround themselves with courtiers and bureaucrats. Asabiyyah weakens. Within three to four generations — roughly a century — the dynasty is hollow. A new rough people on the periphery, possessing fresh and hard asabiyyah, repeats the conquest.

Ibn Khaldun saw this pattern across Islamic history from the Maghreb to Persia: Berbers, Arabs, Turks, Mongols, all following the same arc. He believed it was a near-universal law of civilization.

Now look at where we are.

The Western political establishment — center-left and center-right — governed for roughly six decades after 1945 on a particular set of shared assumptions: liberal democracy, free trade, technocratic governance, multilateral institutions. By the 2000s, this coalition had become, in Ibn Khaldun's terms, a settled dynasty. It had stopped fighting for legitimacy and started assuming it. It produced politicians who sounded like management consultants, institutions that served their own continuity, and a foreign policy class that exported its preferred order by force when persuasion failed. Iraq. Libya. Afghanistan.

Then came the rough people on the periphery. Not literal nomads, but people who felt peripheral — economically, culturally, geographically. They had asabiyyah of a different kind: resentment, solidarity in grievance, a willingness to tear things down. Trump, Bolsonaro, Orban, Le Pen, the Brexit coalition. They did not win because they had better policies. They won because they had stronger group feeling than the establishment they were fighting.

The establishment's response — this is fascism, these voters are deplorable, institutions must be protected — was exactly what Ibn Khaldun would have predicted from a dynasty in decline: an appeal to legitimacy from a group that had stopped earning it.

This is not a defense of any of these movements. Ibn Khaldun was descriptive, not prescriptive. The conquering nomads were not morally superior to the dynasties they toppled. They were just hungrier.

What makes the Muqaddimah remarkable is not just the theory — it is the method. Ibn Khaldun was the first person, as far as we can tell, to treat history as a social science: to look for structural causes rather than divine will, to identify patterns rather than merely record events, to theorize about why things happen rather than just chronicle that they did. He predated Montesquieu by three centuries, Vico by two, Marx and Weber by five. He was a 14th-century man doing 20th-century intellectual work.

And yet he is barely in the Western canon. He appears in footnotes. A few political scientists cite him. He is far less discussed than Machiavelli, who was writing a century later and covering narrower ground.

This is partly a product of which civilizations get to narrate their own intellectual history and which do not. Islamic scholarship of the medieval period — Averroes, Avicenna, Al-Ghazali, Ibn Khaldun — is treated as a relay race, passing Greek learning to Europe, rather than as a tradition of genuine intellectual development. That framing is a distortion with consequences. It means students reading about theories of civilization learn Spengler and Toynbee instead of the man who got there first and did it better.

The deeper lesson of asabiyyah is uncomfortable: solidarity matters more than argument. You can be right and still lose, if the people who are wrong are more tightly bound to each other than the people who are right. Democratic politics, scientific consensus, institutional legitimacy — none of these are self-enforcing. They rest on a bedrock of people who believe in them enough to defend them. When that belief hollows out, the form remains but the substance is gone.

Ibn Khaldun watched that happen, in real time, to the civilization he lived in. He did not panic. He tried to understand it. That is the right response.
