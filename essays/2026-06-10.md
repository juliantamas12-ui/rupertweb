# The Afternoon of 4 August 1914 in Downing Street When Grey's Ultimatum Was Designed to Expire Unanswered and Britain Chose the Balance of Power Over Belgium

On the afternoon of Tuesday, 4 August 1914, at approximately 14:00 London time, Sir Edward Grey, the Foreign Secretary, sat in his room at the Foreign Office and signed a telegram to Sir Edward Goschen, the British ambassador in Berlin, instructing him to require from the German Imperial Government, before midnight Berlin time, a "satisfactory answer" to the British demand that Germany respect the neutrality of Belgium. The telegram was sent at 14:05. Goschen received it at 17:30 Berlin time and presented the demand to the Reichskanzler Theobald von Bethmann Hollweg at 19:00. Midnight Berlin time was 23:00 London time. There was no satisfactory answer. At 23:00, Big Ben having just struck the hour, the British Empire was at war with the German Empire — by operation of an ultimatum that Grey had drafted in the certain knowledge that no German government, having already crossed into Belgium that morning, could conceivably accept its terms within nine hours.

The standard account treats the British entry into the First World War as a moral response to the German violation of Belgian neutrality. The 1839 Treaty of London, in which Britain stood as one of five guarantors of Belgian independence, is produced as the legal instrument; the German invasion of Belgium at 08:02 that morning, at Gemmenich, is produced as the casus belli; the British declaration of war at 23:00 is produced as the necessary and honourable consequence. Asquith said as much in the Commons on 6 August. Grey said as much in his Twenty-Five Years. Generations of British schoolchildren have been taught it. **It is false in a specific and important sense. The British government had decided, by the evening of 2 August — thirty-six hours before the German army crossed the Belgian frontier — that it would enter the war on the French side regardless of what happened to Belgium. The 1839 Treaty was not the reason for British entry. It was the parliamentary instrument by which Grey converted a decision already taken on balance-of-power grounds into a vote the Liberal Cabinet and the Liberal back benches would actually pass.**

To see this, one has to follow what happened in the Cabinet over the weekend of 1 to 3 August. Grey and Asquith had been working since 29 July on the assumption that Britain would have to enter the war if France were attacked, but they had no Cabinet majority for it. On the evening of Saturday, 1 August, the Cabinet split 11 to 9 against intervention. Lloyd George, then Chancellor, was the leader of the neutralist faction; John Burns, John Morley, Charles Trevelyan, and John Simon were prepared to resign if the government committed to France. The Liberal back benches, the Liberal press, the Trades Union Congress, the City — all were opposed. Grey told the French ambassador Paul Cambon on the afternoon of 1 August that he could give no commitment. Cambon, in the famous remark, asked whether the word "honour" still existed in the English language.

What changed the Cabinet was not Belgium. It was the Naval Memorandum and the German request for British neutrality. On the morning of Sunday, 2 August, Grey laid before the Cabinet the question of the Channel — specifically, whether Britain would commit to defending the northern French coast against the German fleet, on the basis of the 1912 Anglo-French naval agreement under which the French had concentrated their fleet in the Mediterranean on the understanding that the Royal Navy would cover the Channel. The Cabinet, after three hours of argument, voted in favour: 14 to 6. Burns resigned at lunchtime. Morley, Simon, and Trevelyan signalled they would resign by Monday morning. Lloyd George, crucially, did not. **This was the decisive vote. It committed Britain to a naval war with Germany before Belgium had been mentioned in Cabinet at all that day.** The German invasion of Luxembourg, which had taken place at 04:00 that morning, was discussed only in passing. The British declaration of naval support for France was communicated to Cambon at 14:30 on Sunday afternoon. The German army did not cross the Belgian frontier until 36 hours later.

Belgium entered the Cabinet's deliberations on the afternoon of Sunday, 2 August, only after the naval commitment had been agreed, and only in the specific form of Grey's question: would the Cabinet treat a German violation of Belgian neutrality as a casus belli, even one not contested by the Belgians themselves? The answer, after further argument, was a conditional yes — yes if the Belgians resisted, no if they acquiesced. This was the formula that Grey took into the Commons on the afternoon of Monday, 3 August, in the speech that history records as his finest and that is, on close reading, a careful exercise in the construction of a parliamentary majority for a decision already taken. Grey opened with British obligations to France under the entente. He opened with the naval agreement. He spent perhaps two-thirds of the speech on the moral and strategic case for supporting France in a war that had already begun on the Continent. Belgium was introduced in the last third, when the German ultimatum to Brussels — delivered at 19:00 on Sunday evening, demanding free passage for German troops — was already in the newspapers and the Belgian refusal of it was confirmed. **Grey did not move from France to Belgium because Belgium was the deeper reason. He moved from France to Belgium because Belgium was the reason on which he could secure a Liberal majority.**

The Cabinet of 3 August, meeting after Grey's speech, confirmed the decision. Morley, Simon, and Trevelyan resigned that evening; only Morley made his resignation public on the day. The German army crossed the frontier at Gemmenich at 08:02 on Tuesday, 4 August. The British ultimatum was drafted between 09:30 and 11:00. By the time Goschen presented it in Berlin, the German First Army was twenty miles inside Belgium and the Liège forts were under bombardment. The notion that Bethmann Hollweg, having gambled the German campaign plan on a thirty-eight-day march to Paris through Belgium, could now withdraw — within nine hours, on the demand of a Foreign Secretary in London — was operationally and politically inconceivable. Grey knew this. The ultimatum was a notice of declaration, not a request for compliance.

What does it matter, eighty-eight years later, whether Britain went to war for Belgium or for the balance of power? It matters because the two reasons imply different histories. If Britain went to war for Belgium, the war was a moral war, fought for the sanctity of small nations and the rule of treaties; the Versailles settlement was a continuation of that moral logic by other means; the failure of that settlement is a failure of will or of luck rather than of conception. If Britain went to war for the balance of power — to prevent a single Continental power from controlling the Channel ports and the Belgian coast, which had been the unchanging axis of British foreign policy since the seventeenth century — then the war was a conventional balance-of-power conflict that happened to be fought on a scale the political economy of 1914 could not sustain, and Versailles was the failure of a great power that had won a war it could not afford. **The Belgian framing concealed the strategic continuity. It made 1914 look like a moral departure from nineteenth-century statecraft when it was, in fact, the most consequential expression of that statecraft in British history.**

This concealment had a long downstream cost. It produced, in the 1930s, a British political class that could not honestly admit to its own population what its strategic interests had been in 1914, and which therefore could not coherently explain why those interests required rearmament against Germany again after 1933. The Belgian formula had worked too well in 1914. It had to be reproduced in 1939 — Poland in place of Belgium, the same parliamentary logic of converting a balance-of-power calculation into a moral cause that the Commons would vote for. By 1939 the concealment was producing diminishing returns. Chamberlain's guarantee to Poland in March 1939 was a balance-of-power commitment dressed in moral clothing; it was not believed by Hitler, who correctly judged that the British Cabinet had no operational means of enforcing it, and it was not understood by the British public, who had been told for a generation that 1914 had been about treaties and small nations rather than about the prevention of German hegemony on the Continent.

The lesson, if there is one, is narrow. **A great power cannot indefinitely conceal its real reasons for going to war from its own electorate without eventually losing the capacity to make its own strategy intelligible.** Grey's 3 August speech was a tactical masterpiece. It was also the beginning of a British strategic culture that would spend the next quarter-century failing to explain to itself what it was for. The Liberal Cabinet had wanted to enter the war on balance-of-power grounds. It had felt unable to do so honestly. It had reached for Belgium. The price of that reach was paid not in 1914, but in 1938 and 1939, when the same Cabinet logic was attempted again with weaker materials. By then the German Foreign Office, reading Grey's memoirs, had drawn the obvious conclusion: that British ultimata were parliamentary instruments rather than strategic ones, and could be discounted accordingly. They were wrong about that, in the end. But they were not wrong because Grey had told the truth in 1914. They were wrong because, after Dunkirk, Britain found a Prime Minister who was willing to.
