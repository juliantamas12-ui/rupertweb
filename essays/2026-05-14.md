# The Forgery That Killed the First Labour Government: The Zinoviev Letter

On 25 October 1924, four days before a British general election, the *Daily Mail* led with a banner that read: **CIVIL WAR PLOT BY SOCIALISTS' MASTERS**. Below it, in full, was a letter purportedly written by Grigory Zinoviev, head of the Communist International, to the Central Committee of the Communist Party of Great Britain, instructing them on how to use the British Labour government to advance world revolution. It urged the formation of cells in the armed forces, the radicalisation of the working class, and the exploitation of the Anglo-Soviet treaty then being ratified by Ramsay MacDonald's minority government. It was dated 15 September. It was signed in facsimile. It was a complete fabrication.

Four days later Labour lost ninety seats. The Conservatives took 419. MacDonald's nine-month government — the first Labour administration in British history — was destroyed. The conventional reading is that a foolish electorate, panicked by a press lord's stunt, threw out a competent reformist government on the strength of a letter that turned out to be fake. **This reading is wrong in almost every detail.** The electorate did not move much. The Liberals collapsed, and their voters went overwhelmingly to the Tories. The letter probably shifted, at most, a handful of marginals. What it actually did was something more interesting and far more consequential: it taught the British state that it could destroy a Labour government from inside its own intelligence apparatus, and it taught the Labour Party that the British state would, given the chance, do exactly that.

The forgery itself was crude. The text contained Russian idioms transliterated into English in ways no native speaker would produce. The signature was wrong: Zinoviev signed himself "Zinoviev" in Latin script on official Comintern correspondence, never in the form used on the letter. The Comintern's internal numbering system was botched. The Soviet archives, when they opened in 1991, contained no record of the letter — and the Comintern was, if nothing else, a fanatical archivist. We now know, from the 1999 report commissioned by Robin Cook from the chief historian of the Foreign Office, Gill Bennett, that the letter was almost certainly forged by a White Russian émigré named Ivan Pokrovsky working with the Berlin station of British intelligence, that it was passed to the Conservative Central Office through cut-outs in the SIS, and that **at least four senior figures in MI6 and the Foreign Office knew it was a forgery and chose to let it run**.

That is the part that matters. The letter was the instrument. The conspiracy was the willingness of a permanent state apparatus to deploy a known fake against an elected government of its own country. Bennett's report, which the Blair government quietly buried after publication, names names. Major Joseph Ball, head of B Branch at MI5, who later moved to Conservative Central Office and ran the party's intelligence operation against the left for the next two decades. Desmond Morton, the SIS officer who ran the émigré networks and who would later become Churchill's personal intelligence adviser. Vernon Kell, the head of MI5. Stewart Menzies, then a rising SIS officer, later "C" through the Second World War. The leak to the *Daily Mail* was authorised at a level senior enough that, as Bennett puts it with academic understatement, "the question of whether the Prime Minister of the day was aware cannot be definitively answered". The Prime Minister of the day was the outgoing MacDonald himself. He found out from the morning papers.

Consider what this means about the British state in 1924. The Labour Party had been in office for nine months. It had recognised the Soviet Union, negotiated a trade treaty, and dropped the prosecution of the communist journalist J. R. Campbell for incitement to mutiny — the prosecution itself a confected provocation by the Director of Public Prosecutions. None of this amounted to revolution. MacDonald was, by the standards of the European left, a moderate verging on the timid. He was governing in a hung parliament with Liberal sufferance and could not have passed a radical measure if he had wanted to. **The intelligence services nevertheless treated his government as an enemy occupation.** They acted accordingly. They forged a letter, leaked it to the most hostile press in Europe, and brought down the government four days before a vote.

The lesson the Labour Party learned was not that the press was hostile or that the electorate was fickle. Labour already knew those things. The lesson was that the apparatus of the British state — its intelligence services, its senior civil service, the men who sat behind the green leather doors that did not change when ministers did — would, in extremis, intervene directly to remove a Labour government from office. This understanding shaped the next half-century of British politics. It is the unspoken context for the 1931 crisis, when MacDonald's second government was broken on the rocks of a "bankers' ramp" engineered partly through the same Bank of England networks that had cooperated with the intelligence services in 1924. It is the context for the security service operations against Harold Wilson in the late 1960s and mid-1970s, which Peter Wright described in *Spycatcher* and which the Cabinet Secretary Burke Trend was sent to investigate in 1974. It is the context for the moment in 1975 when, according to multiple sources including Wilson's press secretary Joe Haines, senior army officers war-gamed the seizure of Heathrow.

The Labour right has spent a hundred years denying any of this matters — that it is all paranoia, that the British state is neutral, that Westminster is a level playing field. The Labour left has spent the same century insisting that it does matter, that the playing field tilts, and that any serious government of the left must plan accordingly. Both positions are partly performances. But the documentary record, slowly extracted from the archives over the past thirty years, is unambiguous. **The British state, in 1924, decided that a Labour government was a national security threat, and acted on that decision through forgery, leak, and electoral sabotage.** No one was ever prosecuted. No one was even publicly named until 1999. The men responsible received knighthoods, pensions, and obituaries in *The Times*.

Zinoviev himself was shot in Lubyanka in 1936, denouncing himself in the first of the great Moscow show trials. He had never written the letter that destroyed the British left. He had never even seen it. The forgery that ended his political existence in Britain twelve years before his actual death is, in its way, the perfect emblem of the twentieth century: a fake document, planted by a real state, that produced a real political consequence on the strength of a fictional Russian threat. The British learned, in 1924, that you could rule by inventing your enemies. They have never quite forgotten the lesson.
