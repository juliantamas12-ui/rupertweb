The Saturday Morning at the Plaza That Broke Japan: 22 September 1985

On the morning of Saturday, 22 September 1985, the finance ministers and central bank governors of the United States, Japan, West Germany, France, and the United Kingdom arrived at the Plaza Hotel on Fifth Avenue in Manhattan, sat down in a conference room on the second floor, and in just under three hours produced a one-page communiqué that announced, in carefully neutral language, that the dollar was overvalued and that the five governments would coordinate market intervention to bring it down. The Japanese delegation was led by Finance Minister Noboru Takeshita, a man who had flown to New York via a circuitous route through California to avoid being photographed at Narita, and who was so determined to keep the meeting secret from the Japanese press that he checked into the Plaza under a false name and let it be reported in Tokyo that he was playing golf at home. By the close of trading on Monday, the yen had risen eight per cent against the dollar. Within twelve months it had nearly doubled in value. Within four years the Tokyo stock market and the Tokyo property market had inflated into the largest twin bubbles in the history of capitalism, and within five years both had collapsed, beginning a deflationary trap that, four decades later, Japan has still not fully escaped. The conventional reading is that the Plaza Accord was a successful piece of multilateral coordination that corrected a destabilising imbalance and that what happened to Japan afterwards was the fault of the Bank of Japan's monetary mismanagement. This reading is half right and entirely misleading. What happened at the Plaza was that James Baker, the new United States Treasury Secretary, presented the Japanese with a demand they could not refuse, and the long catastrophe of the Japanese economy after 1990 was the direct and predictable consequence of a decision Tokyo made under duress in Manhattan five years earlier.

The pressure had been building for two years. Reagan's first-term combination of tax cuts and military spending had blown the federal deficit out from $74 billion in 1980 to $212 billion in 1985. The Federal Reserve under Paul Volcker, fighting the inflation of the 1970s, had kept real interest rates at historically extraordinary levels. The result was a dollar that had risen by roughly fifty per cent in trade-weighted terms since 1980, an American manufacturing sector that was haemorrhaging jobs to imports, and a Congress that by the summer of 1985 had assembled a bipartisan majority for protectionist legislation of a kind not seen since Smoot-Hawley. The Danforth bill, which would have imposed a twenty-five per cent surcharge on Japanese imports, had cleared the Senate Finance Committee in July. The Gephardt amendment, which would have mandated automatic retaliatory tariffs against any country running a large bilateral surplus with the United States, was gaining ground in the House. Baker, who had moved from the White House to Treasury in February 1985 and who understood American politics far better than his predecessor Donald Regan, recognised that unless the dollar fell quickly the Reagan administration would lose control of trade policy to a Congress in a protectionist mood. The Plaza Accord was, in its essence, a piece of American domestic politics dressed as international monetary coordination.

The Japanese knew this. The bureaucrats of the Ministry of Finance, the most capable economic civil service in the world at that point, had been warning for two years that the rising dollar was unsustainable and that a correction was coming. What they did not anticipate, and what Baker did not tell them in advance, was the scale of the move he intended to engineer. Internal Treasury memoranda released in the 1990s show that Baker's working target was a dollar depreciation of between ten and fifteen per cent against the yen over twelve months. What he got was a depreciation of more than fifty per cent in eighteen months. The intervention itself was small — roughly $10 billion of coordinated central bank selling in the weeks after the announcement — but the announcement effect was overwhelming. Currency traders understood that the world's five most important monetary authorities had publicly committed themselves to a direction, and they front-ran the policy on a scale that no government had imagined possible. The yen, which had stood at 240 to the dollar in August 1985, was at 200 by December, 160 by the following summer, and 120 by the end of 1987. Japanese exporters, whose entire cost base was priced in a currency that had effectively doubled, faced a crisis of survival.

The Bank of Japan responded as any central bank would. It cut interest rates aggressively to ease the burden on exporters and to stimulate domestic demand to compensate for the collapsing external sector. The discount rate fell from five per cent in January 1986 to two and a half per cent by February 1987, the lowest level in Japanese postwar history. The cuts were not, in themselves, unusual. What was unusual was that the Japanese government, under American pressure that did not relent after the Plaza, was simultaneously committed not to use fiscal policy to absorb the shock. The Maekawa Report of April 1986, commissioned by Prime Minister Nakasone explicitly in response to American complaints, instructed the Japanese economy to shift from export-led to domestic-demand-led growth — but it did so by recommending deregulation and consumption rather than the large public-works programme that the structure of the Japanese economy actually required. The result was that the entire weight of the post-Plaza adjustment fell on monetary policy. Cheap money flooded into an economy whose real-side investment opportunities were already saturated. It went into land. It went into the stock market. By the end of 1989 the land beneath the Imperial Palace in Tokyo was, on conventional valuation methods, worth more than the entire state of California. The Nikkei stood at 38,915.

When Yasushi Mieno took over the Bank of Japan in December 1989 and immediately began raising rates to prick the bubble, he found that the bubble did not deflate gradually. It collapsed. The Nikkei has never recovered to its 1989 peak — thirty-six years and counting — and Japanese commercial property prices remain, in 2026, at roughly forty per cent of their 1991 high in nominal terms and substantially less in real terms. The lost decade became the lost two decades and then the lost three. The deflation that set in after 1995 destroyed the balance sheets of the major banks, hollowed out corporate investment, and produced the slow demographic and economic decline that has defined Japanese politics ever since. Hashimoto, Obuchi, Koizumi, Abe — every Prime Minister of substance from 1996 onward defined himself in relation to a problem that had been set in motion by Takeshita's signature at the Plaza.

None of this was inevitable. Had the Japanese refused at the Plaza — had Takeshita walked out, as he was urged to do by some of his own officials — the Reagan administration would have faced a domestic political crisis it could not easily have managed, and the eventual currency adjustment would have been smaller, slower, and more absorbable. The Japanese did not walk out because they were operating under the strategic assumptions of 1985: that Japan's security was an American gift, that the alliance was non-negotiable, that economic friction with Washington had to be defused at almost any cost lest it spill into the military relationship. These assumptions were not absurd in the context of the late Cold War. They produced, however, a willingness to absorb economic damage of a kind that no European ally would have tolerated. When Helmut Kohl was asked by Baker to make equivalent concessions on the Deutschmark, he made vague noises and quietly did nothing. The Bundesbank intervened minimally and let the dollar fall against the mark by amounts it considered acceptable rather than amounts Washington requested. The Germans understood, as the Japanese did not, that an American Treasury Secretary in a domestic political bind could be politely defied.

The longer lesson is not about exchange rates. It is about what happens when a country accepts that its monetary sovereignty is a chip to be played in the management of its alliance relationships. Japan in 1985 was the second largest economy on earth and the largest creditor of the United States. It had, on any objective measure, more bargaining power in the global monetary system than any state other than the issuer of the reserve currency itself. It chose not to use that power because it had not learned to think of itself as a great power, and it had not learned because the United States had spent forty years carefully arranging that it should not. The Plaza Accord was not an accident of bad luck or central bank incompetence. It was the moment when the structural subordination of Japan to the United States produced, for the first time, an economic cost large enough to define a generation. The men who signed the communiqué did not know what they were signing. The men who wrote it did.
