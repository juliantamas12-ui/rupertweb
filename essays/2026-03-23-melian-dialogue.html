The Melian Dialogue and the Lie at the Heart of International Order

In the summer of 416 BC, Athens sent a fleet to the small island of Melos and delivered what may be the most honest speech in the history of diplomacy. The Melians had stayed neutral in the Peloponnesian War. Athens wanted tribute. The Melians appealed to justice. The Athenian envoys replied, in Thucydides' rendering: "The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must." When the Melians refused to submit, Athens besieged the island, killed every man of military age, and sold the women and children into slavery.

The episode takes up only a few pages of Thucydides' History of the Peloponnesian War, but those pages contain an argument that has never been resolved — only papered over. Every United Nations resolution, every international law textbook, every invocation of human rights in foreign policy is, in some sense, a response to the Athenians on Melos. And the response has never been quite adequate.

The Melians made three arguments. First, that the gods would favor the just side. Second, that Sparta would come to their aid. Third, that justice itself was a real force in the world that Athens would eventually have to reckon with. All three were wrong. Sparta did not come. The gods, if they existed, were indifferent. And Athens suffered no immediate punishment for the massacre — it launched its Sicilian Expedition the following year, an overreach that eventually destroyed Athenian power, but the connection between the destruction of Melos and Athenian decline is at best oblique.

The Athenians' argument was simpler and more durable: justice is a concept that only operates between equals. Between unequals, the strong impose terms and the weak accept them. They were not being cruel for the sake of cruelty — they were being honest. Athens needed Melos for strategic reasons, and no appeal to abstract principle would change that calculus. To pretend otherwise, they suggested, would be to waste everyone's time.

This is what E.H. Carr called the "realist" tradition in international relations, and his 1939 book The Twenty Years' Crisis remains the most devastating critique of liberal internationalism ever written. Carr's argument, developed in the shadow of the League of Nations' failure, was that utopian thinking about international law and collective security was not merely naive — it was actively dangerous. It encouraged weaker powers to believe in protections that didn't exist, and it gave stronger powers a vocabulary of morality that disguised power interests as humanitarian ones. The result was a world that combined the worst of both: neither honest power politics nor genuine restraint, but a theater of norms that nobody actually followed when it mattered.

This pattern has replicated itself with uncomfortable regularity. The United Nations Charter prohibits aggressive war. It has not prevented aggressive war. The Geneva Conventions prohibit targeting civilians. Civilians are targeted. The International Criminal Court has jurisdiction over war crimes. It has convicted a handful of warlords from countries too weak to resist its reach, while the leaders of great powers operate with effective immunity. When the United States invaded Iraq in 2003 — a war that had no UN authorization and was based on fabricated intelligence — the international order did not stop it. When Russia annexed Crimea in 2014, international law did not stop it. When it invaded Ukraine in 2022, the legal condemnations were immediate, voluminous, and largely beside the point on the battlefield.

None of this means that international law is worthless. Hans Morgenthau, who built the academic discipline of international relations on a Thucydidean foundation, was careful to note that norms and institutions do constrain behavior at the margins — they raise costs, they shape expectations, they provide frameworks for negotiation. States comply with international law when compliance serves their interests, and the existence of the law can shift what those interests look like. This is not nothing. But it is a long way from the picture presented in law schools and foreign ministries, where the international order is treated as a genuine legal system rather than an elaborate gentleman's agreement backed by no sovereign authority.

What makes the Melian Dialogue so uncomfortable is that Thucydides clearly does not endorse the Athenian argument, even as he presents it with devastating clarity. The History is structured to show that Athens's moral collapse — the abandonment of the moderation that had made it great — preceded and contributed to its military collapse. The destruction of Melos is not presented as a triumph. It is presented as a symptom. A state that can speak the way the Athenians spoke at Melos is a state that has begun to believe its own power is without limit. That belief is always, eventually, wrong.

This is the part that both hardline realists and liberal internationalists tend to miss. The realists are right that power is the fundamental currency of international politics and that moral language is often a disguise for power interests. But they sometimes draw from this the conclusion that moral considerations are simply irrelevant to statecraft, which is incorrect. Reputation, legitimacy, and the willingness of other states to cooperate are all real strategic assets. Athens's treatment of Melos alienated potential allies and hardened the resolve of enemies throughout the Greek world. Moral considerations matter — just not in the way that the Melians thought they did. They matter instrumentally, not absolutely.

The honest position, then, is neither the Melians' faith in abstract justice nor the Athenians' contempt for it. It is something more uncomfortable: a recognition that international norms are real but fragile, that they constrain behavior at the margins but cannot substitute for power, and that the comfortable liberal story about an international rules-based order is a useful fiction that should be maintained while never being fully believed.

The Melians believed the fiction completely. That is why they are not here to tell their side of the story.
