The Lights Go Out: The Bronze Age Collapse and What It Teaches About Systemic Fragility

The Lights Go Out: The Bronze Age Collapse and What It Teaches About Systemic Fragility

Around 1200 BC, within the span of roughly fifty years, almost every major civilization in the Eastern Mediterranean ceased to exist. The Hittite Empire — which had fought Egypt to a standstill at Kadesh and negotiated the oldest known peace treaty in history — vanished so completely that its very existence was forgotten for three thousand years. Mycenaean Greece, the world of Agamemnon and the palace economies that supplied Homer's heroes, collapsed so thoroughly that literacy itself disappeared from Greece for four centuries. Ugarit, one of the most cosmopolitan port cities in the ancient world, was burned and never rebuilt. The palace of Pylos was destroyed while its scribes were apparently still writing warning tablets about a threat from the sea. Cyprus, Egypt, and the Levant were hit. Trading networks that had connected tin mines in Afghanistan to copper works in Cyprus to gold in Egypt — a genuinely global economy in miniature — stopped functioning almost overnight.

This is arguably the single greatest civilizational collapse in human history, larger in scope than Rome's fall, and it happened so fast and so completely that historians spent the better part of the twentieth century arguing about who did it. The answer, it now appears, is that nobody did it — at least not in any simple causal sense. And that is the lesson worth sitting with.

For decades, the standard explanation pointed to the "Sea Peoples," mysterious raiders who appear in Egyptian reliefs attacking from the north. Pharaoh Ramesses III claimed to have defeated them around 1177 BC in a famous naval battle at the Nile delta. The Sea Peoples were convenient villains — external, violent, disruptive. But this explanation has always been unsatisfying. The Egyptians repelled them. Yet Egypt still contracted massively, never recovering its New Kingdom power. And the Sea Peoples were not the invaders; they were largely refugees — displaced populations fleeing the same collapse they were supposedly causing, much like the people crossing the Mediterranean today are fleeing war rather than conducting it.

The scholar Eric Cline, in his 2014 book 1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed, makes the more convincing argument: the Bronze Age Collapse was a systems collapse, not a military defeat. The Late Bronze Age world had become extraordinarily interconnected and consequently extraordinarily fragile. Palace economies across the region depended on continuous flows of rare materials — tin from far Afghanistan, copper from Cyprus, grain from Egypt, luxury goods from everywhere. None of these states was self-sufficient; they were nodes in a network. When multiple stresses hit simultaneously — drought (confirmed by palaeoclimatic data), crop failures, internal rebellions, disruption of trade routes, earthquakes (several major cities show destruction layers consistent with seismic events) — the network could not absorb the shock. One failure cascaded into the next. The Hittites could no longer receive Egyptian grain; the Mycenaeans could no longer get copper and tin; the craftsmen stopped working; the armies stopped being paid; the kingdoms lost legitimacy; the palace bureaucracies, which had maintained writing and record-keeping, dissolved. And when the palaces went, so did literacy.

What makes this historically arresting is that it was not caused by stupidity, weakness, or decadence in any obvious moral sense. These were sophisticated states with professional armies, extensive diplomatic correspondence, complex economies. The Ugaritic king received a letter from Cyprus warning of famine and ships sighted offshore. He wrote back that his troops were away, his ships deployed elsewhere, and he had no grain to send. He was, by any reasonable measure, managing a crisis thoughtfully. It did not matter. The letter was found undelivered in a kiln at Ugarit, fired by the same conflagration that ended the city.

The lesson that tends to go unlearned is this: complexity is not strength. The more interconnected and specialized a system becomes, the more efficient it is under normal conditions and the more catastrophically it fails under abnormal ones. The Bronze Age world had achieved something genuinely remarkable — a trading network spanning thousands of miles, written diplomacy, international law of a primitive kind, peaceful exchange between kingdoms of very different cultures. It also created a fragility so profound that no single king, however competent, could prevent collapse when the right combination of stresses arrived simultaneously.

This is not an abstract lesson. Modern supply chains are the Bronze Age Phoenician network at planetary scale. The semiconductor shortage triggered by COVID-19 — a single disruption that cascaded into automotive shortfalls, consumer electronics delays, and military procurement problems — is a small-scale demonstration of the same dynamic. The difference is that after a modern supply chain shock, the knowledge is retained, the cities are not burned, and recovery is measured in months. After 1200 BC, the lights went out for centuries. The Greeks who eventually built Athens had no idea what the Mycenaeans were. They thought the enormous stones of Mycenae's walls must have been built by giants.

That forgetting — the complete rupture of continuity — is perhaps the most unsettling aspect of the collapse. We assume knowledge accumulates, that civilization is a ratchet that only turns one way. The Bronze Age says otherwise. Under the right conditions, you can lose everything, including the memory that you had it.
