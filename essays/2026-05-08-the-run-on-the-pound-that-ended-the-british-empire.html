The Run on the Pound That Ended the British Empire: Eisenhower, Suez, and the Five Days in November 1956

The conventional account of the Suez Crisis is that Britain, France, and Israel colluded to seize the Suez Canal in November 1956, that the United States and the Soviet Union both opposed them, and that the resulting humiliation marked the end of Britain as a great power. This is true in the way that saying "the Titanic hit an iceberg" is true. It identifies the proximate cause and ignores the mechanism. The mechanism was a deliberate, surgical run on sterling engineered by the Eisenhower administration over five days in early November 1956, the most precise use of financial coercion by one ally against another in the twentieth century, and the moment at which the United States demonstrated to itself, to Britain, and to anyone else paying attention that the dollar was now the only currency that mattered.

The setup is well known. Nasser nationalised the canal on 26 July 1956. Eden, Mollet, and Ben-Gurion concocted the Sèvres Protocol in late October: Israel would invade Sinai, Britain and France would issue an ultimatum demanding both sides withdraw from the canal, and when Egypt refused — as Egypt obviously would — the two European powers would intervene to "protect" the waterway. Israeli paratroopers dropped at the Mitla Pass on 29 October. The Anglo-French ultimatum went out on 30 October. British and French aircraft began bombing Egyptian airfields on 31 October. Paratroopers landed at Port Said on 5 November. Seaborne forces went ashore on 6 November.

What is less well known, and what actually decided the crisis, is what was happening simultaneously in the foreign exchange markets and in the offices of the United States Treasury.

Britain in 1956 had a problem the war had bequeathed and Bretton Woods had institutionalised. The pound was a reserve currency, pegged at $2.80, backed by reserves of gold and dollars at the Bank of England that totalled roughly $2.4 billion. The sterling area — the Commonwealth and various former dependencies — held perhaps £4 billion of sterling balances, claims on those reserves. The ratio was already perilous. Any sustained outflow could drain the reserves below the conventional minimum of $2 billion, at which point Britain would face a choice between devaluing the pound or imposing exchange controls so severe they would terminate sterling's role as an international currency. The Treasury knew this. The Bank of England knew this. The Americans knew this best of all because they had designed the system that produced the vulnerability.

The run began on 30 October, the day of the ultimatum. By 2 November, Britain had lost $50 million in reserves. By 5 November, the figure was $85 million. By the end of the week the Bank of England had lost roughly $279 million — more than ten percent of its total reserves in five trading days, a pace that, if sustained, would empty the vaults inside three weeks. Harold Macmillan, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, told the cabinet on 6 November — the same day British forces were landing at Port Said — that the run was now uncontrollable and that he could not guarantee sterling for more than another fortnight without external help.

The external help, of course, was American. The IMF held $1.3 billion that Britain was technically entitled to draw upon. The Export-Import Bank could provide a further $500 million. Together, this would have been enough to defend the pound through the crisis. Both institutions required, in practice, the consent of the United States. And here is the part of the story that most accounts skim over: the United States Treasury, on the explicit instructions of Eisenhower and Secretary George Humphrey, was actively selling sterling. Not refusing to support it. Selling it. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York, acting as the Treasury's market agent, dumped sterling holdings into the London market on 30 October, 31 October, and 1 November, deepening the run that British speculators and panicked sterling-area depositors had begun. The IMF drawing was blocked. The Ex-Im Bank credit was blocked. Humphrey told Macmillan, in a phone call on 6 November that Macmillan recorded in his diary in unusually clipped prose, that there would be no American assistance of any kind until Britain accepted a ceasefire in Egypt and a timetable for withdrawal.

The cabinet meeting that afternoon is the moment the British Empire ended, and it ended over a balance sheet. Macmillan, who had been the most enthusiastic supporter of the invasion ten days earlier, was now the most insistent voice for immediate withdrawal — "first in, first out", as a colleague bitterly described his pivot. Eden, who understood neither macroeconomics nor the disposition of his own Chancellor, was outvoted. The ceasefire was announced at midnight on 6 November, less than thirty-six hours after British troops had landed. The withdrawal followed in December. The IMF drawing was permitted on 10 December, the day the British government formally committed to a complete evacuation. The pound stabilised within a week.

The financial mechanics matter because they reveal the shape of the new world order more clearly than any speech or treaty. The American refusal at the United Nations was theatre; the votes there were not binding and Britain held a veto in the Security Council. The Soviet threat to launch missiles at London, made by Bulganin on 5 November, was bluff and was treated as such by everyone serious. The thing that ended the operation was not diplomatic isolation or military difficulty — the British and French had achieved every operational objective by the time the ceasefire was called. It was the discovery that an ally, in possession of the world's reserve currency, could destroy the British currency at will and was prepared to do so. Macmillan's pivot was rational because the alternative was a sterling collapse that would have triggered the breakup of the sterling area, the loss of imperial preference, and a domestic financial crisis comparable to 1931. He chose the loss of Suez over the loss of sterling. It was the correct choice.

The longer consequence is that every subsequent British government understood, in a way that French governments did not, that independent foreign policy required either alignment with American financial preferences or a degree of monetary autonomy Britain had given up at Bretton Woods and would not recover. De Gaulle drew the opposite lesson and spent the next decade cashing in French dollar holdings for gold to prove the point. The 1971 Nixon shock that ended Bretton Woods was, in part, a delayed consequence of Suez: it was the moment the United States accepted that the system it had used to discipline Britain in 1956 could be used against it, and chose to dismantle the system rather than be disciplined by it.

The Suez Crisis is usually taught as a moral fable about post-imperial overreach. It is more useful read as the founding case study in the weaponisation of reserve currency status — a playbook the United States has run, with variations, against Iran in 1979, against the Soviet Union in the 1980s, against Russia in 2022. The instrument is the same. The first time it was used, it was used against the British, and they have not been the same country since.
