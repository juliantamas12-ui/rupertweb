The Iron Law of the Ruling Class: Pareto, Mosca, and Why Democracies Don't Rule Themselves

The Iron Law of the Ruling Class: Pareto, Mosca, and Why Democracies Don't Rule Themselves

Every few years, somewhere in the Western world, a politician rises on a wave of anti-establishment fury. They promise to give power back to the people. They win. And within a term or two, they are the establishment — governing much as their predecessors did, serving many of the same interests, and being replaced by the next wave of anti-establishment fury. The cycle is so reliable it barely warrants comment.

Gaetano Mosca noticed this pattern before most democracies were old enough to betray their promises. Writing in 1896 in Elementi di Scienza Politica, the Italian political theorist proposed what he called the "political class" — a minority that always, in every society, organizes power and rules over the majority. Not because the minority is smarter or more virtuous, but because organization itself is power. A coherent minority can always defeat an incoherent majority. Fifty men acting in concert will beat five hundred acting independently every time.

Vilfredo Pareto — economist, sociologist, and one of history's more uncomfortable thinkers — refined and extended this into a general theory of social equilibrium. In his Treatise on General Sociology (1916), Pareto argued that history is essentially "a graveyard of aristocracies." Elites rise, consolidate, decay, and are replaced by new elites. He called this the circulation of elites, and it is one of the most useful and least fashionable ideas in all of political theory.

Pareto divided elites into two psychological types, borrowing from Machiavelli's bestiary: lions and foxes. Lions rule by force, tradition, and a willingness to use coercion. They are stable but inflexible. Foxes rule by cunning, manipulation, and the fabrication of consent. They are adaptable but corrupt. Healthy societies cycle between them. Societies that stop cycling — where one type ossifies into permanent power — become either tyrannies or decadent oligarchies, and eventually collapse.

The foxes of modern liberal democracy are not politicians. Politicians are, for the most part, performance. The real foxes are the permanent managerial and administrative class — what James Burnham, writing in 1941, called the "managerial elite": the senior bureaucrats, central bankers, regulatory officials, foundation directors, and think-tank strategists who set the parameters of policy regardless of which party wins an election. They do not need to win elections. They only need to persist. And they are very good at persisting.

This is not conspiracy. It is structure. When a government changes, thousands of civil servants remain. Regulatory frameworks remain. International treaty obligations remain. The personnel of the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the IMF — they remain. The new government inherits the architecture of its predecessors and, constrained by that architecture, governs more similarly than differently. Voters sense this without always being able to name it, which is why anti-establishment sentiment keeps rising even as establishment policy keeps continuing.

Pareto would have found nothing surprising in the last decade of Western politics. Donald Trump, Brexit, the gilets jaunes, Meloni, Milei — these are textbook Paretian lion moments: a reaction against an overly comfortable fox elite that had confused its own preferences with universal good sense. The lions charge in. They disturb things. Some norms genuinely break. But the institutional architecture — the legal structures, the bond markets, the permanent bureaucracy — absorbs and outlasts most of the disruption. The foxes reconstitute. The cycle continues.

Where Pareto becomes genuinely unsettling is in his treatment of ideology. He called political justifications "derivations" — post-hoc rationalizations that elites construct to legitimize their hold on power. Democracy, divine right, the vanguard of the proletariat, the efficiency of markets: these are, in Pareto's framing, the stories that ruling classes tell about themselves. The stories change when elites change. The structure of elite rule does not.

This is too cynical as a complete account of politics. Institutions do matter, law does constrain, and democratic elections do sometimes produce genuine shifts in power. But Pareto's framework captures something that civic idealism systematically misses: the will of the majority is always mediated by organized minorities, and those minorities always have interests of their own. The question is not whether there is a ruling class. The question is which ruling class, how it is composed, and how permeable it is to new entrants.

The healthiest political orders, historically, are not those that eliminate elite rule — no such order has ever existed — but those that maintain a genuine circulation: where old elites can be displaced, where new talent and new interests can rise, and where the concentration of power does not become hereditary. Venice's Great Council was, for centuries, a masterwork of managed circulation within a closed aristocracy. The Roman Republic's competitive cursus honorum served a similar function. The moment circulation stopped in both cases — when the aristocracy closed, when the Senate became a gerontocracy of dynastic families — decline followed.

Modern democracies are not immune to this. The growing hereditary character of elite reproduction — the same universities, the same neighborhoods, the same networks, generation after generation — suggests that circulation is slowing. When elites stop renewing themselves, they lose the vitality to govern effectively and the legitimacy to govern at all. The lions at the gate are always, in the end, a symptom of that failure.

Pareto was wrong about many things and repellent about some of them. His sociology was later embraced, selectively and dishonestly, by Mussolini's movement, which tells you something about how ideas can be kidnapped. But the core insight — that political power is always exercised by organized minorities, that ideology is partly mask, and that the health of a polity depends on how well it manages the circulation of those minorities — is not fascism. It is observation. And it remains one of the sharper tools available for looking at politics without flinching.
