# The Coup That Almost Wasn't: Why Operation Ajax Failed Before It Succeeded

The standard story of the 1953 Iranian coup is a story of American omnipotence. Mohammad Mosaddegh, the elected prime minister who nationalised Anglo-Iranian Oil, is overthrown by a CIA operation called TPAJAX, the Shah is restored, and a quarter-century of authoritarian rule begins that ends only when the 1979 revolution sweeps the whole apparatus into the sea. It is told as a clean operation — the founding myth of CIA covert action, the template for Guatemala the next year and Chile twenty years later, the original sin of American interference in the Middle East. Almost none of this is correct. The coup that actually happened in August 1953 was not Operation Ajax. It was a salvage job conducted by one man who had been ordered to leave the country and refused.

The real Operation Ajax, as authorised by Eisenhower and planned in Nicosia by Kermit "Kim" Roosevelt Jr. — Theodore's grandson, FDR's distant cousin — was set to execute on the night of 15-16 August 1953. The plan was straightforward. The Shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, would sign two royal decrees: one dismissing Mosaddegh, one appointing General Fazlollah Zahedi in his place. The decrees, called *firmans*, would be delivered to Mosaddegh's house by Colonel Nematollah Nassiri of the Imperial Guard, who would simultaneously arrest the prime minister. Pro-Shah army units would seize the radio station, the telegraph office, and the army headquarters. By dawn, Iran would have a new government and Anglo-Iranian Oil would have its concession back.

Almost everything that could go wrong did. **Mosaddegh's intelligence service had penetrated the conspiracy at least a week before it launched.** When Nassiri arrived at the prime minister's residence shortly before midnight on 15 August with the firman of dismissal, he was met not by a startled household but by loyalist troops who had been waiting for him. They arrested him on the spot. The pro-coup units that were supposed to seize Tehran's strategic points either never moved or were rounded up by loyalist officers who knew exactly where they would be heading. Mosaddegh went on the radio in the early hours of the 16th and announced that a royalist conspiracy against the constitution had been crushed. The Shah, on holiday at his Caspian Sea retreat at Ramsar, panicked, flew to Baghdad in his personal aircraft, and from there to Rome, where he checked into the Excelsior Hotel and told Western journalists he was probably abdicating.

In Washington and London, the operation was declared dead. Allen Dulles, the CIA director, was on holiday in Switzerland and could not be reached. The State Department began drafting cables to recognise Mosaddegh's continuing premiership. Most importantly, on 17 August, the CIA station in Tehran received an explicit cable from headquarters ordering Roosevelt to terminate the operation and exfiltrate. The British, who had pushed the whole adventure for two years to get their oil concession back, started looking for ways to blame the Americans. *The coup was over. It had failed in less than four hours.*

Roosevelt did not leave. This is the part of the story that gets buried under the mythology, because it is the part that most embarrasses the institutional CIA: their most famous victory was, at the decisive moment, an act of insubordination by one field officer who decided his own government was wrong. He stayed in his safe house in northern Tehran with two Iranian agents and roughly $1 million in unmarked banknotes, and he spent the next 72 hours doing two things. First, he leaked the dismissal firman — the genuine royal decree dismissing Mosaddegh, which Nassiri had been carrying when he was arrested — to friendly newspapers and to the United States embassy, which began circulating Persian-language flyers throughout Tehran. The legal point was thin but useful: the Shah had constitutionally the right to dismiss the prime minister, the firman was real, therefore Mosaddegh was now technically in power illegally. Second, and more decisively, he hired a mob.

The accounts of who exactly was paid, and how much, vary, but the basic structure is clear. Roosevelt had two principal assets in the south Tehran underworld — Shaban "the Brainless" Jafari, a thug and weightlifter who ran a bazaar protection racket, and the Rashidian brothers, three Anglophile merchants who had been on the British payroll for years. On the morning of 19 August, after three days of escalating disorder that the CIA had been quietly funding, a crowd of perhaps several thousand emerged from the southern bazaars chanting for the Shah, beating up suspected Tudeh (communist) party members, and marching on the centre of the city. **Within hours the crowd had become an army, because army units who had been wavering now defected to the apparent winning side.** This is the crucial mechanism of all coups, and the one that planners consistently fail to model: *officers do not pick the winning side because they have been bribed; they pick the winning side because they want to be on it.* Roosevelt's $1 million did not buy a coup. It bought the *appearance* of a coup, which then bought the actual coup for free.

By late afternoon on 19 August, loyalist units had been overrun, Mosaddegh's residence was being shelled by tanks that had switched sides during lunch, and the prime minister himself had escaped over a back wall in his pyjamas. Zahedi was installed by evening. The Shah flew back from Rome on 22 August, told the press he had always known his people loved him, and began the slow construction of the SAVAK police state that would eventually produce 1979. Roosevelt was awarded the National Security Medal in a private ceremony at the White House. He retired from the CIA in 1958 and went to work, with no apparent sense of irony, for Gulf Oil.

The lesson historians usually pull from Ajax is that the CIA was dangerously good at overthrowing governments. The opposite is closer to the truth. **The CIA was bad at overthrowing governments — its plan failed completely on the first try — and was rescued only because one officer ignored his recall order, and because the Iranian military preferred to back whichever side appeared to be winning over whichever side was constitutionally legitimate.** Every later American intervention in the Middle East has been built on the assumption that Ajax was a model of how covert action works. It is in fact a model of how covert action almost never works, salvaged by luck and by a man who was never punished for disobeying his superiors because his luck happened to hold. The most consequential American foreign policy operation of the twentieth century was, on the night of 15 August 1953, an unambiguous failure. Nobody in Washington has ever quite reckoned with what that means.
