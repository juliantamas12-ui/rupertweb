The Three Hours at Fashoda When France Decided Germany Was the Enemy: 18 September 1898

On the morning of 18 September 1898, a small steamer flying the white ensign came up the White Nile and tied to the bank below a half-ruined Egyptian fort at a place called Fashoda, in what is now South Sudan. Out of it stepped Lord Kitchener, Sirdar of the Egyptian Army, sweating in a tarboush and an Egyptian uniform he had put on instead of his British one for diplomatic reasons. Waiting for him on the bank was Captain Jean-Baptiste Marchand of the French Marine Infantry, who had spent the previous fourteen months crossing Africa on foot from the Atlantic with eight French officers, a hundred and twenty Senegalese tirailleurs, and a collapsible steam launch carried in pieces by porters. Marchand had reached Fashoda on 10 July. He had run up a French tricolour. He had, in the technical language of late-nineteenth-century colonial law, "effectively occupied" the upper Nile. The two men sat down in the shade of a tamarind tree with a bottle of champagne Marchand had been saving since Brazzaville, and over the next three hours they decided, between themselves, that there would be no war.

Fashoda is the moment France gave up the world and chose Europe. Not the Entente Cordiale of 1904, which was the public consequence; not the Algeciras Conference of 1906, which ratified the new alignment; not the military conversations of 1911, which made it operational. The act that reoriented French strategy from a hundred-year contest with Britain to a forty-year contest with Germany was the decision taken in Paris, between 19 September and 3 November 1898, to recall Marchand without firing a shot. Théophile Delcassé became foreign minister in the middle of the crisis, looked at what had nearly happened, and concluded that France could not afford both an enemy in London and an enemy in Berlin, and that since Berlin was the one occupying Alsace-Lorraine, London would have to stop being an enemy. Everything that followed — the Entente, the alliance with Russia hardened into a triple combination, the iron defensive crouch of 1914 — followed from that one inference, drawn under pressure, in six weeks.

The conventional account treats Fashoda as the high-water mark of the Anglo-French colonial rivalry and the last great crisis of the scramble for Africa. This reading mistakes the surface for the substance. The colonial rivalry was not, by 1898, the central question of French foreign policy. It was the residue of one. The central question was the German army on the Rhine. France had spent the years since 1871 trying to find a configuration of allies, threats, and distractions that would make a war of revanche thinkable, and had failed. The Russian alliance of 1894 was the first solid result. The colonial empire was the second: a project pursued with enormous energy by a small group of officials in the Quai d'Orsay and the Ministry of Marine on the explicit theory, articulated by Léon Gambetta and codified by Jules Ferry, that overseas expansion was a substitute for European war, a way of making France important enough to matter without having to fight Germany before she was ready. Fashoda was the moment this theory ran out of room.

The Marchand expedition had been authorised in 1896 by Gabriel Hanotaux, the foreign minister, on the calculation that a French foothold on the upper Nile would give France a lever to force Britain to evacuate Egypt — which Britain had occupied in 1882 and which French opinion had not forgiven her for since. The lever depended on a chain of assumptions: that Marchand could actually reach Fashoda; that Britain would care about the upper Nile enough to negotiate; that the negotiation could be conducted between equals; and that the European balance would prevent Britain from simply taking what it wanted. The first assumption proved correct. The second proved correct in the worst possible way — Britain cared so much that she sent Kitchener with a flotilla of gunboats and five battalions of British infantry as soon as the news from Khartoum allowed. The third and fourth collapsed on the riverbank.

What Marchand saw when Kitchener arrived was a column of force he could not match. The Sirdar had behind him an army that had just destroyed the Mahdist state at Omdurman three weeks earlier, killing eleven thousand dervishes in a morning at a cost of forty-seven of his own dead. He had river gunboats with quick-firing artillery against which Marchand's hundred and twenty rifles could do nothing. Kitchener was perfectly courteous. He invited Marchand aboard for whisky and warm beer. He raised the Egyptian flag — not the British one, on the careful legal fiction that this was a reconquest of territory that had belonged to the Khedive — alongside the French tricolour. He left a small force at Fashoda under a British officer and steamed downstream to wire London. And then, in Paris, the question stopped being a colonial question and started being a European one.

The French naval staff, when consulted, reported that the Mediterranean fleet was not in a condition to fight the Royal Navy in 1898, that the Channel fleet was in worse condition, and that a war would be lost in the first three months. The Russian foreign ministry, when consulted, indicated that the alliance of 1894 was a continental alliance directed against Germany and Austria-Hungary and did not extend to a colonial quarrel with Britain. The German chancellor, Bülow, when consulted by neither side but watching closely, briefed the Kaiser that the right policy was to let France and Britain bleed each other and then collect the survivors — a position which, when it leaked through the usual channels, hardened Delcassé's conviction that the colonial rivalry was a German trap. The French press worked itself into a fury for about ten days and then quieted abruptly as the cabinet realised the country would not actually fight.

On 3 November 1898, the order went out from Paris for Marchand to evacuate Fashoda. He received it ten days later, raised his flag for the last time, and marched east to Djibouti, where he was received as a national hero and given a parade on the Champs-Élysées at which the crowd shouted for war with England. The crowd was about a fortnight behind events. By then Delcassé had already opened the channels in London that would, six years later, produce the Entente Cordiale. The bargain he proposed was simple: France would concede Egypt, where Britain had been for sixteen years and was not leaving; Britain would concede Morocco, where France wanted to go; the two empires would stop pretending they were the principal threat to each other and turn to face the actual one.

The Germans did not understand what had happened at Fashoda. Bülow, briefing the Kaiser in the winter of 1898–99, treated the French recall as a humiliation that had weakened France's diplomatic position and made her more dependent on the Russian alliance and therefore more available to Berlin. This was the exact opposite of the truth. The recall had been a strategic decision to liquidate the wrong war so that the right one could be prepared for. Six years later, when the Kaiser landed at Tangier and tried to detach France from the new British connection by threatening her over Morocco, he discovered that the connection he was trying to break had been built precisely to absorb the kind of pressure he was applying, and that the more pressure he applied the harder it set.

Empires do not generally end at the moment they appear to. The French empire did not end at Fashoda; it continued to expand for another twenty years. But the French project for which the empire had been built — the project of being a global rival to Britain while keeping a free hand against Germany — ended in three hours under a tamarind tree, when Marchand drank Kitchener's whisky and understood that his hundred and twenty men were not, after all, the leading edge of a strategy but the rear guard of one. The strategy had been to fight Britain. The new one would be to fight Germany. France would lose the next war anyway, but for entirely different reasons, and against an enemy she had at least correctly identified.
