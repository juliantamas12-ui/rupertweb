The Sunday Mass Assassination That Made the Medici Untouchable

On Sunday, 26 April 1478, during High Mass at the Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore in Florence, two priests pulled daggers from beneath their robes and attacked Lorenzo de' Medici at the moment of the elevation of the Host. A second team, led by a young nobleman named Francesco de' Pazzi, fell on Lorenzo's younger brother Giuliano with such enthusiasm that Francesco stabbed himself in the thigh in the frenzy. Giuliano died on the cathedral floor with nineteen wounds. Lorenzo, slashed across the neck, vaulted the altar rail, locked himself in the sacristy, and survived.

This is usually told as a thriller — Renaissance Florence, daggers in the cathedral, the surviving Medici brother emerging bloodied and triumphant. It is a thriller. But it is also one of the cleanest examples in European history of how a botched assassination can entrench the regime it was meant to destroy, and of how the line between banking and statecraft in Quattrocento Italy was not blurry but nonexistent.

The conspiracy was not a private vendetta. It was a coordinated operation involving the Pazzi family — Florence's second-largest banking dynasty — the Archbishop of Pisa, the condottiere Federico da Montefeltro, the King of Naples, and Pope Sixtus IV himself. The Pope's own nephew, Girolamo Riario, was the operational lead. The Pope later claimed he had authorised the overthrow of the Medici but not, he insisted, the actual killing, because killing was sinful. This is the kind of distinction that only makes sense if you understand that for Sixtus IV, removing Lorenzo de' Medici was not a foreign policy goal but a debt-collection strategy.

Here is what the cathedral sermon obscures. The Medici Bank was the principal financier of the Papacy. It had been since the 1410s. The bank's Rome branch handled curial deposits, papal alum monopolies, the transfer of crusade tithes, and — crucially — short-term lending to the Pope's relatives, who in the late Quattrocento were the actual instrument of papal policy. When Sixtus IV came to the throne in 1471, he wanted to buy the lordship of Imola for his nephew Girolamo. Lorenzo de' Medici refused the loan. The Pazzi Bank, hungrier and politically ambitious, agreed to fund it. Sixtus thanked them by stripping the Medici of the papal account and transferring it to the Pazzi.

This is the context the daggers come out of. The Pazzi conspiracy was a hostile takeover. Removing Lorenzo would have given the Pazzi the Florentine state, the papal banking account, and a clear field to consolidate Tuscany. The conspirators were not crazed ideologues. They were a competing financial network attempting, with the Pope's blessing and military backup from Naples and Urbino, to restructure the political-economic equity of central Italy.

What is striking is how nearly it worked, and how completely it failed. The plan called for Lorenzo and Giuliano to be killed simultaneously, after which papal troops under Riario and Neapolitan forces under King Ferrante would march on a leaderless Florence, the Pazzi would proclaim a "liberation" from Medici tyranny, and the city's traditional anti-Medici factions would supposedly rise. The plan went wrong at the first stage — Lorenzo lived — and from that moment forward every subsequent stage made the situation worse for the conspirators. The Florentine crowd, summoned to the Piazza della Signoria expecting to celebrate liberation, instead saw the bloodied Lorenzo on the balcony of the Palazzo Medici and turned on the Pazzi in real time. Within hours Francesco de' Pazzi was dragged naked from his bed and hanged from a window of the Palazzo Vecchio. The Archbishop of Pisa was hanged in his vestments from the same window, where he allegedly bit Pazzi's corpse on the way down. By nightfall something like eighty conspirators and their dependents were dead. Botticelli was hired to paint the hanged men on the wall of the Bargello as a warning.

The deeper consequence is what Lorenzo extracted from the catastrophe. Before the Pazzi conspiracy, the Medici were the dominant family in a republic that was still, formally, a republic. The constitutional fictions were threadbare but maintained. Lorenzo ruled through influence, marriage alliances, and control of the appointment lottery for the Signoria, but he was not yet a prince. After the conspiracy, he was. The Florentine state, traumatised, gifted him a new permanent council — the Council of Seventy — packed with loyalists and effectively immune to electoral rotation. The republican machinery continued to grind through its forms, but every meaningful decision now flowed through Lorenzo. His enemies were dead, his rival bank dissolved, and Sixtus IV's two-year war of revenge against Florence ended in an exhausted stalemate that left Lorenzo's prestige higher than before.

The lesson, for anyone who has watched a coup attempt in real time, is the one Machiavelli would later codify: if you strike at the king, do not miss. A failed coup is not a return to the previous equilibrium. It is a one-way ratchet that hands the surviving target the moral authority, the legal pretext, and the popular consent to dismantle every check that had previously constrained him. The Pazzi conspirators believed they were ending Medici rule. They founded it.

Florence remained a republic in name for another fifty-four years. In substance, it died on the cathedral floor with Giuliano.
