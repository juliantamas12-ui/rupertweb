The Two Weeks in 1923 That Killed the League of Nations

On 27 August 1923, an Italian general named Enrico Tellini and four members of his staff were ambushed and murdered on the Greek side of the Greek-Albanian border. Tellini was working for an international boundary commission set up under the auspices of the Conference of Ambassadors, the body of victorious Allied powers still cleaning up the post-Versailles map. The killers were never identified. The Greek government, which had been at war with itself, with Turkey, and with its own monarchy for the better part of a decade, denied responsibility and offered to investigate. Mussolini, who had been Prime Minister of Italy for ten months and had been looking for exactly this kind of opportunity, gave Athens a 24-hour ultimatum demanding a 50-million-lira indemnity, a formal apology delivered by the entire Greek cabinet, military honours for the dead at the Catholic cathedral in Athens, and the execution of the perpetrators — perpetrators whom no one had identified. When the Greeks refused some of the conditions, the Italian fleet bombarded and occupied the island of Corfu on 31 August, killing about twenty civilian refugees, mostly Armenian children, sheltering in an old fort.

This episode has been almost entirely forgotten. It is filed somewhere between the Ruhr Crisis and the Locarno Treaties as a piece of post-war detritus. It deserves to be filed as the moment the League of Nations died, twelve years before everyone agrees it died, and as the prototype for every subsequent piece of fascist foreign policy that would eventually destroy the European order.

The legal facts were trivially clear. Greece appealed to the League under Articles 12 and 15 of the Covenant, which obliged member states to submit disputes to arbitration before resorting to force. Italy was a member. Italy had used force. The case was, on the face of the Covenant, exactly the kind of test the League had been built for: a great power attacking a small one, the small one invoking the rules, the rules requiring the great power to back down. Geneva was, in late August 1923, the venue at which the entire Wilsonian project either worked or did not.

It did not work. Mussolini's response to the suggestion that the League might consider the matter was to threaten Italian withdrawal from the organisation. The British, under Stanley Baldwin, were initially inclined to take the League's side; the French, under Poincaré, were not. Poincaré had French troops sitting in the Ruhr at that exact moment, extracting reparations from Germany in violation of arguably the same principles, and he had no interest in establishing a precedent that great powers could be made to disgorge what they had seized by force. The matter was quietly transferred from the League Council to the Conference of Ambassadors — the same body whose employee had been killed in the first place — which had no jurisdiction over Italy except the moral authority Italy chose to grant it. The Conference ordered Greece to pay the full 50 million lire. Italy withdrew from Corfu on 27 September. Mussolini had got everything he asked for, plus the cash, and had taught the international system a lesson it would spend the next sixteen years failing to unlearn.

The lesson was not that the League had no army. Everyone knew that. The lesson was that the League's members would not enforce its rules even by means already available to them — economic sanctions, diplomatic isolation, denial of recognition — when doing so would inconvenience their own foreign policy. The Covenant was not a flawed instrument. It was an unused one. Mussolini understood this within ten months of taking office. Hitler, watching from Munich while serving a sentence for the failed Beer Hall Putsch, certainly noticed. The Japanese General Staff, planning the next war on the Asian mainland, definitely noticed. By the time the League failed to stop the invasion of Manchuria in 1931, the Italian invasion of Ethiopia in 1935, and the German remilitarisation of the Rhineland in 1936, the script was already eight to thirteen years old. Corfu had written it.

There is a tendency to treat the interwar collapse as a slow-motion accident, as if the men running the world in the 1920s could not have known what they were enabling. This is sentimental. The British Foreign Office knew. Lord Robert Cecil, the most committed Leaguer in the Cabinet, threatened resignation over the British failure to back Greece and was talked down with promises that this would be the last time. The French knew, and did not care, because they were doing something analogous in the Ruhr and would shortly be doing something worse in the Rhineland. The Greeks knew — Eleftherios Venizelos, the country's most experienced diplomat, told anyone who would listen that the precedent would be catastrophic — and were ignored because Greece in 1923 was a country that had just lost a war to Turkey, expelled its king, and had no leverage of any kind. The small powers always know. They are the ones who get killed when the system fails.

The deepest lesson of Corfu is not about Mussolini. Mussolini was a thug who had stumbled into office and was looking for cheap wins. The deepest lesson is about Poincaré, Baldwin, and the men in Geneva. They had built, with enormous effort and at enormous human cost, a machine designed to do exactly one thing: prevent a great power from bullying a small one without consequence. They were given, in late August 1923, the easiest test the machine would ever face. The aggressor was relatively weak. The victim was relatively sympathetic. The legal case was uncomplicated. The required response was not military. They failed the test because they did not, in the end, want to pass it. Everything that followed — Manchuria, Ethiopia, Austria, Czechoslovakia, the corpses in the snow at Katyn and the ash in the air over Warsaw — was contained, in embryo, in that decision. Institutions do not collapse because they are weak. They collapse because the people running them decide, in some specific and identifiable moment, that the cost of using them is higher than the cost of letting them rot. Corfu was that moment for the League. Whether something similar has already happened to whatever order we currently inhabit is a question that should keep people awake.
