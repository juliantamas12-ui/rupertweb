The Line Two Men Drew in 1494 That Still Runs Through South America

On 7 June 1494, in a small Castilian town called Tordesillas, representatives of the Spanish and Portuguese crowns signed a treaty dividing the entire non-European world between their two kingdoms. The mechanism was almost comically simple: a meridian running from pole to pole, 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands. Everything to the west of the line belonged to Spain. Everything to the east belonged to Portugal. Africa, Asia, and whatever else lay beyond the horizon were disposed of in a single afternoon by men who had, between them, seen almost none of it.

Columbus had returned from his first voyage sixteen months earlier. No European had yet set foot on the South American mainland. Magellan's circumnavigation was twenty-five years away. Portugal had not yet reached India. The two negotiators at Tordesillas were drawing a boundary through territories whose shape, size, and inhabitants they could not even guess at. And the boundary stuck. Not in the sense that the Spanish and Portuguese empires it created lasted forever — they did not — but in the sense that the cultural, linguistic, and legal consequences of that one afternoon are still visible from orbit, five centuries later. Draw a map of where Portuguese is spoken in South America today and you have, almost exactly, drawn the Treaty of Tordesillas.

This is the genuinely strange thing. Treaties do not usually survive the empires that wrote them. The Congress of Vienna's map was erased by 1918. Sykes-Picot was gone in thirty years. The Molotov-Ribbentrop line lasted two. Tordesillas has outlived the Spanish Habsburgs, the Portuguese Braganzas, the whole institution of European colonial empire, two world wars, and the Cold War, and it is still the reason a Brazilian and an Argentine cannot understand each other without effort. How?

The short answer is that the treaty was not really about sovereignty. It was about a monopoly on information, and information, in the fifteenth century, was the one thing the Iberian crowns were actually good at controlling. What Tordesillas divided was not land — neither kingdom could occupy land on that scale — but the right to explore. Portuguese captains could sail east without fear of Spanish interference. Spanish captains could sail west without Portuguese interference. Each side therefore specialised. Portugal poured its resources into the Indian Ocean, built a chain of fortified trading posts from Mozambique to Macau, and essentially ignored the Atlantic coast of its half of South America for the first thirty years. Spain poured everything into the Caribbean, Mexico, and Peru, and left the Brazilian coast alone because the treaty said it was not theirs to take.

By the time anyone noticed that the line, as actually measured, cut off a huge bulge of eastern South America and handed it to Portugal, it was too late. Pedro Álvares Cabral made landfall on what is now Bahia in 1500 — possibly by accident, possibly not; historians still argue — and claimed it under the treaty. The Portuguese did not do much with the claim for a generation. But when sugar started paying, and later when gold was discovered in Minas Gerais in the 1690s, the Portuguese had a legal document older than any other European claim to the Americas telling them that this was their territory. The Spanish, having never contested the line, had no grounds to dispute it. The other European powers — French, Dutch, English — did try to contest it, and were militarily repelled one by one. Tordesillas worked because the two powers who signed it never broke ranks with each other, and because they held the coastline long enough for settlement, language, and religion to become facts on the ground.

Here is where it gets philosophically interesting. The line itself was arbitrary. There is no geographical logic to 370 leagues west of Cape Verde. The negotiators picked the number because it was a compromise between what the two kings wanted. But the consequences of that arbitrary line were not arbitrary. They were, in the jargon of modern social science, path-dependent. Once Portuguese was being preached in Bahian pulpits, Portuguese law was being administered in Pernambuco courts, and Portuguese administrative categories were being applied to Indigenous populations in the Amazon, the reversal cost of all this rose asymptotically. By 1750, when the Treaty of Madrid superseded Tordesillas and formally acknowledged that Portugal had pushed far west of the original line, nobody cared about the line any more because the facts on the ground had made it irrelevant. Brazil was Portuguese not because of what was signed in 1494 but because of what that signature had caused to happen in the 250 years since.

What Tordesillas reveals is how institutions outlive the reasons for their creation. The treaty was signed to prevent a war between two Iberian kings over some islands neither of them had ever seen. It ended up deciding which language 200 million people would speak, which legal tradition would shape their constitutions, which form of Christianity would dominate their spiritual lives, and which European metropole would serve as their cultural reference point for centuries after that metropole itself had become a backwater. None of those outcomes were in the minds of the negotiators. They could not have been. The people whose lives the line would shape had not yet been conceived.

There is a lesson here about the modesty with which one should approach the question of what any given political decision is for. Tordesillas was not for Brazil. Brazil was a side effect of Tordesillas — a side effect so large it eventually became the main thing, while the original dispute dissolved into historical trivia. Most durable political acts are like this. The Constitution of the United States was not written to govern a continental industrial superpower; it ended up governing one. The European Coal and Steel Community was not designed to produce a common currency; it ended up producing one. The things we decide for small reasons have a way of outliving the reasons and ruling the world long after we have forgotten what we thought we were doing.

The map of Latin America is the purest surviving monument to this principle. Two men drew a line. The line was based on nothing. And it is still there.
