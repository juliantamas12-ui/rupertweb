# The Eleven Days in November 1923 When Germany Stopped a Hyperinflation With a Lie

On 15 November 1923 the German government began issuing a new currency called the Rentenmark, at an exchange rate of one Rentenmark to one trillion paper marks. Twelve days earlier, on the morning of 3 November, a dollar had cost 420 billion paper marks at the Berlin Bourse; by closing on the 14th it cost 2.52 trillion. On the 16th, the day after the new note appeared, the rate of depreciation stopped. On the 20th, the Reichsbank fixed the dollar at 4.2 trillion paper marks — the prewar parity, multiplied by a trillion — and held it there. Within ten weeks prices in Berlin had stabilised. Within four months unemployment, which had quadrupled during the inflation, began to fall. The hyperinflation, which most economists now regard as the most extreme monetary disorder in the history of an industrial country, simply ended.

**The Rentenmark had no proper backing, no gold reserve, no foreign loan, no credible fiscal rebalancing behind it. It was a confidence trick that worked because everybody involved, including the people running it, behaved as if it would not.** That is what makes the November 1923 stabilisation one of the strangest events in monetary history, and one of the most important. It is the case study every central banker in the twentieth century read, and almost none of them drew the right lesson from it.

The technical mechanics first, because they are absurd. The Rentenmark was issued by a new institution, the Rentenbank, with a capital of 3.2 billion Rentenmark in the form of a first-mortgage charge on every piece of agricultural land and industrial real estate in Germany. The mortgages paid interest in gold-equivalent rye and coal — units of account that did not actually exist as legal tender. The total notes the Rentenbank was permitted to issue against this collateral was capped at 2.4 billion Rentenmark, of which 1.2 billion was earmarked for the government and 1.2 billion for commerce. The mortgages were a fiction. There was no mechanism by which a Rentenmark holder could actually go to a farm in Pomerania and claim a slice of it. The Rentenbank was not a bank in any meaningful sense; it had no reserves, no foreign assets, and no ability to defend the new currency against a run. The Reichsbank, which continued to exist alongside it and to issue the old paper mark, had only fifteen million dollars in foreign exchange — enough, at the new parity, to redeem perhaps three percent of the Rentenmark notes in circulation.

What made the trick work was the second leg, which was political rather than monetary. On 13 October 1923 the Reichstag had granted the cabinet of Gustav Stresemann emergency decree powers under Article 48. On 15 October a man named Hjalmar Schacht, until then a moderately successful private banker with a sideline in writing newspaper articles about the currency, was appointed Currency Commissioner. Schacht's first act in office was to refuse to occupy the Currency Commissioner's offices. He installed himself in a converted broom cupboard at the Reichsbank, on the grounds that anything more elaborate would suggest a permanent post. His second act was to issue a circular on 18 October stating that the Rentenbank would, under no circumstances and for no political purpose, advance a single Rentenmark to the government beyond the 1.2 billion ceiling. His third act, on 12 November, was to refuse a request from the Reich Finance Ministry for an emergency advance of 400 million Rentenmark to pay civil-service salaries due on the 15th. The civil servants were paid in old paper marks, which by then were worth roughly the cost of the paper they were printed on. Several departments in the Wilhelmstrasse went unpaid for ten days. Schacht did not yield.

This is the part that mattered. **The Rentenmark stabilised the currency not because of its mortgage backing, which was nonsense, but because Schacht used the first three weeks of its existence to demonstrate, in concrete cases that the financial press could observe, that he would let the German state default on its own obligations before he would print one extra Rentenmark.** Every refusal was reported in the morning editions of the Berliner Tageblatt and the Frankfurter Zeitung. Every refusal subtracted a marginal expectation of future debasement from the market's pricing of the new currency. By the end of November the foreign exchange dealers in Berlin and Amsterdam had decided that Schacht meant it. The dollar rate held at 4.2 trillion. The new currency stopped being tested.

The reason the rye-and-coal collateral story matters at all is that it gave the German public a face-saving narrative for accepting a currency they would otherwise have refused on sight. After four years in which every monetary promise made by a German institution had been broken, no piece of paper saying "Reichsbank" was going to be taken seriously. The Rentenmark was not a Reichsbank note. It was, nominally, a claim on the land, which Germans understood and trusted in a way they no longer trusted any government instrument. That the claim was unenforceable did not matter, because nobody intended to enforce it. They intended to spend the note. As long as the next person would also accept it, the fiction worked. Schacht's refusals demonstrated that the next person would.

The conventional account treats the Rentenmark as the end of the hyperinflation and the Dawes Plan of August 1924 as the consolidation of the recovery. This gets the causation backwards. The Dawes loan worked because the Rentenmark had already worked; the Rentenmark had worked because Schacht had behaved, for eleven days in November 1923, as if a German central banker would rather watch his own state miss payroll than monetise its deficit. Almost nothing in the previous five years of German monetary history suggested this was a credible commitment. Schacht made it credible by acting it out.

The lesson the twentieth century did not learn — or learned and then unlearned at intervals — is that the technical paraphernalia of monetary stabilisation, the reserve ratios and the currency boards and the inflation targets, are stage furniture. What stabilises a currency is the visible willingness of the institution issuing it to impose costs on politically powerful actors before it imposes costs on the holders of the currency. The Rentenbank had no reserves. It had Schacht's broom cupboard, and Schacht's circular of 18 October, and the unpaid civil servants of the second week of November. That turned out to be enough.

It is worth remembering, given how much of subsequent German political history rests on the trauma of 1923, that the hyperinflation was not ended by a heroic mobilisation of national resources or by a foreign rescue. It was ended by one bad-tempered banker in an unheated office refusing to take phone calls from the Finance Ministry, while the rest of the German state pretended that an unbacked piece of paper was secured against farmland it could not touch. The trick was, in the most literal sense, a confidence trick. The remarkable thing is not that it was a trick. The remarkable thing is that it has, more or less, been the basis of every fiat currency that has worked ever since.
