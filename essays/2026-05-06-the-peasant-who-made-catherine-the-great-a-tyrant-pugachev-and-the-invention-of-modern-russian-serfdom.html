The Peasant Who Made Catherine the Great a Tyrant: Pugachev and the Invention of Modern Russian Serfdom

In September 1773, a semi-literate Don Cossack deserter named Yemelyan Pugachev rode into a fortified settlement on the Yaik River, declared himself to be the murdered Tsar Peter III miraculously returned from the dead, and within ten months commanded an army of perhaps a hundred thousand peasants, Cossacks, Old Believers, runaway serfs, Bashkirs, Tatars, and factory workers. He took Kazan. He massacred the nobility of half a dozen provinces. At the high water mark of the rebellion in the summer of 1774, the Russian state had effectively lost control of everything between the Volga and the Urals — an area larger than France — and Catherine II, who had spent eleven years cultivating the European reputation of a philosopher-empress in correspondence with Voltaire and Diderot, was reduced to writing private letters in which she referred to Pugachev as "the Marquis."

The standard account of the Pugachev rebellion is that it was crushed, that Pugachev was caged and beheaded in Moscow's Bolotnaya Square in January 1775, and that Catherine resumed her enlightened reforms with the Provincial Reform of that same year and the Charter to the Nobility a decade later. This is true at the level of chronology and false at the level of substance. What actually happened in the decade after Pugachev is that Catherine the Great, the most prolific reader of the French Enlightenment ever to wear a crown, doubled the percentage of the Russian population held in legal servitude and entrenched a system of bondage that would survive her, her son, her grandsons, and would not be dismantled until 1861. The serfdom that Tolstoy's Levin agonised over and that Lenin grew up in the receding shadow of was, in a precise sense, Pugachev's legacy. It was not the Russia Catherine inherited. It was the Russia she built in response to the only thing that had ever genuinely frightened her.

The mechanics matter. When Catherine seized power in 1762 from her husband, somewhere between forty and forty-five percent of the empire's peasants were privately owned serfs. Most of the rest were state peasants, monastery peasants, or "free" categories — odnodvortsy, single-homesteaders on the southern frontier, the Cossack hosts on the Don and the Yaik, the various tribal peoples of the Urals and Siberia. The line between a state peasant and a serf was meaningful: state peasants paid a tax, served in the army, and were not subject to private sale, mortgage, or corporal punishment by a private master. They were, in the eighteenth-century Russian context, something like citizens. Catherine had spent the first decade of her reign convening the Legislative Commission of 1767 to discuss, in full Enlightenment fashion, the reform of Russian law on first principles. Her Nakaz — her instruction to the Commission — quoted Beccaria and Montesquieu at length and contained passages criticising serfdom as morally and economically indefensible. The historian Isabel de Madariaga catalogued the references. The young empress was, on paper, an abolitionist.

Pugachev ended this. The rebellion was, from Catherine's standpoint, the precise nightmare the Nakaz had warned about — a peasant rising in the empire's vulnerable southeast, fuelled by the very grievances she had identified — and it had done what no aristocratic conspiracy or palace coup could have done: it had put her on a horse and made her count cannon. The conclusion she drew was the opposite of the one her reading might have predicted. The problem, she decided, was not that serfdom was unjust. The problem was that the state had insufficient grip on the countryside. The empire had run out of nobility on the periphery. Where there was no resident landowner with a personal financial stake in keeping his peasants quiet, there was rebellion. The solution was to manufacture more landowners, give them more peasants, and tie those peasants more tightly to the land.

This is what the Provincial Reform of 1775 actually did, beneath its veneer of administrative rationalisation. It created fifty governorates, each requiring a resident gentry administration, and the Crown handed out enormous tracts of state land — together with the people on it — to fund the new posts. Between 1775 and 1796, Catherine personally distributed approximately 800,000 male state peasants (so something on the order of 1.6 million people once you count women and children) to private owners as gifts and pensions. The Charter to the Nobility of 1785 then constitutionalised what the distributions had created: a hereditary aristocratic order with absolute jurisdiction over the people on its land, exemption from corporal punishment, exemption from taxation, and a corporate identity that the Romanov state could not legally touch. In 1796, when she died, the proportion of the population in private serfdom had risen to roughly fifty-five percent. The "free" categories of peasant on the southern and eastern frontier — the very communities from which Pugachev had recruited — had been systematically liquidated as legal categories. The Yaik Cossack Host was dissolved by name, renamed the Ural Host, and brought under direct military discipline. The Zaporozhian Sich was destroyed by Potemkin in 1775. The Don was reorganised. The empire, which had been a patchwork of bargains with frontier populations, became a uniform field of crown-and-noble authority.

The intellectual scaffolding of this reaction is what makes it worth attention now. Catherine did not stop quoting Voltaire. She continued to commission French sculptors, French gardens, and French librarians for the Hermitage. The Nakaz remained on her bookshelf. What she had decided was that Enlightenment was a matter of sensibility, not policy — that one could be a philosopher in correspondence and a slaver at home, and the contradiction would be absorbed by the sheer magnitude of the empire and the credulity of the audience. She was right. Voltaire continued to praise her as the Semiramis of the North until his death in 1778, three years after the Provincial Reform began and during the largest mass enserfment in modern history. Diderot, who actually visited St Petersburg in 1773–1774 and saw the rebellion's last phase up close, came home and wrote critical observations that Catherine read, annotated, and ignored. The pattern in which a regime trades policy substance for cultural prestige with a foreign intellectual class — and the foreign intellectual class accepts the trade because the alternative is to admit they have been flattered by a butcher — is not a Russian invention, but Catherine industrialised it.

What Pugachev demonstrated, and what Catherine drew from it, was that a multi-ethnic agrarian empire cannot be liberalised at the periphery without loosening the centre's grip on the core, and that an autocrat who has been frightened will choose the grip every time. The serfs who rose for the false Peter III in 1773 ensured that their grandchildren would die owned. This is the mechanism by which revolutions, when they fail, deepen the conditions that produced them — and it is the reason Russian autocracy survived every shock the nineteenth century threw at it until 1917, when it finally met one it could not absorb.

Catherine understood, in a way her Western admirers did not, that an empire is held together by the credible threat of force against its weakest members. The lesson she taught her grandson Nicholas I was the lesson Nicholas's grandson Alexander III remembered after 1881, and the one Stalin remembered after the Civil War. The Russian state has always been, in this sense, Pugachev's monument. It is the structure that was built to ensure that the next Pugachev would not get as far.
