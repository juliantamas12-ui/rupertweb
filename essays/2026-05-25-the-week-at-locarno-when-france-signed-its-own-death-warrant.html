The Week at Locarno When France Signed Its Own Death Warrant: October 1925

In the second week of October 1925, in a lakeside hotel in the Swiss canton of Ticino, the foreign ministers of seven European states initialled a set of treaties that the chancelleries of the continent immediately hailed as the founding document of a new European order. Aristide Briand and Gustav Stresemann embraced for the cameras. The Nobel Peace Prize was awarded to both men jointly the following year. The Times of London called it "the real end of the war." Locarno entered the diplomatic vocabulary as a synonym for reconciliation, the way Munich would enter it thirteen years later as a synonym for capitulation, and almost nobody at the time noticed that the two words named the same process at different stages of its completion.

Locarno was not the moment Germany rejoined Europe as an equal. It was the moment France formally consented to the proposition that the eastern half of the Versailles settlement could be revised by force, and signed a document saying so. Everything that happened to Czechoslovakia in 1938 and to Poland in 1939 was permitted, in advance, by the precise structure of the treaties Briand put his name to in October 1925. The mechanism by which this was achieved is one of the most elegant pieces of diplomatic engineering in the twentieth century, and it was designed almost single-handedly by a man who had been German foreign minister for two years and who would be dead within four.

The conventional reading of Locarno treats it as a sincere attempt at Franco-German rapprochement that was subsequently undermined by the Depression, by the death of Stresemann in October 1929, and by the rise of Hitler. This reading mistakes the rhetoric of the treaties for their content. What was actually signed at Locarno consisted of a Rhineland Pact, in which Germany, France and Belgium mutually guaranteed their existing borders with British and Italian co-signature, and a set of arbitration treaties between Germany and its eastern neighbours — Poland and Czechoslovakia — which provided procedures for settling disputes but, critically, did not contain any guarantee of those borders by anyone.

The asymmetry was the entire point. Stresemann had spent the better part of 1925 explaining to the Reichstag, in speeches whose stenographic records survive and whose meaning is unambiguous, that the purpose of accepting the western frontier was to free Germany's hand in the east. In a letter to the former Crown Prince dated 7 September 1925 — a document that did not become public until after the Second World War — he wrote that the great task of German foreign policy was "the readjustment of the eastern frontiers, the recovery of Danzig, the Polish Corridor, and a correction of the frontier in Upper Silesia," and that this could be achieved only if Germany first secured itself a position from which "we are no longer treated as a defeated power."

Locarno was that position. By guaranteeing the western frontier in exchange for what Stresemann correctly understood was an implicit French renunciation of the right to enforce the eastern one, Germany acquired three enormous strategic assets at once. It got British endorsement of the principle that German revisionism in the east was a legitimate diplomatic activity rather than a violation of the peace. It got the early withdrawal of Allied troops from the Rhineland, which followed in 1926-1930 as a direct consequence of Locarno. And it got the implicit subordination of France's eastern alliances — with Poland and Czechoslovakia — to the new framework, which meant that French military action in defence of those allies would now constitute a breach of the Rhineland Pact unless it could be characterised as legitimate self-defence.

The French negotiators understood the asymmetry. The records of the Quai d'Orsay show Briand's officials raising the question of an eastern guarantee repeatedly through the summer of 1925 and being told, politely and consistently, that no such guarantee was possible: Germany would not give one, Britain would not give one, and France was therefore offered a choice between a treaty without an eastern guarantee and no treaty at all. Briand chose the treaty. He did so, his memoranda make clear, because he believed that the alternative — an indefinite continuation of armed coexistence with a revanchist Germany — was worse, and because he calculated that British involvement in the western frontier, even on these terms, was the maximum France could ever extract from a London government that had been trying to disengage from continental commitments since 1919.

This calculation was rational. It was also wrong. What Briand failed to perceive, and what Stresemann's correspondence shows he perceived very clearly, was that the act of signing a multilateral treaty distinguishing between guaranteed and unguaranteed frontiers established, in international law, a hierarchy of European borders. The Rhineland was sacred. The Polish Corridor was negotiable. Once that distinction was on paper, the work of moving particular borders from the second category into active dispute was a matter of patience and opportunity. Hitler did not invent the structure that allowed him to dismember Czechoslovakia in 1938; he inherited it. The Munich agreement was Locarno's logical conclusion, executed by less subtle men.

There is a deeper structural point here about the politics of multilateral guarantees. A treaty in which different signatories receive different levels of protection does not produce peace by averaging the protection across the system. It produces peace at the protected frontiers and increased instability at the unprotected ones, because the existence of a privileged tier signals to revisionist powers exactly where the international community will not enforce the existing order. Stresemann's achievement was to identify this principle and to embed it in a treaty that France was politically incapable of refusing. The hierarchy created at Locarno was not a flaw in the design; it was the design.

The Polish ambassador to France, Alfred Chłapowski, wrote to Warsaw on 17 October 1925 — the day after the initialling — that the treaties had reduced Poland's status from that of a French ally to that of "a country whose existence depends on the goodwill of its enemies." This was correct. The Polish-French alliance of 1921, which had committed France to military assistance in the event of German aggression against Poland, was not formally abrogated at Locarno, but it was subordinated to the Rhineland Pact in any case where the two might conflict. After 1925, French military planning quietly ceased to include any operation that would have required French troops to cross the Rhine in support of an eastern ally. The general staff continued to draw up such plans on paper; the political authorisation to execute them had been signed away at the lake.

The two men who designed this outcome were both dead by the time it produced its consequences. Stresemann died of a stroke on 3 October 1929, three weeks before the New York stock market crashed and twenty-three days before the world he had constructed began to come apart. Briand survived until 1932 but was driven from office in 1931 by an electorate that had begun, too late, to grasp what Locarno had cost. Neither lived to see the Wehrmacht enter the demilitarised Rhineland in March 1936, an act that destroyed the Rhineland Pact and freed Germany simultaneously from the only treaty obligation Locarno had imposed on it. France did not respond. The reason it did not respond was not, in the end, the Depression or pacifism or the failure of British support. The reason was that the only treaty France had ever been willing to fight for was the one Germany had just torn up, and the treaties that protected France's eastern allies — the ones Germany would shortly proceed to dismember — had never existed in the first place. Locarno had made sure of that, eleven years earlier, at a lake in Ticino, while the photographers were taking the picture of the embrace.
