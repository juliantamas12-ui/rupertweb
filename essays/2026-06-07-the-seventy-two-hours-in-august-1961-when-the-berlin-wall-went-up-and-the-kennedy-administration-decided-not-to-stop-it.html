The Seventy-Two Hours in August 1961 When the Berlin Wall Went Up and the Kennedy Administration Decided Not to Stop It

On the morning of Sunday, 13 August 1961, at roughly 01:00 Berlin time, units of the East German Volkspolizei and the Kampfgruppen der Arbeiterklasse — armed factory militias drawn from the SED's industrial cadres — began stringing barbed wire along the twenty-seven-mile boundary between East and West Berlin. By dawn the wire was up. By the end of the day the S-Bahn and U-Bahn lines between the sectors had been severed at sixty-eight stations. By Tuesday evening the wire was being replaced with concrete blocks. By Saturday a wall, in the literal architectural sense, existed.

The standard account of those seventy-two hours treats them as a fait accompli. Khrushchev moved. The West watched. There was nothing to be done. This is wrong. The Wall, on the morning of 13 August, was a string of fence posts and concertina wire that any platoon of American military police could have pushed over with a jeep. The Allied garrisons in West Berlin had standing rights of access throughout the city under the Four-Power agreements of 1945. The East German troops conducting the operation had been issued explicit orders, signed by Erich Honecker, who was running the operation, that any Western military intervention was to be met with retreat. The Soviet Twentieth Guards Army, positioned in reserve outside Berlin, had been told by Marshal Konev — recalled from retirement specifically to command the contingency — that engagement with American forces was forbidden without direct authorisation from Moscow, and that no such authorisation would be forthcoming.

The Wall, in other words, was put up by people who expected it to be torn down, and who had operational instructions to fold if it was. It was not torn down because the Kennedy administration, between Sunday morning and Wednesday afternoon, decided that the alternative to the Wall was worse than the Wall.

Consider the situation as it appeared in Washington on the morning of 13 August. Berlin had been the most dangerous problem in the Cold War for three years. Khrushchev had issued his ultimatum in November 1958, demanding a peace treaty that would freeze West Berlin into a "free city" with no Allied garrison and no access rights. The ultimatum had been renewed at Vienna in June 1961, where Kennedy had met Khrushchev for the first time and had come away — by his own account to James Reston in the immediate aftermath — convinced that Khrushchev thought he was weak. The refugee flow out of East Germany through the Berlin loophole had reached two thousand a day by early August. The East German economy was haemorrhaging skilled workers. The SED regime, Walter Ulbricht had told Khrushchev privately at the Warsaw Pact conference of 3–5 August, would not survive another six months at that rate.

Khrushchev's strategic problem was that he could not let East Germany collapse, and he could not stop the refugee flow without closing the Berlin border, and he could not close the Berlin border without breaching the Four-Power agreements. The political solution he hit upon was elegant in a thuggish way: he would close the border using East German forces only, not Soviet ones; he would close it overnight, before any Western response could be coordinated; and he would close it with materials light enough to be presented, in the first instance, as a security measure rather than a permanent partition.

The American intelligence apparatus had picked up the preparations. Allen Dulles at CIA had been reporting since late July that something was being prepared along the sector boundary. The specifics were unknown. Two operational hypotheses were being run at the Berlin desk: a full closure, or a tightening of controls short of closure. The institutional consensus, captured in a memorandum from Foy Kohler at the State Department dated 4 August 1961, was that closure was the preferable Soviet move from the American point of view, because closure would solve Khrushchev's refugee problem without requiring him to challenge Allied access rights to West Berlin. This is the sentence to which the Wall owes its existence. It was written nine days before the wire went up. It described, with bureaucratic precision, the trade the Kennedy administration was prepared to accept.

The reasoning was not stupid. The Kennedy administration had decided, after Vienna, that the survivable core of the Berlin commitment was the freedom and the Allied presence of West Berlin. Everything else — the unification of Germany, the rollback of the Soviet position in Eastern Europe, the formal sanctity of the Four-Power regime governing all of Berlin — was beyond what Kennedy was willing to fight for. He had said this explicitly to his National Security Council on 19 July in what came to be called the Three Essentials speech, broadcast in modified form on 25 July: the United States would defend Western presence in West Berlin, Western access to West Berlin, and the viability and freedom of West Berlin. He did not mention East Berlin. He did not mention the Four-Power regime. He did not mention free circulation between the sectors. The omissions were noticed in Moscow, where Anatoly Dobrynin and Mikhail Smirnovsky read the speech the next morning and cabled the Foreign Ministry that Kennedy had drawn a line they could walk up to without crossing.

This was the diplomatic context in which the wire went up on the morning of 13 August. The American minister in Berlin, Allan Lightner, was telephoned at his residence at approximately 04:00 by the press attaché Daniel Schorr, who had been woken by a tip from a Reuters stringer. Lightner drove to the Brandenburg Gate, saw the wire being unrolled, and cabled Washington at 05:30 Berlin time, which was 23:30 on Saturday evening Washington time. The cable went to the State Department Operations Center, which woke Foy Kohler, who decided not to wake the Secretary of State. Dean Rusk was at a Washington Senators baseball game by Sunday afternoon. Kennedy, who was sailing on the Marlin off Hyannis Port, was not informed until late Sunday morning, and even then was told only that "the East Germans have closed the border in Berlin." He cut his weekend short by half a day. He returned to Washington on Monday.

What happened over the next three days is the architecture of the Wall as a settled fact. On Sunday afternoon, before Kennedy had been formally briefed, the Berlin Task Force convened at the State Department. The participants — Kohler, Martin Hillenbrand, Charles Bohlen, Paul Nitze from Defense, Allen Dulles from CIA — agreed within ninety minutes on three things. First, the closure did not breach the formal Four-Power regime, because the East Germans had not interfered with Allied military access between West Berlin and West Germany. Second, the closure did breach the Four-Power regime, because civilian movement between the sectors had been a guaranteed feature of the 1945 settlement. Third, this contradiction would be resolved by treating the breach as a technical one rather than a substantive one, and by lodging a protest rather than mounting a response.

The minutes of this meeting, declassified in 1998, show that no operational option was tabled. Nobody proposed sending tanks to the Brandenburg Gate. Nobody proposed sending engineers with bolt cutters. Nobody proposed an air corridor demonstration. The phrase "kinetic response" was not used because the category did not exist in the Berlin Task Force's vocabulary. The vocabulary was diplomatic. The decision not to act was, in the formal sense, not made: it was simply that acting did not occur to the people in the room as a thing within their professional remit.

On Monday morning, with Kennedy back in Washington, the National Security Council met. By this point the wire had been up for thirty-two hours and was being supplemented at multiple points with cinder blocks and slabs. Konrad Adenauer in Bonn had not yet been briefed by the Americans. Willy Brandt, the governing mayor of West Berlin, was attempting to reach Kennedy by direct line and being routed instead to the State Department, where his calls were being taken by Hillenbrand. Brandt's position, telegraphed in a hand-written note delivered through the West Berlin liaison officer on Monday afternoon, was that the Allies should send a battle group of military police through Checkpoint Charlie into East Berlin, on the formal grounds of exercising rights of access, and that the East German forces would withdraw if confronted. Brandt was, in this assessment, almost certainly correct. The Honecker operational order has been declassified. The Soviet contingency orders to Konev have been declassified. Both required withdrawal in the face of Western military pressure.

Kennedy received Brandt's note through Pierre Salinger on Tuesday morning, 15 August, and did not respond for forty-eight hours. The response, when it came, was a letter that did not address Brandt's proposal at all. Instead it offered to dispatch Vice President Johnson to Berlin to demonstrate American resolve, and to reinforce the Berlin garrison with a battle group sent overland from West Germany down the Helmstedt autobahn. Johnson went. The battle group, fifteen hundred men of the 18th Infantry, drove down the autobahn on the morning of Saturday, 19 August, was harassed at the checkpoints but not stopped, and arrived in West Berlin to be greeted by half a million Berliners. The reinforcement was a real act. It demonstrated that Allied access would be defended. It also confirmed, by its precise scope, that the access being defended was the access to West Berlin from West Germany, not the access from West Berlin to East Berlin. The Wall, as a separation between the two halves of the city, was now an American as well as a Soviet fact.

The deeper question about the seventy-two hours is what they tell us about how superpowers manage strategic crises that they understand, in advance, to be unwinnable. The Kennedy administration had reasoned itself, through the spring and summer of 1961, into a position in which the formal closure of the Berlin sector boundary was the least bad outcome available. It had reasoned itself there because the alternatives — accepting Khrushchev's free-city proposal, fighting a tactical nuclear war over Berlin, watching the East German regime collapse and provoking Soviet intervention — were each catastrophic in their own ways. The closure was the only outcome that solved Khrushchev's immediate problem without forcing Kennedy to confront his own.

This was a coherent strategic position. It was also a position that the administration could not state publicly, because to state it would be to concede that the Four-Power regime, which had legally governed Berlin since 1945 and which the United States had spent fifteen years defending rhetorically, was now being abandoned in fact while being preserved in form. The Wall was, in this sense, a piece of diplomatic infrastructure that everybody in the room understood would not be removed, and that everybody in the room agreed never to describe in those terms. Kennedy's later remark to Walt Rostow — "a wall is a hell of a lot better than a war" — captured the operational view exactly. Rostow recorded it. It was kept out of the public record for thirty years.

The cost was paid by the Berliners. The Wall stood for twenty-eight years, two months, and twenty-seven days. One hundred and forty people, by the latest count of the Berliner Mauer Stiftung, were killed attempting to cross it. Approximately five thousand made it. The number who would have crossed had it not been built — projected forward from the August 1961 refugee rate of two thousand a day, with appropriate decay assumptions — is somewhere between one and three million people, depending on which demographer is doing the modelling.

The lesson Berlin teaches about the management of great-power crises is not the one usually drawn. It is not that firmness deters aggression, or that ambiguity invites it, or that nuclear weapons make conventional confrontation impossible. It is that strategic crises are routinely resolved by the side that loses agreeing not to notice that it has lost, in exchange for the side that wins agreeing not to celebrate the victory. The Wall went up because the Kennedy administration decided that this trade was the best one available. It was probably right about that. It was, however, wrong to tell itself and its successors that no trade had been made at all. The Berlin Wall was the most successful act of strategic accommodation in the entire Cold War, and the United States spent the next three decades pretending it was the opposite. The pretence cost the Berliners twenty-eight years and somewhere upwards of a million lives unlived. It cost the American foreign policy apparatus its capacity to learn from what had happened. The Cold War was won, in the end, by waiting. The Wall was the device by which the waiting was made structurally possible. That was a serious achievement. It deserved, and never received, an honest name.
