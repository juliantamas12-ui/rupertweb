The Three Days in January 1923 When Stanley Baldwin Sold Sterling to Pay America

On the evening of Wednesday, 17 January 1923, the British delegation to the World War Debt Funding Commission arrived in Washington aboard the Majestic. The Chancellor of the Exchequer, Stanley Baldwin, was forty-five years old, six months into the job, and had been given by the Cabinet a written negotiating instruction signed by Bonar Law as Prime Minister. The instruction set a floor: Britain would not accept any settlement of its $4.6 billion war debt to the United States Treasury at an interest rate higher than 2.5 per cent over sixty-two years, and would walk out of the conference rather than concede more. Andrew Mellon, the Secretary of the Treasury, opened the American position at 4.25 per cent over twenty-five years. The two figures were not bridgeable. The Cabinet's expectation, recorded in the minutes of 11 January, was that Baldwin would return without an agreement and that the debt question would be reopened at the level of heads of government later in the year.

What happened over the next three days is the most consequential thing Stanley Baldwin ever did, and almost no one in Britain understood it at the time. By Saturday evening, 20 January, Baldwin had signed a settlement at 3.3 per cent over sixty-two years, well above the Cabinet's ceiling, and had cabled London a brief telegram describing the terms as the best obtainable. He sailed for home on the Olympic on 22 January. On arrival at Southampton on 27 January, he walked off the gangway, gave a brief and now famous interview to the assembled press in which he praised American generosity and described Mellon as a friend of Britain, and travelled by special train to London. The interview was on the front page of every British newspaper by Sunday morning. The Cabinet read the terms at the same time as the public. Bonar Law, who was already mortally ill with throat cancer, drafted a letter of resignation that night. He was dissuaded by Lord Beaverbrook over breakfast on Monday and instead recorded in his diary that Baldwin had, in three days in Washington, settled in cash what Germany was going to be allowed to settle in paper, and that the British Empire would pay for this mistake for two generations.

Bonar Law was wrong about the duration and right about everything else.

The arithmetic of the Baldwin settlement is worth setting out, because the political history has buried it. Britain agreed to repay $4.6 billion of principal, plus accrued interest of $0.5 billion, at 3 per cent for the first ten years and 3.5 per cent thereafter, with full amortisation over sixty-two years. The total cash cost over the life of the agreement was $11.1 billion, of which $6.5 billion was interest. The annual debt service for the first decade was $161 million, equivalent at the 1923 exchange rate to £33 million. British government revenue in the 1922–23 fiscal year was £914 million. The American debt service alone, in other words, would consume 3.6 per cent of total revenue every year for sixty-two years. This was on top of the domestic war debt, on which Britain was paying £325 million annually, and on top of debts owed to Britain by France, Italy, Russia (repudiated), and the smaller Allies, on which collection was at best partial and at worst nil.

The settlement had three immediate consequences and one delayed one.

The first immediate consequence was that the Bank of England now had a hard-currency outflow commitment to New York of approximately £33 million a year, payable in dollars, at a time when the pound was trading at $4.63 and the pre-war parity to which Britain intended to return was $4.86. The Bank's working assumption from late January 1923 onwards was that the pound had to be brought back to $4.86 as quickly as politically tolerable, because every penny the pound rose against the dollar reduced the sterling cost of the annual debt service, and because failure to do so would compound the burden indefinitely. The decision to return to the gold standard at the pre-war parity, taken in April 1925 under Churchill as Chancellor, was driven by many considerations, but the binding constraint was the dollar cost of the Baldwin settlement. Keynes, whose pamphlet The Economic Consequences of Mr Churchill in 1925 attacked the gold decision in isolation, understood this perfectly well and said so in a private memorandum to Reginald McKenna in November 1925: the gold parity is the tail, the American debt is the dog. Sterling at $4.86, on a domestic price level still 70 per cent above 1914, made every British export 10 per cent uncompetitive overnight. The General Strike of 1926, which began in the coal industry where the export effect was most acute, was a direct downstream consequence of the Washington settlement of January 1923.

The second immediate consequence was diplomatic. France was watching. The French government had been told privately by the British Treasury in December 1922 that any Anglo-American settlement would be the floor for a parallel Franco-American settlement, and that France could expect terms no better than Britain received. When Baldwin's terms became public, Raymond Poincaré in Paris concluded that the only way France could service an American debt on comparable terms was to extract reparations from Germany in cash and in full, immediately, by any means necessary. The French and Belgian occupation of the Ruhr began on 11 January 1923, six days before Baldwin sailed; it was extended and hardened after the Washington terms became known. The Ruhr occupation, the German passive-resistance response, the collapse of the mark in the summer and autumn of 1923, the November 1923 Beer Hall Putsch, and the political conditions that produced the National Socialist breakthrough in the September 1930 Reichstag election are all on the same causal chain, and the first link is Baldwin's signature in Washington.

The third immediate consequence was internal. Bonar Law, who had taken office promising tranquillity and a return to Edwardian budgetary norms, never recovered politically from the Washington shock. His health collapsed in March, his Cabinet split in April, and he resigned in May. The succession question was decided by George V, advised by Lord Stamfordham, in favour of Baldwin over Curzon. Baldwin became Prime Minister on 22 May 1923 — four months and two days after signing the settlement that had destroyed his predecessor's authority. The man who had created the political crisis was promoted to resolve it. He went on to dominate British politics for the next fourteen years.

The delayed consequence took until 15 June 1934 to manifest. On that date, Britain made its last payment on the Baldwin debt — a token $10 million, paid in silver because Britain had run out of dollars — and then defaulted on the remainder, along with every other European debtor except Finland. The total amount Britain paid the United States between 1923 and 1934 was $2.025 billion of the $11.1 billion contracted. The Johnson Act of April 1934 then prohibited American loans to any government in default on its First World War debt, which meant that when Britain went to war again in September 1939, the United States was prevented by its own legislation from financing the British war effort. The mechanism that had to be invented in March 1941 to get round the Johnson Act was Lend-Lease, which transferred the entire residual gold and dollar reserves of the British Empire, and the residual British direct investment in the United States, to American ownership in exchange for matériel. The price of the 1941 financing was the liquidation of British financial primacy and the formal end of sterling's claim to be a reserve currency in its own right. The seed of that liquidation was planted in Washington on 20 January 1923.

What is interesting about the Baldwin episode is not that a junior Chancellor exceeded his negotiating mandate. That happens. What is interesting is that the British political system, having seen the consequences within a fortnight, promoted him rather than punished him, and then spent the next eighteen years constructing economic policy around the constraint he had imposed without authority. The gold standard return, the General Strike, the 1931 currency crisis, the Imperial Preference system at Ottawa in 1932, the appeasement-era reluctance to confront Germany without American backing, and the 1941 Lend-Lease surrender are all consequences of a decision taken by one man in three days in a Washington hotel, against explicit Cabinet instructions, with no domestic political cost to himself. The British Empire paid for Stanley Baldwin's career in instalments until 2006, when the final Lend-Lease payment was wired to Washington. The cheque was for $83.25 million. No British minister attended the ceremony. By then there was nothing left to commemorate.
