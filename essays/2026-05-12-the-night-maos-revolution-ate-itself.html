The Night Mao's Revolution Ate Itself: Lin Biao and the Trident at Öndörkhaan

At 02:30 on 13 September 1971, a British-built Hawker Siddeley Trident jet bearing the markings of the Chinese air force crashed in a grassland near Öndörkhaan in eastern Mongolia. Nine bodies were found among the wreckage. Mongolian and Soviet investigators, the first on the scene, identified one of them — burned, but with a partial dental match and a Mao Zedong Thought lapel pin — as Lin Biao, Marshal of the People's Republic, Vice Chairman of the Communist Party, the man Mao had personally inscribed as his successor in the Ninth Party Congress constitution of 1969. Two years after being declared the heir apparent, Mao's chosen son was dead in a foreign desert, fleeing his master.

The official Chinese explanation, released to senior cadres in late 1971 and to the public only in 1972, was that Lin had attempted a coup, failed, and fled toward the Soviet Union with his wife, son, and a handful of loyalists. The plane ran out of fuel. End of story. This explanation is almost certainly false in its details and entirely true in its meaning, and the gap between the two is where the Cultural Revolution dies.

The factual problems are immediate. A Trident with full tanks, which this one had when it left Shanhaiguan, has a range of well over 3,500 kilometres; it crashed 250 kilometres into Mongolia, barely outside Chinese airspace, with fuel still in the wing tanks according to the Soviet metallurgist who examined the wreck. The pilot, Pan Jingyin, was the deputy commander of the 34th Air Division and one of the most experienced Trident pilots in China — he had flown Zhou Enlai. The aircraft was not low on fuel; it was attempting a forced landing without flaps or landing gear, on a grass plain in the dark, with a partial crew and no co-pilot. Frederick Teiwes and Warren Sun's painstaking 1996 reconstruction, The Tragedy of Lin Biao, working from interviews with the Chinese investigators and the partial PLA archive, established that the most plausible sequence is not a coup attempt at all. It is a panic.

Here is what the panic was about. By the summer of 1971, Mao had decided to destroy Lin Biao. He had not yet decided how. At the Lushan plenum in August 1970, Lin's faction had pushed for the restoration of the post of Head of State — abolished after Liu Shaoqi's purge — with the obvious intention of installing Lin in it. Mao, reading this as an attempt to acquire institutional power independent of his personal favour, struck back. Through the spring and summer of 1971 he toured the southern military regions, meeting commanders one by one, telling each the same thing in slightly different words: someone in the leadership was building a personality cult, someone was plotting, and the army's loyalty must be to him alone. The transcripts of these conversations, leaked piecemeal in the 1980s, are a masterclass in the destruction of a rival by indirection. Lin was not named. Everyone understood.

Lin Biao's son, Lin Liguo — a 25-year-old air force political officer with delusions of being China's answer to the Kennedys — apparently understood it too. In March 1971, working with a small circle of young officers in the air force command, Lin Liguo drafted a document later published as Outline of Project 571 — wǔ-qī-yī being a Chinese pun on wǔzhuāng qǐyì, "armed uprising". The Outline is one of the strangest political documents of the twentieth century: a furious, almost teenage denunciation of Mao as a "contemporary Qin Shi Huang", a "feudal tyrant" who "uses Marxism-Leninism to deceive". It contemplates assassinating Mao by various methods — bombing his train, gas, "flame-thrower and rocket launcher" — without any plausible operational plan for what to do afterwards. It reads less like a coup blueprint than like the diary of a frightened young man writing what he wished he had the courage to do.

There is no evidence Lin Biao himself ever saw it before the night he died. His wife Ye Qun probably did. The marshal, by all reliable accounts, had spent the last two years of his life in a depressive haze, dosed with barbiturates and amphetamines by his personal physicians, refusing wind and light, terrified of crowds and water, unable to read documents for more than minutes at a time. The image of this man personally directing a coup is grotesque on its face. The image of his wife and son, watching him being slowly destroyed by Mao and panicking into a desperate flight, is consistent with every piece of testimony from the surviving aides.

What happened on 12 September is approximately this. Mao, returning to Beijing from his southern tour, made a cryptic remark to senior officers near Shanghai that he had "matters to settle" with the Lin family. The remark reached Lin Liguo via the air force network within hours. Liguo, having no idea what his father knew or did not know, ordered the Trident readied. Ye Qun and Liguo collected the marshal — drugged, half-conscious, by one account carried — from his villa at Beidaihe, drove through the night to the airstrip at Shanhaiguan, and forced a take-off so chaotic that the ground crew chief was killed by the aircraft's wing as it rolled. They flew north because north was away from Beijing. They crashed because the plane was overloaded, undercrewed, flown without proper navigation in the dark, and almost certainly because the pilot — who may or may not have known what was happening — attempted a landing he could not execute.

The political consequence was incalculable. Mao had spent five years tearing the Communist Party apart in the name of revolutionary purity, elevating Lin Biao as the personification of that purity, inscribing him in the Party constitution as the embodiment of correct line. Now Lin was dead, fleeing, branded a traitor, with a son who had written a document calling the Chairman a feudal tyrant. The Cultural Revolution did not survive this. The revolution had eaten its designated heir, and there is no recovery from that, only the long, embarrassed walk back toward Zhou Enlai, then Deng Xiaoping, then the dismantling of everything the Cultural Revolution had been for. The China that opened to Nixon four months later, the China that would eventually open to capital and the world, begins not with Deng's southern tour in 1992, not with the Third Plenum of 1978, but at 02:30 on a Mongolian grassland on 13 September 1971, when the last serious ideologue of Maoism died trying to escape Mao.

The lesson the Chinese Communist Party drew from Öndörkhaan is the lesson it still operates on. Designated succession is fatal. Cults of personality consume their objects. The system survives only if no individual is allowed to become indispensable. Xi Jinping's 2018 abolition of presidential term limits is interesting precisely because it is the first formal repudiation of the Lin Biao lesson in fifty years. The Party in Beijing knows what happened at Öndörkhaan better than anyone. We will find out, in the next decade, whether it remembers.
