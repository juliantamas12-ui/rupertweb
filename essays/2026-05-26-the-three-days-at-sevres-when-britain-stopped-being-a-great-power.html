The Three Days at Sèvres When Britain Stopped Being a Great Power: 22-24 October 1956

On the afternoon of Monday, 22 October 1956, a small French air force aircraft landed at a private airfield outside Paris carrying two passengers travelling under false names. One was Selwyn Lloyd, the British Foreign Secretary. The other was Sir Patrick Dean, a senior official of the Foreign Office. They were driven, in cars with curtains drawn across the windows, to a villa in the suburb of Sèvres, the same town where the Allied powers had imposed the terms of the Ottoman partition in 1920. Waiting for them were the French Prime Minister Guy Mollet, his defence and foreign ministers, and a delegation from Israel led by David Ben-Gurion himself, Moshe Dayan, and Shimon Peres. Over the next seventy-two hours, the three governments agreed in writing to fake a war.

Sèvres is the moment Britain ceased to be a great power. Not Suez itself, which was the public consequence; not the run on sterling a fortnight later, which was the mechanism; not Macmillan's "I am the first dead chancellor" telegram from Washington, which was the recognition. The act that destroyed Britain's position in the world was the signature of the Protocol of Sèvres on the evening of 24 October 1956 — a document committing Anthony Eden's government to manufacture a casus belli against Egypt in collusion with Israel and France, and then to lie about it in the House of Commons, at the United Nations, and to the United States. The Americans found out, the lie collapsed, sterling collapsed with it, and Britain spent the next thirty years discovering what it meant to be a medium-sized European country with delusions of grandeur.

The conventional account of the Suez Crisis treats the Sèvres meeting as a tactical embarrassment within a larger strategic miscalculation. This reading mistakes the part for the whole. The strategic miscalculation — that Britain could topple Nasser without American consent — was not a sudden error of judgement in the autumn of 1956. It was a working assumption of British policy that had survived intact from 1945, was tested at Sèvres, and was disproved within ten days. The reason Sèvres matters is that it was the first occasion on which a British government was compelled to put the assumption in writing, sign it, and find out what it was actually worth. The answer was nothing.

The structure of the Protocol was unambiguous. Israel would attack Egypt across the Sinai on 29 October. Britain and France would then issue an ultimatum to both belligerents demanding their withdrawal to ten miles either side of the Suez Canal — a line which, by design, lay deep inside Egyptian territory and well behind Israeli forward positions. Egypt would refuse, as both Eden and Mollet knew Egypt would have to refuse. Britain and France would then bomb Egyptian airfields and land paratroopers at Port Said, ostensibly to "separate the combatants" and "safeguard the Canal," in reality to occupy the Canal Zone, destroy Nasser politically, and install a more compliant Egyptian government. The Israelis insisted on a written copy. Mollet's people typed three. Dean signed for Britain after an anguished telephone call back to London, and Eden, when he saw the document, was reportedly so appalled by the existence of physical evidence that he sent a Foreign Office official to Paris a fortnight later to demand all three copies back. The French refused. The Israeli copy survived in the Ben-Gurion archive at Sde Boker and was published, in facsimile, in 1996. The Eden government had spent forty years denying the document existed.

It is worth being precise about what the lie consisted of. On 30 October 1956, after Israeli forces had crossed the frontier as agreed, Eden told the House of Commons that the Anglo-French ultimatum had been issued because of "the threat to international shipping" in the Canal, and that the British government had "no prior knowledge" of Israeli military plans. Both statements were false, and both were known by Eden, Lloyd, and the cabinet inner group to be false at the moment they were made. The Foreign Office had drafted the ultimatum at Sèvres in coordination with the Israelis whose actions it purported to condemn. The Royal Air Force had agreed in advance not to interfere with Israeli aircraft over the Sinai. The dates and movements of British military operations had been timed to follow Israeli ones by precisely the interval the Protocol specified.

Eisenhower, who was running for re-election the following week on a peace platform and had been lied to systematically by the British and French ambassadors throughout October, understood within forty-eight hours what had happened. The American response was not, as is sometimes suggested, an emotional reaction to being deceived. It was the cold execution of a strategic decision that had been latent in American policy since the Marshall Plan: that British and French imperial commitments in the Middle East were a liability to the Western position against the Soviet Union, that they should be wound up, and that the right moment to wind them up was when the British government had given the United States an unanswerable pretext to do so. Sèvres provided the pretext. The Treasury and the Federal Reserve, acting on White House instructions, refused to support sterling through the IMF, blocked British attempts to draw on the Fund, and made clear that no oil from American or Venezuelan sources would be made available to compensate for the closure of the Canal and the Syrian sabotage of the Trans-Arabian pipeline. By 6 November, the Bank of England's reserves had fallen by about fifteen per cent in a week. Macmillan, who had been the most hawkish member of the cabinet at the start of the crisis, told Eden on the morning of 6 November that the run could not be stopped without an American rescue, that the American rescue would not be forthcoming unless Britain accepted an immediate ceasefire, and that the choice was between humiliation in Egypt and bankruptcy at home. Eden chose humiliation. He had no other option, which was the point.

The structural lesson Sèvres taught — and which the British political class understood, with varying degrees of clarity, within a fortnight — was that the post-war British position rested on a single, unstated assumption: that in any conflict between British and American strategic preferences, the United States would, in the last analysis, defer to its closest ally because the alliance was more valuable to it than the immediate stake. This was the operating principle of British foreign policy from 1945 onwards. It worked over Palestine in 1948 only because the Americans were unsure of their own position. It worked over Iran in 1953 because the Americans agreed with the British objective. It did not work at Suez, and the reason it did not work was that Eisenhower decided to test what would happen if he simply refused to defer, and the answer was that British power evaporated within ten days. After 1956, no British government attempted a major foreign policy initiative against American opposition until Iraq in 2003, and the Iraq decision was taken in the opposite direction: in favour of an American policy that London disliked, on the calculation that disagreement with Washington was now a higher cost than the policy itself.

The men in the villa at Sèvres did not know they were ending an empire. Ben-Gurion, who did know roughly what he was doing, secured for Israel a tacit guarantee of French nuclear assistance that produced the Dimona reactor and the Israeli bomb. Mollet got nothing he wanted and resigned within nine months. Eden returned to London on 25 October still believing that the operation would succeed, that the Americans would acquiesce, and that British influence in the Middle East would be restored. He was wrong on all three counts in sequence, and by January he was out of office and the rest of the century had begun.

Empires do not generally end at the moment they appear to. The British Empire, in any meaningful sense, ended on the second floor of a villa in Sèvres on the evening of 24 October 1956, when three governments signed a document acknowledging, by the very fact of needing to sign it, that none of them could any longer act in the world without the permission of a fourth that had not been invited to the meeting.
